## 1. Water Heater Leaking: What to Do First, Repair Cost, and When to Replace It

**SEO Title:** Water Heater Leaking? What to Do First and When to Replace (Homeowner Guide)
**Meta Description:** A leaking water heater can mean anything from a loose valve to a failed tank. Here's how to identify the source, what repairs typically cost, and when replacement is smarter.

### Full Draft

#### Step 1: stop the water and power before you investigate

If you just found standing water near the water heater, the first move is not diagnosis, it is damage control. Shut off the cold water supply valve at the top of the tank (turn the handle clockwise until it stops). If you have a gas unit, turn the gas control dial to the "off" or "pilot" position. If it is electric, flip the breaker labeled for the water heater at your main panel. If any of those steps feel unfamiliar or the area around the unit is actively wet and energized, stay back and call a licensed plumber or electrician — a leaking electric water heater can be a shock hazard, and that is not a risk worth taking.

Once the water and power are off, put down towels or a shallow bin to control the pool, and move any cardboard, rugs, or stored items away from the base. Water heaters sit in basements, garages, and closets where nearby drywall, baseboards, and subflooring can start absorbing within minutes.

#### Step 2: figure out where the water is actually coming from

People often say "the water heater is leaking" when the real source is a fitting above it. Before assuming the tank is dead, dry everything off with a towel and watch for a minute or two. Look specifically at four places:

- **The cold and hot water connections at the top.** A drip here is usually a loose fitting, a failed flex connector, or corrosion at a dielectric union — often a repair in the low hundreds of dollars.
- **The temperature and pressure relief (T&P) valve on the side or top**, with a discharge tube running down. If this is leaking, it is either doing its job (releasing excess pressure) or the valve itself has failed. Either way, it is not something to ignore; pressure issues can be dangerous.
- **The drain valve near the bottom.** These plastic or brass valves can weep, especially after a recent flush. Sometimes a simple hose cap tightened onto the outlet slows it while you schedule service.
- **The tank itself.** If water is coming from under the unit with no clear fitting above it, and the drip pan is filling, that is usually the inner tank leaking through corrosion. At that point, the heater is done. Tanks cannot be patched in any lasting way.

#### Common causes, in plain language

Sediment buildup is the quiet killer of tank water heaters. Over years, minerals settle to the bottom, the burner or element has to work harder, and the steel shell corrodes from the inside. A sacrificial anode rod is supposed to rust in the tank's place, but once it is used up, the tank itself starts to go. That is why most standard tank water heaters last roughly 8 to 12 years, give or take, depending on water quality and maintenance.

Other common causes of leaks: a failed T&P valve, a cracked nipple fitting at the top, loose flex lines after a nearby plumbing job, condensation on a cold tank that looks like a leak but isn't, and a bad drain valve. Tankless units leak much less often, but when they do, it is usually at the inlet/outlet fittings or the pressure relief — and the diagnosis path is different.

#### What is reasonable DIY vs. what isn't

Tightening a visibly loose water connection with a wrench, replacing a drain valve cap, or swapping a flexible supply line are within reach for a confident DIYer who is willing to shut off water and gas/power first. Replacing a T&P valve is technically doable, but you are working with a pressurized hot water system, and a mistake there can scald or cause a tank rupture. That one is fair to hand off.

What is not reasonable DIY: replacing a tank water heater. It involves gas lines or 240V wiring, venting, code-required expansion tanks and drip pans in many jurisdictions, and safe disposal of an 150-pound corroded tank. Botched installs cause CO issues, water damage, and failed home inspections later.

#### Typical cost ranges

These are rough estimates and vary by region, labor rates, and the specifics of your setup:

- Minor fitting or flex-line repair: roughly $150 to $350 with a plumber.
- T&P valve replacement: roughly $200 to $400.
- Drain valve replacement: roughly $150 to $300.
- Full replacement of a standard 40–50 gallon tank water heater: roughly $1,500 to $2,800 installed, more in high-cost metros or if code upgrades (expansion tank, pan, drain, venting) are needed.
- Tankless replacement/new install: often $3,500 to $6,000+ installed because of venting and gas-line sizing.

If you're pricing quotes, ask specifically what is included: permit, haul-away, expansion tank, new shutoff, sediment trap for gas, and whether the quote assumes the existing location.

#### When replacement is smarter than repair

Replacement usually wins when the tank itself is leaking, when the unit is over 10 years old and needs a significant repair, when the anode has never been inspected and the water is visibly rusty, or when you are already unhappy with recovery time or capacity. Spending $600 on repairs to a 12-year-old tank that will still fail within a year or two is money you would rather put toward the new unit.

#### When to stop reading and make a call

Call for same-day help if: the tank is actively discharging water, the T&P valve is releasing on its own, you smell gas, the area is wet and electrically live, or water is reaching finished floors, subfloor, or a ceiling below. None of those wait until tomorrow.

#### Document for insurance before you clean up

If there is any meaningful water damage, take photos of the unit, the affected flooring, the drip pan (or lack of one), and the surrounding area before you mop up. Note the date and time. Homeowners insurance often covers sudden water damage from a failed appliance, but not the appliance itself, and not long-term seepage. Clean records help.

### FAQ

**Q: Is a small drip from a water heater an emergency?**
A: Not always, but it is not something to leave for next month either. A slow drip from a fitting can be handled within a few days. A drip from the base of the tank almost always means the tank is corroding through and will fail suddenly.

**Q: How long do water heaters usually last?**
A: Standard tank units typically last about 8 to 12 years. Tankless units often last longer with maintenance. Hard water and skipped anode replacements shorten lifespan noticeably.

**Q: Can I patch a leaking water heater tank?**
A: No. Temporary epoxy fixes don't hold against internal pressure and heat for long, and a tank that is leaking through the shell is at end of life.

**Q: What is a T&P valve and why does it matter?**
A: It is a safety valve that releases water if temperature or pressure inside the tank gets too high. If it drips regularly, either your system has a pressure problem or the valve is failing. It should not be plugged or capped.

**Q: Should I replace with tank or tankless?**
A: Tankless costs more up front and may need gas/venting upgrades, but saves space and offers continuous hot water. Tank is cheaper to install and fine for most households. The right answer depends on household size, fuel type, and how long you plan to stay.

## 2. AC Not Cooling: DIY Checks, Repair Cost, and When to Call HVAC

### Full Draft

#### Quick triage

Before you assume the worst, do a fast check: is the thermostat set to "Cool," is the setpoint actually below the current room temperature, and is the fan set to "Auto" rather than "On"? A thermostat on "Fan On" will blow air constantly, giving the impression the system is running even when it is not actively cooling. It sounds obvious, and it is also the source of a surprising number of service calls.

Next, look at the filter. A filter choked with dust will throttle airflow across the indoor coil, drop cooling performance, and in bad cases freeze the coil into a block of ice. If you cannot remember the last time you changed it, change it now.

#### What a healthy AC should feel like

When the system is working, the air at a supply register should be roughly 15–20°F cooler than the air going into the return. If the supply air is only a few degrees cooler, or feels room temperature, something is wrong — either airflow, refrigerant, or the compressor itself is not doing its job.

#### The five usual suspects

1. **A clogged filter or blocked return.** The cheapest, most common cause. Restricts airflow, drops capacity, and can freeze the evaporator coil.
2. **A frozen evaporator coil.** Often the downstream effect of a dirty filter, closed vents, or low refrigerant. You will sometimes see ice on the copper line at the air handler or a puddle under it when it melts.
3. **A failed run capacitor.** A common failure on outdoor condensers, especially after heat waves. The fan may not spin, or the compressor may hum and shut off. This is a common, relatively affordable repair for a tech.
4. **Low refrigerant from a leak.** Refrigerant doesn't "get used up" in a healthy system. If it's low, there is a leak somewhere. Topping off without finding the leak is a short-term fix.
5. **A failing compressor.** The most expensive cause. Often the moment where repair vs. replace becomes a real conversation, especially on older systems.

#### Homeowner-safe checks, in order

Start inside. Replace the filter. Make sure supply registers are open and returns aren't blocked by furniture. Check the drain pan under the air handler — if it's full, a safety float switch may have shut the system down. Look at the breaker panel; a tripped AC breaker is a clue, not a thing to just reset repeatedly.

Then go outside to the condenser. Is it humming? Is the big fan on top spinning freely? If the unit is running but the fan isn't turning, or the fan spins when you nudge it with a stick but not on its own, that's a classic capacitor symptom — and that is where homeowner checks end. Capacitors hold a charge even when power is off, and opening a condenser is a shock and warranty risk. Note the symptom and call.

Also check that the outdoor unit has airflow space. Tall grass, leaves packed into the coil fins, or a shrub growing against it will strangle performance. Gently rinse the outside of the coil with a garden hose, top to bottom, with the power off at the disconnect.

#### What a service call typically looks like

A standard diagnostic visit often runs in the ballpark of $90 to $200 depending on the region and whether it is an after-hours call. Common repairs and rough ranges: capacitor replacement roughly $180 to $450; contactor replacement roughly $200 to $400; refrigerant leak search and recharge often $400 to $1,500+ depending on refrigerant type and whether the leak is found; fan motor replacement roughly $400 to $700; compressor replacement often $1,800 to $3,500+, which is usually where replacing the whole outdoor unit or the full system enters the conversation.

If your system uses R-22 refrigerant, which has been phased out, recharges are expensive and the case for replacement is stronger.

#### When to stop and call today

Stop if: the outdoor unit is making a loud buzzing or grinding sound, the breaker trips immediately on reset, there is visible ice on the lines, or there is a burnt smell at the air handler. Running a damaged compressor to try to "push through" a hot afternoon is how a $400 repair becomes a $3,000 one.

#### When replacement is the smarter spend

If your system is over 12–15 years old, uses R-22, and is facing a major repair (compressor, coil, or multiple simultaneous failures), putting that money into a new high-efficiency system usually makes more sense. Newer systems also tend to dehumidify better and run quieter. Get two quotes, get equipment model numbers in writing, and ask specifically about the Manual J load calculation — oversized systems short-cycle and cool unevenly.

### FAQ

**Q: Why is my AC running but not cooling?**
A: Most often it's a restricted filter, a frozen coil, low refrigerant, or a failed capacitor in the outdoor unit. Start with the filter and a thermostat sanity check.

**Q: Can I add refrigerant myself?**
A: No. In the US, refrigerant handling requires EPA certification, the refrigerant in residential systems is regulated, and "topping off" without finding the leak wastes money and can damage the compressor.

**Q: My outdoor unit is humming but the fan isn't spinning — what is that?**
A: Classic symptom of a failed run capacitor or a seized fan motor. This is a tech call, not a DIY one.

**Q: How long should an AC system last?**
A: Central AC systems typically last 12 to 18 years with maintenance. Heat pumps tend to run closer to the lower end because they work year-round.

**Q: Is it worth repairing an old AC or replacing it?**
A: A rough rule many homeowners use: if the repair is more than about half the cost of a new system and the unit is past 10–12 years, replacement usually wins.

## 3. Ceiling Water Stain: What It Means, What to Check, and What It May Cost

### Full Draft

A water stain on the ceiling is almost never the problem itself. It is evidence that water has been somewhere it shouldn't. The job is not to hide the stain — it is to figure out why it is there before you repaint the ceiling and watch the spot come right back.

#### Active or historic?

First, decide whether the leak is ongoing or old. Press a dry paper towel against the stain. If it comes away damp, this is active. If the stain is dry, hard, and flaky around the edges, it may be from a past event (a prior roof leak, an overflowed tub upstairs, a burst pipe that's been repaired). A moisture meter, which is inexpensive, makes this much easier — readings above about 15% in drywall generally indicate current moisture.

If it is active, note when you first saw it, whether it grew after rain, after a shower upstairs, overnight with the heat or AC running, or at no obvious pattern. Those clues narrow the cause more than almost anything else.

#### Three categories of ceiling stains

Most ceiling stains fall into one of three buckets:

**Roof or envelope leaks.** These are weather-driven. They grow after rain or snowmelt and dry out between storms. The stain is often on a top-floor ceiling, near an exterior wall, a valley, or under a roof penetration (vent pipe, chimney, skylight). Stains can appear many feet from the actual leak because water travels along rafters and trusses before dropping onto drywall.

**Plumbing leaks from above.** These are usage-driven. They get worse after a shower, after laundry runs, or when a fixture is used. The stain often sits under a bathroom, kitchen, or laundry room upstairs. A slow drip under a toilet flange or tub drain will produce a classic round stain right below.

**HVAC or condensation.** These are temperature-driven. A clogged AC condensate line can overflow into the ceiling in summer. A poorly insulated duct running through an attic can sweat in humid weather. A bath fan vented into the attic instead of outside will produce stains on the ceiling in the room next to it in cold weather.

#### What's directly above?

Before checking anything, stand under the stain, look up, and ask "what is directly above this?" A bathroom, a toilet specifically, a dishwasher, a refrigerator ice-maker line, or an HVAC unit in the attic? The answer almost always points at the likely culprit.

If it is a top-floor ceiling with only the roof above, look for the nearest roof feature: a plumbing vent boot with dried-out rubber, a chimney flashing, an ice dam history, or a skylight. Old vent boot gaskets are one of the most common small-dollar roof leak causes.

#### Safe DIY checks

You can go into the attic (if you have safe access and walkable framing) with a flashlight on a dry day and look for staining on the underside of the roof deck, daylight around penetrations, or wet insulation. A second check on a rainy day, from the attic, often catches the exact drip. Do not walk on anything other than the joists or a board laid across them — stepping between joists goes through the ceiling.

For plumbing causes, run each suspect fixture for a couple of minutes and check the stain after. A slow toilet flange leak often only drips during or right after a flush.

For HVAC, in summer, check the condensate drain line exit outside. If it isn't dripping during a cooling cycle, the line may be clogged and backing up inside.

#### What repairs can cost

Wide ranges here because it depends entirely on the cause:

- Replacing a single worn roof vent boot: often $150 to $400.
- Flashing repair at a chimney or skylight: often $300 to $900.
- Clearing a clogged AC condensate line: often $150 to $300.
- Repairing a leaking toilet flange or supply line: often $200 to $600, more if subfloor damage is involved.
- Drywall repair of a typical ceiling stain area: often $250 to $600 for a small patch, including texture-matching and paint, more for large areas or high ceilings.

#### Don't just paint over it

Stain-blocking primer will cover the visual, which is why it is tempting. But if water is still getting in, paint traps moisture against drywall and makes mold more likely. Fix the source, let the drywall dry fully (a moisture meter helps confirm), then prime and paint. Painting first doesn't save time — it usually adds a second round.

#### Which pro to call

If the stain grows after rain: a roofer. If it grows after fixture use: a plumber. If it appears in cooling season near a supply vent or under an attic air handler: an HVAC tech. If you genuinely can't tell, a home inspector or a general contractor with leak-detection experience can run the diagnostics for a flat fee and save you from hiring the wrong trade twice.

### FAQ

**Q: Is a small ceiling stain dangerous?**
A: Usually not immediately, but a wet ceiling can grow mold within days, and saturated drywall can fall. The urgency depends on whether it is active.

**Q: Can I just prime and paint over a dried stain?**
A: Only if you've confirmed the source is fixed and the drywall is fully dry. A stain-blocking primer will hide the color, but painting over active moisture is a short-lived fix.

**Q: Why does the stain appear far from the actual leak?**
A: Water travels along framing and the back of drywall before it drops, so the stain often shows up several feet from the entry point, especially with roof leaks.

**Q: Should I cut open the ceiling?**
A: Sometimes yes — a small inspection hole can save guesswork. If you are comfortable cutting and patching drywall, a careful 4x4-inch hole near the stain's edge often reveals the source.

**Q: My ceiling stain hasn't grown in months. Do I still need to fix it?**
A: If you've genuinely confirmed it is dry (a moisture meter helps), you can likely just prime and repaint. But document when it appeared, so if it returns, you'll know it is active.

## 4. Breaker Keeps Tripping: Common Causes, Safe Checks, and When to Call an Electrician

**SEO Title:** Breaker Keeps Tripping: Safe Homeowner Checks Before You Call an Electrician

**Primary Keyword:** breaker keeps tripping
**Secondary Keywords:** circuit breaker tripping repeatedly, breaker won't reset, why is my breaker tripping, overloaded circuit, short circuit at home, arc fault breaker tripping, ground fault tripping breaker, when to call electrician

### Outline

### Full Draft

#### Stop resetting it

A breaker isn't a bug, it is a safety device. It is tripping because it is sensing something — too much current, a short, or a ground fault. Resetting it five times in a row without understanding why is how home fires start. So before we go anywhere, the rule is: if a breaker trips immediately on reset, especially with nothing plugged in, or if you smell anything burning at the panel or an outlet, stop and call a licensed electrician. That is not the homeowner-fix scenario.

#### Three reasons a breaker trips

Breakers trip for one of three reasons.

The first is **overload**: the total current on that circuit exceeded its rating (usually 15 or 20 amps for typical household circuits). A space heater plus a hair dryer on the same bathroom or bedroom circuit is a textbook overload.

The second is **short circuit**: a hot wire is touching a neutral or another hot somewhere — inside a damaged appliance cord, at a miswired outlet, behind a wall. Shorts usually trip the breaker instantly and hard.

The third is **ground fault**: a hot wire is leaking current to ground through an unintended path — often water intrusion, a damaged appliance, or a wire touching a metal box. GFCI and some AFCI/GFCI combination breakers are designed to catch this.

#### Figuring out which one

Here is the useful question: does the breaker trip right away when you reset it with nothing running, or does it trip only after something specific is turned on, or only after a while under normal use?

If it trips only when a specific appliance runs, that appliance or its circuit is overloaded or the appliance is faulty. Unplug everything on the circuit and reset. Plug things back one at a time and note which one causes the trip. A microwave that trips a breaker on its own when started is a bad microwave, not a bad breaker.

If it trips after a period of normal use, it is usually an overload — you are genuinely running more load than the circuit is designed for, and the fix is either moving some loads to a different circuit or, in some cases, having an electrician add a dedicated circuit.

If it trips immediately on reset, even with the circuit's outlets and switches off, this is very likely a short or a ground fault inside the wiring, a device, or the panel itself. That is a stop-and-call moment.

#### Safe checks

You can:
- Count what is on the circuit and identify obvious overloads.
- Unplug everything on that circuit, reset once, and see if it holds with nothing drawing power.
- Replace obviously damaged plugs or appliance cords (with the appliance unplugged).
- Note whether the breaker is labeled AFCI, GFCI, or a combo — those are more sensitive by design.

You should not:
- Open the electrical panel cover. There is exposed line voltage behind it.
- "Upgrade" a 15-amp breaker to a 20-amp to stop the trip. The breaker protects the wire. A bigger breaker on the same wire is how you overheat the wire inside a wall.
- Keep resetting a breaker that trips instantly.

#### AFCI and GFCI breakers are a special case

Arc-fault (AFCI) breakers, required in many US bedrooms and living areas, are designed to detect arcing — the tiny electrical sparks that precede a wiring fire. They are more sensitive than standard breakers and are sometimes tripped by specific electronics (older vacuums, treadmill motors, even some LED drivers). A repeatedly tripping AFCI should still be investigated, because the whole point is to catch real hazards.

GFCI breakers or outlets, required in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, and near sinks, detect current leaking to ground. A GFCI that trips every time it rains outside usually means water is getting into an outdoor outlet, not that the GFCI is "bad."

#### Typical cost ranges

- Electrician diagnostic call: often $100 to $200.
- Replacing a standard breaker: often $150 to $300.
- Replacing an AFCI or GFCI breaker: often $200 to $400.
- Tracing and repairing a short inside wiring: $250 to $800+, depending on access and how much drywall needs to be opened.
- Adding a dedicated circuit (e.g., for a kitchen appliance or window AC): often $350 to $900+.
- Panel replacement: usually $1,800 to $4,500+, more in older homes with aluminum branch wiring or legacy panel brands with known safety issues.

#### Red flags that mean stop

Warm or hot breaker to the touch, a burning smell at the panel, crackling or buzzing from outlets, discolored outlets or switch plates, flickering lights across multiple rooms — these are all symptoms that move the job from "inconvenience" to "safety," and they are worth an immediate call.

### FAQ

**Q: Is it bad to reset a tripped breaker?**
A: Resetting once after an obvious overload (you unplugged the space heater) is fine. Repeatedly resetting a breaker that keeps tripping is not.

**Q: Can a breaker go bad on its own?**

**Q: Why does my GFCI trip during rain?**

**Q: Can I replace a breaker myself?**
A: Physically, you can — but working inside a live panel carries real shock and arc-flash risk. Most homeowners are better off hiring this one out.

**Q: Is a tripping breaker a fire risk?**
A: The breaker itself is preventing a fire by tripping. The problem is ignoring it. Repeated tripping means something is wrong that needs attention.

**SEO Title:** Garbage Disposal Leaking? Here's Where It's Coming From and What to Do

**Search Intent:** Active problem — wants quick identification and a cost-honest answer on repair vs replace.

### Full Draft

#### Find the leak point first

Before you do anything, dry the disposal and everything under it with paper towels, then run water for a moment and watch. A disposal can appear to "leak" from three very different places, and the fix for each is completely different.

#### Top: sink flange

If the water is coming from the top of the disposal where it meets the sink, the sink flange — the metal piece you see when you look down the drain — has likely lost its plumber's putty seal. This happens after years of vibration. Water leaks from above and runs down the outside of the disposal, so it looks like the unit itself is leaking when it isn't.

This is a moderately involved DIY. You have to disconnect the disposal, drop it, remove the old flange, clean the sink surface, and reseat the flange with fresh plumber's putty (or silicone, depending on the sink material). A confident DIYer can do it in an afternoon. Otherwise, a plumber can do it in under an hour.

#### Side: dishwasher inlet or drain elbow

If the leak is at the side, look for two connections. On one side, there is often a dishwasher drain hose clamped to a small inlet. On another side, the drain elbow connects to your sink drain pipe. A loose hose clamp, a cracked rubber dishwasher hose, or a bad gasket at the drain elbow all cause side leaks. These are usually the easiest fixes: tighten the clamp, replace the hose, or replace the drain elbow gasket.

Watch for a subtle one: the knockout plug inside the disposal's dishwasher inlet. If a plumber forgot to knock it out when a dishwasher was installed, the dishwasher will back up into itself and appear to leak at the disposal. It is an easy fix once you know.

#### Bottom: the disposal itself

If the water is coming from the bottom of the unit — dripping from the very base — the internal seal around the motor shaft has failed, or the housing has corroded. Disposals are not designed to be opened and resealed. A leak from the bottom means the unit is done. Replace it.

#### DIY reality check

A flange reseal is achievable for most homeowners who own a flashlight, a bucket, and a willingness to work under a sink. A hose clamp or drain elbow swap is genuinely easy. A full disposal replacement is also within reach for a determined DIYer if the existing mount is in good shape — modern disposals often share a standard mounting system, so you can swap without plumbing redesign.

The hard parts: it is heavy and awkward to hold up under a sink while tightening the mount, the electrical connection has to be wired correctly (disposals are usually hard-wired or plugged into a switched outlet under the sink), and you have to align the discharge pipe and dishwasher hose properly.

#### Cost ranges

- Plumber service call for a disposal leak: often $150 to $350.
- Flange reseal (labor): often $150 to $300.
- Full disposal replacement installed by a plumber: often $300 to $600, sometimes more for high-horsepower units or difficult access.
- DIY unit purchase: category-level, typical mid-range disposal is roughly $120 to $300.

#### Is it worth repairing?

A 10-year-old disposal that is leaking from the bottom, getting jammed frequently, or running noticeably louder is telling you it is near end of life. Spending $200 to repair it doesn't extend its life meaningfully. Replacement often makes more sense. If you've had to hit the reset button repeatedly over the last year, that is another signal.

#### What to look for in a replacement

Think in terms of horsepower, grind stages, and sound insulation — not brand chest-pounding. A 1/2 HP unit is fine for a small household with a light food-waste load. A 3/4 HP or 1 HP unit makes sense for larger households, harder food waste, and generally lasts longer under load. Insulated units are meaningfully quieter, and once you've lived with one, it is hard to go back. Confirm the new unit's mount matches the old one; otherwise you'll be swapping the mount assembly too.

Also: check your septic system. If you are on septic, a specific septic-safe model with a lower grind rate is the right choice.

### FAQ

**Q: Can I seal a leaking garbage disposal with plumber's tape or silicone?**
A: For the flange or a drain elbow, yes, you can reseat it properly with putty or silicone. For the bottom of the unit, no — an external sealant won't hold, and that leak means the unit is done.

**Q: How long do garbage disposals last?**
A: Often about 8 to 12 years, depending on use and water hardness.

**Q: Is it safe to use the disposal while it's leaking?**
A: Depends where. A slow top leak you can usually limp along with for a day or two while you schedule a fix. A bottom leak means you risk water on the electrical connection under the sink — stop using it and shut off the power to that outlet.

**Q: Can I install a garbage disposal myself?**
A: If the electrical is already in place and the mount matches, yes, it is one of the more achievable kitchen DIY jobs. If you have to run new wiring or switch outlets, that part is better handed off.

**Q: Why does my disposal smell bad?**
A: Food residue stuck under the splash guard. Smell is separate from leaking, but running cold water with ice and citrus peels through a running disposal cleans it reasonably well.

## 6. Toilet Leaking at the Base: What Causes It and What to Do Next

**SEO Title:** Toilet Leaking at the Base? What's Really Wrong (And What It Costs to Fix)
**Meta Description:** Water pooling at the base of your toilet usually isn't the toilet itself — it's the wax ring, the flange, or the floor underneath. Here's how to tell.
**Slug:** /advice/toilet-leaking-at-base
**H1:** Toilet Leaking at the Base: Causes, Fixes, and Costs
**Page Purpose:** Help homeowners diagnose toilet-base leaks correctly and understand when it's a simple wax ring reset versus a flange or subfloor problem.
**Search Intent:** Active problem, worried about floor damage.
**Primary Keyword:** toilet leaking at base
**Secondary Keywords:** water at base of toilet, wax ring replacement, toilet flange broken, toilet rocks when sat on, subfloor rot under toilet, toilet reset cost, toilet leaking only when flushed, does my toilet need a new wax ring

### Outline
- Confirm it's actually leaking from the base, not condensation
- The wax ring and why it fails
- Flange problems: cracked, sunken, or rusted
- The worst case: rotted subfloor
- Is this a DIY job?
- Cost ranges by scenario
- Signs you should not wait
- What happens if you ignore it

### Full Draft

Water at the base of a toilet sounds alarming, and sometimes it is — but not always. The first job is figuring out whether the toilet is actually leaking or something else is going on.

#### Confirm it is a leak, not condensation

In humid months, a cold porcelain tank can sweat, and the condensation runs down and pools on the floor. If the water is clear, odorless, and shows up mostly in summer, that's likely condensation, and the fix is an insulated tank liner or a mixing valve — not a wax ring.

Also rule out the shutoff valve and supply line behind the toilet. A slow drip there runs down the wall, along the floor, and collects at the toilet base, making it look like the toilet itself is leaking.

To test: dry the floor thoroughly, place a few dry paper towels around the base, and wait. If the paper towels are wet only after flushing, it is very likely the wax ring. If they are wet continuously, it is usually the supply line or shutoff. If they are wet only on a humid morning, it is condensation.

#### The wax ring

The wax ring is the seal between the bottom of the toilet and the closet flange — the pipe fitting mounted to the floor. Over time, if the toilet has rocked even a little (uneven floor, loose closet bolts), the wax ring breaks its seal and water escapes each time you flush. Most "toilet leaking at the base" cases are exactly this.

Replacing a wax ring is one of the classic DIY plumbing jobs. You shut off the water, flush to empty the tank, disconnect the supply line, remove the closet bolts, lift the toilet (it's heavy — 70 to 100+ pounds; get help), scrape off the old wax, set a new ring on the flange, and lower the toilet back down evenly. The key is not to rock the toilet once it touches the new wax — that's how you break the new seal before you even start.

Many pros now prefer a wax-free gasket (rubber or foam) that is more forgiving and reusable if you need to pull the toilet again. Either works.

#### Flange problems

If the flange itself is cracked, sunken below floor level, or rusted through, a new wax ring won't seal properly. Signs of a bad flange: the toilet wobbles even when bolted down, you can see a gap between the flange and the floor, or the flange is visibly rusted or broken.

Fixes range from a flange extender ring (if the flange is too low after a new floor) to a full flange replacement (more invasive, sometimes involves cutting into the floor). A flange replacement on a cast-iron or ABS pipe is genuinely a plumber job for most homeowners.

#### The worst case: rotted subfloor

If a wax ring has been leaking for months, water has been soaking into the subfloor around the flange. When you pull the toilet, you may find darkened, soft, or spongy wood. In bad cases, the subfloor has to be cut out and replaced, sometimes including a section of the floor joist. This is the scenario that turns a $30 wax ring DIY into a $1,500 project, which is exactly why you don't want to let a base leak run for weeks.

#### DIY or hire?

Reseating a toilet with a new wax ring is achievable for most DIYers. Replacing a flange is doable but harder. Replacing rotted subfloor around a toilet is carpentry plus plumbing and is where many homeowners appropriately hand it off.

#### Cost ranges

- DIY wax ring: category-level $5 to $15 for the ring itself, plus maybe $20 for closet bolts.
- Plumber reset of a toilet with new wax ring: often $200 to $400.
- Flange repair or replacement (labor and parts): often $300 to $700+.
- Subfloor repair under a toilet: often $500 to $2,000+, depending on access and extent.
- Toilet replacement (entry-level): often $350 to $700 installed.

#### Don't let it run

A slow toilet-base leak is a floor-destroyer. The water that escapes is minimal each flush, which is why people ignore it for months, but it is sewage-adjacent water soaking into wood framing every day. Fix it soon, even if you have to hand it off.

### FAQ

**Q: How do I know if it's really the wax ring?**
A: Dry the floor, then flush and watch. Wet paper towels that are only wet after flushing, not between flushes, point at the wax ring.

**Q: Can I just caulk around the base?**
A: Many pros do caulk around three sides of a newly set toilet (leaving a small gap at the back so a future leak is visible). But caulking a leaking toilet without replacing the wax ring traps water inside and hides the damage — don't do that.

**Q: How long should a wax ring last?**
A: Often 20 to 30 years if the toilet never moves. It fails sooner if the toilet rocks or if it was installed poorly.

**Q: Does a rocking toilet always mean a bad wax ring?**
A: Not always, but a toilet that rocks will destroy its wax ring over time. Shim and tighten the toilet, then replace the wax ring.

**Q: Should I replace the flange when I replace the wax ring?**
A: Only if the flange is damaged or sits too low. A good flange doesn't need to be swapped on schedule.

### Full Draft

#### See the pipes

The first practical problem with a under-sink leak is visibility. Pull everything out of the cabinet, lay down a towel or a rag, and get a flashlight on the drain assembly. You cannot diagnose a leak through a row of cleaning bottles.

#### Run water and watch

Most under-sink leaks reveal themselves if you watch during and right after a test. Close the sink drain stopper, fill the basin a couple of inches, and release. Watch the p-trap and slip-joint connections as the water drains. Then run the faucet for a steady stream while you watch the tailpiece and the basket strainer from below.

A drip that only shows up when water is actively draining is almost always a slip-joint or p-trap issue. A drip that shows up only when the basin is full is often the basket strainer. A drip that shows up only when the dishwasher runs is the dishwasher drain connection at the disposal or air gap.

#### Four usual culprits

**Slip joints on the p-trap.** The white or chrome nuts holding the trap together rely on plastic or rubber washers. They loosen over time, especially if a disposal vibrates the assembly.

**The basket strainer at the sink drain.** This is the metal assembly you see when you look down into the sink drain. Its putty or gasket seal at the sink bottom can fail and leak only when the basin is full.

**The disposal drain elbow.** Already covered on the disposal page, but worth noting: if there is a disposal, it is the first suspect.

**The dishwasher drain line.** The hose from the dishwasher runs into the disposal or a dedicated dishwasher tailpiece. A cracked hose or loose clamp drips only during a dishwasher cycle.

#### Slip-joint reseat vs. p-trap swap

If a slip joint is weeping, try the simplest thing first: hand-tighten the slip nut a quarter turn. Then run water again. Plastic slip nuts are not supposed to be cranked with a wrench — over-tightening cracks the nut or flattens the washer, and then you're in a worse spot.

If the slip nut is cracked or the washer is deformed, replace the trap. A full p-trap kit is inexpensive and typically takes 10–15 minutes to swap with channel-lock pliers. While you're at it, make sure the trap is pitched correctly (the p-trap should be level, not sagging), and that the trap arm actually reaches into the wall drain by the correct amount.

One tip people forget: double-check that the washer's beveled or flat side is facing the right direction. Installing a slip washer backward is a surprisingly common cause of "it still leaks after I replaced everything."

#### Basket strainer

If water is pooling under the sink drain itself, and the p-trap is dry, it is usually the basket strainer. Fixing it means dropping the tailpiece, loosening the large locknut holding the strainer to the sink from underneath, pulling the strainer up, cleaning the putty off the sink, and resetting it with fresh plumber's putty or silicone (depending on sink material). It is a more involved DIY than a slip joint but still within reach for a confident homeowner.

#### Testing after the fix

Dry everything with paper towels. Run water for a steady minute. Fill the sink and drain it. Then, importantly, leave dry paper towels wrapped around the suspect joints and check again the next day. Slow weeps won't show up during a one-minute test but will be obvious on dry paper after 24 hours.

#### Costs

- Slip-joint washer or full p-trap DIY kit: category-level, often $10 to $25.
- Basket strainer DIY kit: often $15 to $50.
- Plumber service call for under-sink drain repair: often $150 to $350.
- Plumber replacing a basket strainer and resetting the trap: often $200 to $400.

#### When to call

If the leak is from the sink-to-countertop seal (water running down into the cabinet from above rather than from the drain), or if the drainpipe in the wall is itself leaking (not the trap you can see), that moves from under-sink DIY into plumber territory. Also call if the cabinet floor is soft — that means the leak has been going longer than you thought.

### FAQ

**Q: Why does my kitchen sink only leak when the dishwasher runs?**
A: The dishwasher drain hose connects to your disposal or tailpiece under the sink. A cracked hose or loose clamp leaks only during a cycle.

**Q: Can I use Teflon tape on slip-joint nuts?**
A: No. Slip-joint nuts seal with a washer, not threads. Teflon tape doesn't help and can actually prevent a proper seal.

**Q: How tight should slip nuts be?**
A: Hand-tight plus a light snug with channel locks. If you need real force, something is wrong (misaligned pipe, wrong-size washer, cracked nut).

**Q: My p-trap keeps clogging and leaking — are those related?**
A: Often yes. A poorly pitched trap or an undersized drain catches grease and food, which then puts pressure on every joint. Fixing the pitch and cleaning the trap often solves both.

**Q: Is a leaking basket strainer dangerous?**
A: Not dangerously, but it destroys the cabinet floor surprisingly fast because the water sits and doesn't evaporate.

## 8. Shower Caulk Failing: When to Recaulk, What to Buy, and Mistakes to Avoid

**SEO Title:** Shower Caulk Failing? When to Recaulk, What to Buy, and Mistakes to Avoid
**Meta Description:** Peeling or moldy shower caulk isn't just ugly — it lets water into your walls. Here's when to recaulk, what type of caulk to buy, and the common mistakes to skip.
**Slug:** /advice/shower-caulk-failing
**H1:** Shower Caulk Failing: Recaulking the Right Way
**Page Purpose:** Help a homeowner recaulk a shower or tub correctly, pick the right product category, and avoid the mistakes that cause recaulking to fail within months.
**Search Intent:** DIY project oriented — ready to do the job, wants a good result.
**Primary Keyword:** shower caulk failing
**Secondary Keywords:** how to recaulk a shower, best caulk for shower, silicone vs acrylic caulk shower, moldy shower caulk, caulk keeps peeling shower, remove old shower caulk, tub caulk mistakes, shower corner caulk

### Outline
- When caulk needs to come out, not be painted over
- Why recaulking jobs fail (the real reasons)
- What kind of caulk to buy — category, not brand
- The tools that actually make this a clean job
- Step-by-step: removal, prep, apply, tool, cure
- Common mistakes to avoid
- When it is a bigger problem than caulk
- Cost comparison: DIY vs. hiring someone

### Full Draft

#### When caulk is done

Shower caulk that is cracked, pulling away from the tile, blackened with mold in the bead itself, or visibly peeling is past saving. You cannot successfully apply new caulk over old caulk. The old bead has to come out completely before the new one goes in. If you skip that step — and a lot of homeowners do — the new caulk will peel within weeks.

Caulk that is just lightly discolored on the surface but still seated and intact can sometimes be cleaned with a mildew remover and left alone. But the honest answer is that most "failing" caulk is truly failing.

#### Why recaulking jobs fail

Three reasons, almost every time:

1. **The old caulk wasn't fully removed.** Even thin slivers of residue ruin adhesion.
2. **The joint wasn't dry when new caulk went on.** Showers hold moisture in the grout line long after the surfaces feel dry.
3. **The wrong type of caulk was used.** Painters caulk and general-purpose acrylic latex do not belong in a wet shower. They fail within a season.

#### What to buy — category, not brand

For showers and tubs, use 100% silicone caulk rated for kitchen and bath, with mildew-resistant additives. It adheres well to tile, tub surrounds, and glass, and it stays flexible. It is also harder to tool and can't be painted, which is why people sometimes reach for "tub and tile" acrylic latex hybrids — easier to tool, paintable, but don't hold up as long in a daily-use shower.

If you care more about a clean first attempt and less about absolute longevity, a siliconized acrylic is forgiving. If you want a 10-year job, use pure silicone.

Color matters too: white almost always, unless the tub is biscuit/bone or you have a specific tile grout color. Clear can hide minor imperfections but can look greasy in bright bathroom light.

#### Tools that make it clean

A sharp utility knife or a dedicated caulk-removal tool, a plastic putty knife, a bottle of isopropyl alcohol, blue painter's tape, a good-quality caulk gun (dripless, with a smooth pressure rod — cheap guns ruin a job), and a silicone smoothing tool or simply a gloved finger.

Painter's tape on both sides of the joint is the single biggest quality upgrade a DIYer can make. It gives you a crisp edge and hides a lot of imperfection.

#### Step by step

1. Remove all old caulk. Cut along both sides with a sharp blade, then pull the bead out in long strips where possible. Scrape any residue with the plastic putty knife.
2. Clean the joint with isopropyl alcohol (or a caulk-specific prep solvent). Any soap scum or mildew residue will ruin adhesion. Let it dry completely — at least several hours, longer if humidity is high.
3. Run painter's tape along both sides of the joint, leaving about a 3/16-inch gap where the caulk will sit.
4. Cut the caulk tube tip at a 45-degree angle, smaller than you think. A smaller tip gives you more control and a thinner, more professional bead.
5. Run a continuous, even bead into the joint in one pass if possible. Stopping and restarting adds texture.
6. Tool the bead immediately with a gloved finger or smoothing tool, using light pressure.
7. Pull the painter's tape off while the caulk is still wet — not after it has skinned over. The tape pulls a clean edge.
8. Don't shower for 24 hours. Silicone needs time to cure fully.

#### Common mistakes

- Trying to caulk over old caulk.
- Caulking a wet joint.
- Using painter's caulk because it was already in the garage.
- Cutting the tip too big and ending up with a fat, messy bead.
- Tooling the bead too hard, pushing it out of the joint.
- Skipping painter's tape.

#### When it is bigger than caulk

If you pulled the old caulk and found soft drywall, darkened wood behind the tub, or a gap between the tub and wall that is wider than 1/4 inch, the problem isn't the caulk, it is water intrusion that's already happened. Wide gaps should be backed with a foam backer rod before caulking, and real water damage behind the tile needs opening and inspection before it gets sealed up cosmetically.

#### Cost comparison

DIY: a tube of silicone caulk, a caulk gun, a removal tool, and painter's tape is a modest total, often under $40, and the job is two focused hours plus cure time. Hiring a handyman to recaulk a standard tub and surround often runs $150 to $400 depending on region and scope. The DIY version is one of the highest-ROI first projects a homeowner can take on.

### FAQ

**Q: Can I caulk over moldy caulk?**
A: No. The mold will grow through, the bond will fail, and you'll be redoing it in weeks.

**Q: How long does shower caulk last?**
A: A good silicone job in a well-ventilated bathroom can last 5 to 10 years. Poor ventilation and heavy use shorten it.

**Q: Should I use silicone or acrylic caulk?**
A: For a wet shower or tub, use 100% silicone rated for kitchen and bath. Acrylic is easier to tool but doesn't last as long in constant moisture.

**Q: Why does my caulk keep turning black?**
A: Mildew. The caulk may be mildew-resistant but not mildew-proof. Better ventilation, squeegeeing the walls after showers, and periodic cleaning all help.

**Q: Can I shower the same day I recaulk?**
A: No. Give silicone at least 24 hours to cure, longer for thick beads.

### Outline

- The four common causes, in order of severity
- How to investigate without damaging the floor
- What a localized fix looks like
- What a larger repair looks like

- Typical cost ranges
- What to document if this is pre-sale

### Full Draft

A floor should not give underfoot. When it does — when you feel a sponginess, a give, or a hollow sound under laminate or wood — that is a symptom. How serious depends entirely on what's causing it, and the range is wide: from a single warped plank to a rotted joist.

#### Categories of "soft"

Homeowners describe it differently depending on floor type. Under laminate, you'll feel a subtle give and sometimes hear a creak or click. Under engineered hardwood, it might feel cushioned in a way the rest of the floor isn't. Under solid hardwood, soft spots often come with visible cupping (edges raised) or crowning (center raised). Tile rarely feels soft on its own — if tile is soft or moves, the underlayment has genuinely failed.

#### Four common causes

**1. Laminate gap or foam-underlayment compression.** On laminate, sometimes a spot simply feels soft because the foam pad beneath has compressed from heavy traffic or furniture. Not serious. Often no fix is required other than time or swapping an affected plank.

**2. Subfloor water damage.** The most common real cause. A prior slow leak — dishwasher, refrigerator ice line, toilet, tub, or an exterior wall leak — saturated the subfloor for long enough that the plywood or OSB lost its structural integrity. Soft spots near bathrooms, kitchens, and exterior walls almost always point here.

**3. Subfloor fastener failure.** The subfloor has come unstuck from the joists below, usually because old adhesive failed or nails popped. You get movement without rot. Fixable from above with screws in some cases, and more involved in others.

**4. Joist or structural damage.** The worst case. A joist below has rotted, split, or been notched too aggressively (by a plumber or electrician in a prior renovation). Not a finish-level problem. Needs a carpenter or structural pro.

#### How to investigate

Walk the spot and map it. Is the soft area the size of a dinner plate, a rug, or the entire room? Is it only near a specific appliance, fixture, or exterior wall? Is there a corresponding water history — a prior leak, a roof issue, a slow toilet?

Look below if you can. If there is a basement or crawlspace under the affected area, go look from below with a flashlight. Water stains on the subfloor underside, soft or discolored joists, or insulation that is darkened or sagging all tell you this is water damage — and roughly where.

If you're on a concrete slab, you can't look from below. You'll need to pull a piece of baseboard or lift a plank at the edge of the soft area to see the subfloor. A moisture meter is helpful — readings over 20% in wood subfloor indicate active moisture.

#### Localized fixes

If the cause is a single laminate plank with compressed underlayment, replacing that plank and the pad under it solves it. If the cause is a small area of subfloor damage from a now-fixed leak, the fix is cutting out the damaged plywood section, sistering reinforcement where needed, and re-decking. Depending on the floor finish, this may require pulling flooring above, which is where the job gets bigger.

#### Larger repairs

When moisture has soaked a subfloor for months, the repair is often a whole-room pull of the finished floor, replacement of the subfloor in the affected area, and reinstalling the finish. At that point, many homeowners replace the finished floor anyway, because matching 10-year-old flooring is often impossible.

#### When it's structural

Signs a soft spot is structural and needs a pro right now:
- Visible sagging in the floor line when you look across the room.
- The soft spot is worsening noticeably over weeks.
- Water damage extends to a floor joist or band joist.
- The area is above a crawlspace with visible joist rot.

A structural carpenter or a licensed general contractor should look at this, not a flooring installer. In rare cases, a structural engineer is worth the several-hundred-dollar consult for peace of mind and documentation.

#### Cost ranges

- Single laminate plank replacement: DIY possible, materials often under $50; handyman labor often $150 to $300.
- Small subfloor patch (a few square feet): often $400 to $900 with finished floor reinstated.
- Full-room subfloor replacement: often $1,500 to $5,000+ depending on room size, flooring, and access.
- Joist repair or sistering: often $800 to $3,000+ per affected joist, depending on access.

#### If this is a pre-sale situation

Document when you first noticed the soft spot, any moisture readings, and any leak history in the area. Disclosure laws vary by state, and a documented, repaired issue is almost always better than a concealed active one that a buyer's inspector finds.

### FAQ

**Q: Is a soft floor dangerous?**
A: A mildly soft spot from a compressed underlayment is not. A spongy area above a rotted joist is, in the sense that failure is possible. Severity matters.

**Q: Can I just screw down a soft subfloor?**
A: Sometimes, if the issue is adhesion loss, not rot. Screwing into a rotted subfloor won't hold and won't fix the underlying problem.

**Q: Do I need to pull the flooring to diagnose?**
A: Often yes, at least at the edge of the soft area. A small inspection is usually much cheaper than repairing a problem you misjudged.

**Q: My floor feels soft after a flood — how long until it's a real problem?**
A: Within days to weeks, depending on materials. Wet OSB deteriorates faster than plywood. Dry it aggressively within the first 48 hours whenever possible.

**Q: Should I replace the whole floor if only one spot is soft?**
A: Depends on whether a matching replacement is still available. If matching is impossible, replacing the whole room often looks better than a visible patch.

## 10. Clogged AC Condensate Drain Line: Signs, DIY Steps, and When to Call for Help

**SEO Title:** Clogged AC Condensate Drain Line: Signs, DIY Fix, and When to Call
**Meta Description:** A clogged AC condensate line can flood your ceiling or shut down your AC. Here's how to recognize it, clear it yourself, and prevent it from happening again.
**Slug:** /advice/clogged-ac-condensate-drain
**H1:** Clogged AC Condensate Drain Line: What to Do
**Page Purpose:** Teach the homeowner the signs of a blocked condensate line, how to safely clear it, and when it signals a bigger HVAC issue.
**Search Intent:** Active AC issue or preventive knowledge.
**Primary Keyword:** clogged ac condensate drain line
**Secondary Keywords:** ac drain line clogged, ac leaking water inside, condensate line cleaning, ac drain line vinegar, float switch ac shut off, ac not draining outside, hvac drain line maintenance, ac drip pan overflow

### Outline
- What the condensate line does and why it clogs
- Symptoms of a clog
- Safe DIY clearing
- What not to do
- Prevention that actually works
- When a clog is a symptom of something bigger
- Cost of a service call
- Why new homes may be more prone to this

### Full Draft

Your central AC doesn't just cool air. It pulls humidity out of it, and that humidity has to go somewhere. It runs across the cold evaporator coil, condenses into water, drips into a drain pan, and exits the home through a small PVC pipe — usually 3/4 inch — that runs outside. When that pipe clogs with algae, dust, or debris, the water backs up, and trouble begins.

#### Symptoms

The classic signs of a clogged condensate line:

- Water pooling around the indoor air handler or furnace.
- A ceiling stain forming below an attic-mounted air handler, often during cooling season.
- The AC shutting off on its own, thanks to a safety float switch that detects pan overflow.
- A musty smell coming through the vents.
- No water dripping from the line's outdoor exit point during a cooling cycle on a hot, humid day.

If you have an attic air handler, a clog can dump water into the ceiling below within hours. This is one of the more common causes of "ceiling stain in summer" and a very common insurance claim.

#### Safe DIY clearing

You will need: a wet/dry vacuum, tape or a rag, and a small amount of distilled white vinegar.

1. Turn the AC off at the thermostat.
2. Find the outdoor end of the condensate line — a small PVC pipe, often near the foundation, near the outdoor condenser.
3. Hold the wet/dry vac tightly against the end of the pipe. Use tape or a rag to improve the seal.
4. Run the vac for 1–2 minutes. You will often hear the clog come loose and pull into the vac.
5. Locate the line's access point inside — usually a T-fitting or cleanout cap near the air handler.
6. Pour about a cup of warm water to test that it drains freely. Then pour a cup of distilled white vinegar into the line to slow algae growth.
7. Restart the AC and confirm water drips out the outdoor end after a cooling cycle.

#### What not to do

- Don't pour bleach into a PVC drain line that has metal fittings or a metal drain pan — it can corrode them. Vinegar is the safer default.
- Don't blow compressed air forcefully back through the line. It can blow apart glued PVC joints or force water into the air handler cabinet.
- Don't reset the float switch repeatedly without clearing the clog. The float switch shut off your AC to protect your ceiling. Listen to it.

#### Prevention

Pour a cup of distilled white vinegar down the cleanout a couple of times a cooling season. Keep the outdoor end of the line clear of dirt, mulch, and wasp nests. If your unit is in an attic, make sure the secondary drain pan under it is intact and unobstructed — that is your backup when the primary fails.

#### When it's a bigger problem

A clog that comes back within weeks usually means the line slope isn't right, the drain trap is designed poorly, or the evaporator coil itself is dirty and shedding biofilm into the drain. An HVAC tech should look at the slope and the coil condition. Coil cleaning on a schedule (every few years, depending on conditions) keeps this from being a recurring issue.

#### Cost

- DIY: basically the cost of a wet/dry vacuum (which you should own anyway) and a bottle of vinegar.
- HVAC service call for a clog: often $150 to $300.
- Full drain line repair (re-pitching or replacing sections): often $300 to $700.
- Coil cleaning (when recommended): often $200 to $500.

#### Newer homes can be worse

High-efficiency and variable-speed systems pull more condensate than older single-stage systems — they remove more humidity. That is a good thing for comfort and a harder thing for the drain line. Pair that with attic installs common in slab-foundation regions and you have a recurring clog recipe if maintenance is skipped.

### FAQ

**Q: Why does my AC shut off on its own and then restart?**
A: Likely a safety float switch detecting pan overflow from a clogged drain line. Clear the line before you start troubleshooting the AC itself.

**Q: Is vinegar or bleach better for an AC drain line?**
A: Distilled white vinegar is the safer default for most systems. Bleach can corrode metal pan components and some fittings.

**Q: How often should I flush the condensate line?**
A: A couple of times during cooling season is a reasonable baseline, more often in humid climates.

**Q: Can a clogged drain line ruin my AC?**
A: It can damage the air handler cabinet, the electronics inside, and the ceiling below. The AC itself usually survives if you catch it quickly.

**Q: My outdoor drain line doesn't drip — is that always a problem?**
A: Not always. On a dry, cool day, the AC may not make much condensate. On a hot, humid afternoon, you should see a steady drip.

### Outline

### Full Draft

Bathroom sink cabinets are one of the most common hidden water-damage locations in a home. The cabinet is closed, the leaks are slow, and by the time you notice a musty smell or a warped base, the damage is already done. The fix is not glamorous, but the diagnosis is straightforward if you isolate the source carefully.

#### Empty it and look

Take everything out of the cabinet. Wipe the base dry. Get a flashlight. You cannot diagnose this with half a shelf of products in the way.

#### The five bathroom-specific leak points

**1. The shutoff valves on the wall.** The two small valves behind the sink that feed hot and cold to the faucet. They leak at the valve stem or at the compression fitting against the wall.

**2. The supply lines.** Flexible lines, usually braided stainless or plastic, connect the shutoff valves to the faucet inlets. These fail — cracked plastic lines or corroded fittings — more often than people think.

**3. The p-trap and slip joints.** Same story as the kitchen sink: slip nuts loosen over time, washers deform.

**4. The pop-up drain assembly.** The mechanism that lets you close the drain by pulling up the rod behind the faucet. It seals at the sink drain fitting with putty or a gasket, and at the rod entry to the tailpiece. Either can leak.

**5. The faucet-to-sink seal.** Sometimes water running off the handles drips down under the sink through the faucet's mounting holes if the gasket under the faucet has failed.

#### Testing each

Dry the cabinet base. Lay a few dry paper towels under each suspect area: under each shutoff valve, under the p-trap, under the pop-up rod entry, and directly under the drain. Then run the faucet hot and cold for a minute, let it sit, fill and drain the sink, and check the paper towels.

- Wet paper only under the shutoff: it's the valve.
- Wet paper only under the supply line: it's the line.
- Wet paper only under the p-trap after draining: slip-joint issue.
- Wet paper only under the pop-up when water is standing in the sink: drain assembly.
- Wet paper under the faucet center when you splash water around the handles: faucet gasket.

#### Stabilization

If you can't fix it right now but you've identified it, here are reasonable stopgaps:

- Leaking supply line: shut off that valve (hot or cold) and live without it for a day.
- Leaking shutoff valve: shut off the main house supply and use the stop only when you need water.
- Leaking p-trap: don't fill the sink; use the faucet lightly.
- Leaking drain: avoid filling the basin.

A tray or a shallow bin under the leak catches drips and protects the cabinet floor while you schedule the real fix.

#### Reasonable DIY fixes

Replacing a supply line is one of the easiest plumbing DIYs: shut off the valve, unscrew the old line, screw on the new one, turn the water back on. Slip-joint reseats are similar to the kitchen guide. Replacing a pop-up drain assembly is more involved but still achievable with a deep socket or basin wrench and some patience.

Replacing a shutoff valve is harder. Modern compression-fit or quarter-turn valves can be swapped, but it involves shutting off the house main, cutting and refitting the supply stub if the valve is a soldered/sweated type, and verifying the new valve doesn't leak under pressure. A confident DIYer can do it; many reasonably hire this one out.

#### Cabinet base damage

While the cabinet is empty, look at its base carefully. Soft, bubbled, darkened, or delaminated particleboard tells you the leak has been going longer than you realized. You'll often find the damage is worse than the water you see at the moment.

Repair options depend on severity: sealing and painting over minor surface damage is a stopgap, replacing the cabinet base (or the whole cabinet) is the right fix for real damage. Waterproof vinyl cabinet liners help protect a replacement from the next slow leak.

#### When the faucet itself is the leak

If the water is appearing around the base of the faucet on top of the sink, not under it, the faucet's o-rings or cartridge are failing. That is a different repair — often a $20 cartridge and a wrench fits it — and belongs to the faucet repair page.

#### Costs

- Supply line: category-level DIY part is usually $10 to $25.
- Shutoff valve: DIY part often $15 to $30; plumber labor often $200 to $400 installed.
- Pop-up drain assembly: part often $20 to $60; plumber labor often $150 to $300 installed.
- Cabinet base replacement: often $300 to $800 if base only; much more if the whole vanity is replaced.

### FAQ

**Q: How do I know if my bathroom supply line is about to fail?**
A: Look for green or white crust at the fittings, bulges in the line, or any dampness. Many pros replace them preventively every 5 to 10 years.

**Q: Can I fix a leaking shutoff valve without turning off the main water?**
A: No. The shutoff valve is the barrier. To repair it you have to shut off upstream, which in most homes means the main.

**Q: Why does my sink only leak when it's full?**
A: That points at the pop-up drain assembly or a crack at the overflow, not the p-trap.

**Q: Is plastic or metal supply line better?**
A: Braided stainless steel is generally more durable than plastic. Either should be replaced if it's obviously aged.

**Q: My cabinet base is bubbled but still solid — do I have to replace it?**
A: If it's still structurally sound and dry, a vinyl liner over it is a reasonable stopgap. Bubbled particleboard will not get better, though — it's decision-time for the cabinet.

## 12. GFCI Outlet Keeps Tripping: Common Causes and Safe Troubleshooting Steps

**SEO Title:** GFCI Outlet Keeps Tripping: What It Means and How to Troubleshoot Safely
**Meta Description:** A GFCI outlet that keeps tripping is doing its job — but figuring out why takes a careful approach. Here's how to narrow it down.

**Primary Keyword:** gfci outlet keeps tripping

### Outline

- First, find the right GFCI
- Methodical appliance isolation
- Weather and moisture trips

- Safe replacement basics

### Full Draft

#### What a GFCI actually senses

A GFCI (ground-fault circuit interrupter) monitors the difference between current going out the hot wire and coming back on the neutral. In a healthy circuit, those match. If the GFCI sees a mismatch — even a tiny one, around 5 milliamps — it assumes current is leaking somewhere it shouldn't (through water, through a person, through a damaged appliance) and it cuts power very fast. That is the whole point. A tripping GFCI is not a bug; it is what saves people from serious shock.

#### Four real reasons GFCIs trip

**1. Water.** Moisture in an outdoor outlet, a bathroom outlet behind a mirror with condensation, or a basement outlet near a damp wall. Classic cause, especially after rain.

**2. A faulty appliance.** A hair dryer with a damaged cord, an old vacuum, a refrigerator with a failing compressor, or an outdoor pump with worn insulation. The appliance is leaking current to ground and the GFCI is catching it.

**3. A shared or miswired circuit.** Sometimes a GFCI "feeds" other outlets downstream. One bad outlet or fixture anywhere on that chain will trip the upstream GFCI.

**4. An end-of-life GFCI.** GFCI outlets have a lifespan — often 10 to 15 years. The internal mechanism can fail to a "trip" state.

#### Finding the right GFCI

GFCIs often protect more outlets than the one that is obviously affected. An outlet in the garage that is dead may be protected by a GFCI in the bathroom. Before you assume the outlet at your feet is broken, push the "Reset" button on every GFCI in the house — kitchen, bathrooms, garage, outside, basement. A tripped GFCI somewhere else is a common cause of a dead outlet.

#### Methodical isolation

If a GFCI trips as soon as you plug in a specific appliance, that appliance is the cause until proven otherwise. Try it in a non-GFCI outlet briefly (or better, plug in a different appliance to confirm the GFCI holds). If the appliance trips every GFCI you try, it is the appliance.

If the GFCI trips when nothing is plugged in, the problem is likely in the wiring or an outlet downstream of the GFCI. That's the point to stop homeowner-level troubleshooting.

#### Weather and moisture

Outdoor GFCIs often trip after rain or snowmelt. The fix:
- Make sure the "in-use" bubble cover is intact and closed.
- Check that the outlet gasket behind the cover is seated.
- If the circuit has an outdoor fixture or pump, check that fixture for water ingress.

A persistently wet outdoor box sometimes needs to be relocated, re-sealed, or fitted with a weather-resistant (WR) outlet — a conversation for an electrician if basic resealing doesn't hold.

#### When the GFCI itself has failed

Symptoms that the GFCI itself is dying: the reset button won't stay in even with nothing on the circuit, the test button doesn't trip the outlet, the outlet is warm to the touch, or the face is discolored. A modern GFCI also has an end-of-life indicator (often a small LED) that tells you it has self-diagnosed a failure.

#### Safe replacement basics

Replacing a GFCI outlet is within reach for a careful DIYer, but the stakes are real. Shut off the breaker feeding the outlet, verify it is dead with a non-contact voltage tester on both slots (not just by flipping the light switch), and note which wires are "line" and which are "load" — a miswired GFCI won't protect downstream outlets and can appear to work while leaving a shock hazard.

If you are not confident identifying line vs. load, or if the box has multiple cables in it, hand it to an electrician. It is a one-visit job.

#### When to call

Call an electrician if: the GFCI trips immediately with nothing plugged in and nothing downstream, the breaker itself has tripped along with the GFCI, the outlet is warm or discolored, or you smell any burning. Same as with panel breakers: a tripping GFCI is preventing a hazard. Understand it, don't suppress it.

#### Cost ranges

- DIY GFCI outlet: category-level $15 to $40.
- Electrician GFCI replacement: often $150 to $300.
- Full diagnostic for intermittent tripping: often $200 to $400.

### FAQ

**Q: My GFCI won't reset — what do I do?**
A: Unplug everything on the circuit, then try to reset. If it still won't hold, the issue is in the wiring or the GFCI itself. That is an electrician call.

**Q: Can a GFCI trip randomly for no reason?**
A: Not really. There is always a reason — water, a load that leaks a small amount of current, a failing GFCI, or weather. "Random" usually means you haven't found the cause yet.

**Q: Why does my outdoor GFCI trip only when it rains?**
A: Water is getting into an outlet or fixture on that circuit. The GFCI is doing exactly what it's designed to do.

**Q: How long does a GFCI outlet last?**
A: Usually 10 to 15 years. Outdoor GFCIs often fail sooner due to weather exposure.

**Q: Should every outlet in my house be a GFCI?**
A: No. The US electrical code requires GFCI protection in specific locations — kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, near sinks. Your electrician can tell you if your home meets current requirements.

- What "save it" vs "replace it" actually looks like
- Check behind the wall — the step people skip
- Mold: what's homeowner-scope and what isn't
- Patching small areas vs. cutting and replacing
- Primer and paint so the stain doesn't come back

### Full Draft

A leak has been fixed, or is going to be fixed soon, and now there is wet, warped, or stained drywall. The urge is to patch, prime, and paint as quickly as possible. Resist it. What you do in the next few days determines whether this is a $200 repair or a $3,000 one in six months.

#### Dry first

Drywall can hold water well beyond what the surface shows. Before you cut, patch, or even fully diagnose, get air moving. Fans aimed at the affected area, a dehumidifier in the room, and removing trim and baseboards where possible all help. A moisture meter — inexpensive and one of the better single-tool investments for a homeowner — takes the guesswork out. Drywall in the normal range reads roughly 5 to 12% moisture. Above 17% or so, it is still wet.

Most drywall drying takes at least several days, sometimes a week or more. Patching wet drywall is a recipe for mold growth inside the wall.

#### Save it or replace it?

Drywall that is only surface-stained and still firm, flat, and not sagging can often be primed with a stain-blocking primer and painted over. Drywall that is soft, crumbles when pressed, visibly sagging, or has swollen and broken along its paper face cannot be saved. It has to be cut out.

A quick test: press firmly with a gloved finger. If it feels like damp cardboard, it's done.

#### Check behind the wall

This is the step most homeowners skip, and it is the single most important one. Before you close anything back up, look behind the affected drywall.

- Is the insulation wet or moldy? Wet insulation loses R-value and stays wet far longer than drywall. If it is wet, remove it.
- Is the framing — studs, plates, sheathing — wet, discolored, or soft? Wet wood needs to dry thoroughly before closing. A moisture meter reading on framing above about 16–18% means it is still holding water.
- Are there obvious signs the leak actually stopped? If the source wasn't fixed, don't close the wall.
- Is there visible mold on the framing, sheathing, or insulation paper?

This is also the moment to upgrade anything you've been meaning to fix — a questionable plumbing fitting that caused the leak, an undersized shutoff valve, a missing drip loop on a pipe passing through framing.

#### Mold — what's homeowner-scope and what isn't

A small area of surface mildew on drywall, less than about 10 square feet, is generally considered homeowner-scope. Clean with soap and water or a dedicated product, dry thoroughly, and monitor. Beyond that, or any time mold is in the framing cavity, HVAC ducts, or insulation, the right move is a mold remediation professional. That is not the same as painting it over and hoping. Personal health sensitivities, HVAC involvement, and recurring water intrusion all raise the threshold for pro involvement.

This page does not offer medical advice on mold exposure. If anyone in the home has respiratory conditions or severe allergies, getting professional guidance earlier is reasonable.

#### Patching vs. cutting and replacing

For small holes or stain areas, a peel-and-stick drywall patch kit and some joint compound gets you a serviceable result in an afternoon. Feather the compound wider than you think, sand, and prime before painting.

For anything larger than a small patch, or any area where the surface is damaged, cut back to the nearest studs (or to clean drywall on both sides), replace the piece with new drywall of matching thickness, tape and mud the joints, and texture to match. The general pro rule: cut back to where the drywall is clearly dry and undamaged, not where you hope it is.

For ceilings, be extra conservative. Sagging ceiling drywall is a falling hazard. Cut out and replace; don't rely on paint to cover compromised material.

#### Primer and paint

Even on dried, firm drywall, stains bleed through standard latex paint. Use a stain-blocking primer — shellac- or oil-based — over stained areas. Topcoat with matching paint. For ceilings, consider repainting the entire ceiling rather than spot-priming; a freshly primed patch will almost always stand out under interior light.

#### Cost ranges

- DIY small patch kit: category-level, often $15 to $40 including compound, tape, sandpaper, primer, paint.
- Professional drywall patch of a typical leak area: often $300 to $700 including texture match and paint.
- Drywall replacement by the section: often $50 to $80 per square foot of repair (labor + materials + texture + paint), though this varies.
- Full-room ceiling drywall replacement: often $1,500 to $3,500+.
- Mold remediation: varies widely; small-scope often $500 to $1,500, larger jobs much more.

#### When to bring in a pro

Bring in a pro if: the damaged area is larger than a couple of square feet on a ceiling, you suspect mold beyond surface staining, the framing behind the wall is wet or damaged, or the drywall work has to match a textured ceiling (popcorn, knockdown, orange peel) you don't have the tools to replicate convincingly.

### FAQ

**Q: How long should I wait before patching drywall after a leak?**
A: At least several days, often longer. The material must be genuinely dry, not just surface-dry. A moisture meter removes the guesswork.

**Q: Can I just paint over a water stain?**
A: If the drywall is dry and sound, yes — but use a stain-blocking primer first. Regular paint over a stain will bleed through.

**Q: How big a hole can I patch myself?**
A: Peel-and-stick patches work well up to about 6 inches. Larger holes are better handled by cutting a square back to studs and installing a new piece.

**Q: What does mold behind drywall look like?**
A: Dark staining on insulation paper, black or greenish spots on framing, or a musty smell when you open the wall. Not every dark spot is mold, but any visible growth warrants a cautious response.

**Q: Should I replace the insulation behind a wet wall?**
A: Yes, if it's wet. Wet fiberglass or cellulose insulation loses insulating value and holds moisture against the framing.

## 14. Sump Pump Not Working: What to Check First and When Replacement Makes More Sense

**SEO Title:** Sump Pump Not Working? What to Check Now (Before the Basement Floods)
**Meta Description:** A sump pump only matters when it's needed. Here's how to check yours, troubleshoot common failures, and know when replacement is smarter than repair.
**Slug:** /advice/sump-pump-not-working

### Outline
- First-check sequence before anything else

- Burned motor and stuck impeller
- Reasonable DIY vs. hire
- Backup systems are worth discussing
- Cost of a new pump and install

### Full Draft

A sump pump sits quietly for months and then everything depends on it one afternoon. If you have any doubt about yours, especially if a storm is coming or snow is melting, test it now. Don't wait until the basin is filling.

#### First-check sequence

1. Is it plugged in? Sump pumps get unplugged for freezer access, for outlet work, or by accident. Check first.
2. Is the outlet live? Sump pumps are often on GFCI outlets. A tripped GFCI leaves the pump dead. Reset and confirm.
3. Lift the float manually (or pour a bucket of water in the basin). The pump should kick on within seconds. If it doesn't, the problem is electrical or mechanical.
4. If it kicks on and runs but no water moves out the discharge, that is a different failure mode.

#### Why sump pumps fail

Four common failures:

**1. The float switch is stuck.** Debris in the basin, a tether caught on the basin wall, or a worn float itself — the motor can't turn on because the switch isn't signaling. Most common cause, by far.

**2. The check valve failed.** The check valve on the discharge pipe prevents water from running back into the basin. When it fails, water cycles from the discharge back into the pit, and the pump runs constantly.

**3. The discharge line is clogged or frozen.** Debris, dirt, mud, or ice. Pump runs, water doesn't leave.

**4. Motor or impeller failure.** Burnt smell, hot housing, humming with no flow, or silence when the float lifts. End-of-life.

#### Float switch — the quick fix

Unplug the pump, pull it out of the basin (wear gloves), and inspect the float. If it is a tethered float, confirm the tether moves freely. If it is a vertical float, confirm the rod slides without resistance. Clean any debris from the basin bottom. Reinstall and retest by pouring a bucket of water in. Many "dead" sump pumps come back to life after a five-minute float cleaning.

#### Check valve and discharge

With the pump unplugged, look at the discharge pipe. The check valve is usually an inline fitting a foot or two above the pump. A failed check valve is inexpensive to replace — often $15 to $30 for the part.

For the discharge line outside: on freezing days, check that the exterior discharge is clear and not frozen. Ice dams at the discharge point are a common winter cause of pump failure. A freeze guard or a pop-off fitting can help.

#### Motor and impeller

If the motor hums when the float lifts but nothing happens, the impeller may be seized with debris, or the motor is burned out. A seized impeller sometimes frees with a gentle manual turn (pump unplugged). A burnt motor is a replacement conversation.

A sump pump that is running constantly, is hot to the touch, or smells burnt is telling you it is failing. Don't rely on it through the storm; replace it.

#### Reasonable DIY vs. hire

Replacing a sump pump is within reach for a moderately handy homeowner. Shut off the breaker, disconnect the discharge pipe (usually with a union fitting — if there isn't one, you may have to cut and reconnect), lift the old pump, drop in the new one, reconnect, reset breaker, and test with a bucket of water.

The pitfalls: mismatched discharge pipe sizes, a basin that is too small for the replacement, a pump undersized for the home's actual water volume, and primary-only systems in a home that really needs a battery backup.

#### Backup systems

A primary sump pump is single-point-of-failure protection. It fails exactly when the power fails, which is exactly when you need it — during storms. A battery backup sump pump runs on a deep-cycle battery and kicks in when the primary fails or loses power. A water-powered backup uses municipal water pressure and requires no battery. Both add real resilience.

If the finished basement value exceeds a few thousand dollars, a backup system pays for itself on the first storm it handles.

#### Costs

- Sump pump DIY replacement: category-level pump cost often $150 to $400, plus a check valve and maybe a new discharge fitting.
- Professional sump pump replacement: often $500 to $1,000 installed.
- Battery backup system: often $400 to $900 for the unit plus install.
- Water-powered backup: often $300 to $600 plus install.
- Pit cleaning or discharge line repair: often $200 to $500.

#### Sump pump lifespan

Primary sump pumps typically last 7 to 10 years. If yours is that old, especially if it has been running hard, plan replacement before failure rather than after.

### FAQ

**Q: My sump pump is humming but not pumping — what is that?**
A: Usually a seized impeller or a stuck float. Unplug, inspect, and clear debris. If the motor hums without the impeller turning, the pump is likely done.

**Q: How often should I test my sump pump?**
A: At least twice a year, especially before rainy or snowmelt seasons. Pour a bucket of water in the basin and confirm the pump kicks on and the water clears.

**Q: Do I need a battery backup?**
A: If your basement is finished, if you have expensive items down there, or if your area loses power during storms — yes, it is reasonable insurance.

**Q: Why is my sump pump running all the time?**
A: Usually a failed check valve cycling water back into the pit. Sometimes a high water table or a broken discharge line dumping water near the foundation.

**Q: How long do sump pumps last?**
A: Primary pumps typically last 7 to 10 years. Heavy-use pumps fail sooner.

## 15. Faucet Dripping: What Usually Causes It and When It Is Worth Fixing

**SEO Title:** Faucet Dripping? What's Causing It and When It's Worth the Fix

### Full Draft

#### The waste is real, if not dramatic

A steady drip can waste a few gallons a day — enough to notice on a water bill over months, and more than enough to stain a sink or corrode a drain. It is also a slow indicator of something inside the faucet giving up. Worth fixing, if only because it gets worse.

#### Four faucet designs

Most household faucets fall into one of four categories, and each one fails in its own way:

**Cartridge faucets.** A cylindrical cartridge controls hot/cold and on/off. Most common in modern kitchen and bathroom faucets. Leaks usually come from a worn cartridge or worn o-rings on the spout.

**Ball faucets.** Common on older single-handle kitchen faucets. A metal or plastic ball sits over springs and rubber seats. Leaks are typically from worn seats, springs, or the cam gasket.

**Ceramic disc faucets.** A sealed ceramic disc cartridge. Durable, long-lived, but when they finally leak, the whole disc assembly is usually replaced.

**Compression faucets.** The old-school two-handle design where you actually screw a washer down against a seat. Common on outdoor spigots and very old interior faucets. Leaks are almost always a worn rubber washer at the base of the stem or a worn seat in the valve body.

#### Identifying your faucet

- Two handles, fully closing with real resistance? Compression.
- Single handle, older/commercial look? Likely a ball.
- Single handle, smooth modern feel? Cartridge or ceramic disc.
- Two handles, quarter-turn open/close? Ceramic disc.

If you're unsure, take the faucet apart. The shape of the internal part tells you immediately.

#### Cartridge swap — the most common modern repair

Shut off the hot and cold valves under the sink. Open the faucet to release pressure. Remove the handle (usually one screw under a decorative cap). Pull the cartridge straight up and out — sometimes a cartridge puller is needed. Bring the old cartridge to the store, match it exactly, and drop the new one in. Reassemble.

The whole job often takes 20–30 minutes once you've done it once. Write down or photograph the faucet brand and model when you buy — having that info saves time on the next repair years later.

#### O-ring swap — for spout-base leaks

If water leaks around the base of the spout where the spout meets the body (not at the handle or the spout tip), the o-rings around the spout are worn. Pulling the spout out, replacing the o-rings with correctly sized replacements, and applying a thin smear of silicone faucet grease usually solves it.

#### When replacement makes more sense

- The faucet is old and scratched and you don't like it anymore.
- Parts are no longer available for the model.
- The faucet body itself is cracked or heavily corroded.
- A cartridge replacement just doesn't stop the drip.

Mid-range kitchen and bathroom faucets are affordable, and modern ones use widely available replacement cartridges. For many homeowners, replacing a 20-year-old faucet they are already tired of makes more sense than chasing parts.

#### Tools worth owning

A basin wrench (for reaching the nuts behind a faucet), an adjustable wrench, a cartridge puller, channel-lock pliers, and a small tube of silicone faucet grease. That is most faucet work covered.

#### Costs

- DIY cartridge replacement: category-level part often $15 to $50. Time: under an hour.
- DIY o-ring kit: often $5 to $15.
- Plumber faucet repair: often $150 to $300.
- New mid-range faucet DIY install: part often $100 to $300; time: 1 to 2 hours.
- Plumber faucet replacement install: often $200 to $500 plus the faucet.

### FAQ

**Q: Is a drip that isn't constant worth fixing?**
A: Yes, because it will get worse. Internal wear doesn't reverse itself.

**Q: Can I use plumber's tape on faucet threads?**
A: On water supply line connections, yes. Inside cartridges and o-ring seats, no — those rely on proper gaskets or o-rings.

**Q: How long do faucets last?**
A: Quality faucets often last 15 to 20 years with occasional cartridge replacement. Cheaper ones may only last 5 to 10.

**Q: Why does my faucet drip only when the water is hot?**
A: The hot side of a two-handle faucet has worn or the hot side of a cartridge is damaged. Replace the cartridge or the hot-side seat.

**Q: Should I match the cartridge to the brand?**
A: Yes. Cartridges are not universal. Bring the old one to the store, or note brand and model for an exact replacement.
