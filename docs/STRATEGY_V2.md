## A. Honest Evaluation

### Where the revised plan is STRONGER than the original memo

1. **Scope is realistic.** The original plan wanted a solo founder to build 3 tools, hire an expert, publish 20 articles, start a newsletter, and plan a YouTube channel — in 90 days. That's startup suicide for one person. The lean version finishes. The original doesn't.

2. **Single wedge is cleaner.** "DIY or Hire" as THE brand identity is brandable; "cost transparency + decision + appliance diagnostics" is a three-way split that confuses positioning. One door users walk through beats three.

3. **Trust pages (editorial standards / methodology / affiliate disclosure / contact) elevated to launch-day requirement.** The original memo mentioned them but buried them. The revised plan makes them explicit. On a "consumer advocate" brand, these pages ARE the product on day one.

4. **"Recommendations only where tied to cost/decision"** kills the instinct to build a Wirecutter-clone listicle section. That instinct has killed more solo operators than any other single mistake post-HCU.

5. **One tool first, not three.** A real improvement.

### Where it's WEAKER

1. **Permanently killing the appliance error-code database is a strategic mistake.** Defer it, don't delete it. That's the longest-tail, least-competitive, best-defensible traffic engine in the whole plan — thousands of low-competition model-specific queries (iFixit is the only real competitor). Shouldn't build it in the first 90 days, agreed. But labelling it "not part of launch" is different from "not part of the business." Keep it on the 12-month roadmap.

2. **The YMYL contradiction in the first 10 pages is the single biggest risk.** Three of the ten launch topics — panel replacement, GFCI, and garage door opener (springs can kill you) — are YMYL under Google's 2025 Quality Rater Guidelines. Publishing YMYL electrical content without a credentialed reviewer:
   - Won't rank (Google explicitly wants credentialed authorship for life-safety topics).
   - Contradicts the brand promise ("we're more trustworthy than Angi" while having no credentials).
   - Creates real liability if a reader injures themselves and points to your advice.

   You can't have "consumer advocate on home repair decisions" without expertise behind the advocacy. Either (a) hire or partner with an expert for YMYL pages specifically, or (b) don't publish those pages in launch wave. Having neither is the contradiction.

3. **The DIY-or-Hire tool as specified is a BuzzFeed quiz, not a moat.** Inputs like "safety risk" and "skill level" are *user-supplied subjective judgments*. That's not differentiated — every DIY blog has some version of "consider your skill level before starting." A real decision tool works the other way: user inputs the job ("replace GFCI outlet") and the tool outputs the safety classification, permit rules by state, time estimate from real data, DIY cost, and pro cost. The job is the input; everything else is the tool's knowledge. The quiz version asks the user to do the work the tool should do.

4. **10 launch pages is too few for Google to read "this is a real publisher on this topic."** Topical authority signal needs 15–20 pages on day one, not 10. The marginal cost of 5 more pages is 2 weeks of writing; the benefit is meaningful rankings in month 4 vs. month 8.

5. **"Honest home repair decisions for homeowners" is weaker than "consumer advocate for home repair."** "Decisions" is neutral/intellectual; "advocate" is emotional and directional — it implies *fighting for you, against someone*. Since the actual differentiator against Bob Vila/This Old House/Angi is not having their advertiser conflicts, lean into it. Advocacy is a sharper sword.

### What to KEEP from the revised plan

- 3-pillar scoping with DIY-or-Hire central
- Lean page count at launch (directionally)
- Start with one tool
- All standard trust pages at launch
- Brand tagline "Fix it right, not twice"
- Monetization ordering (lead-gen → display → affiliate → email/digital)
- No broad tutorial library, no tool-review section, no media-style operation

### What to CUT from the revised plan

- **"Never build the appliance error-code database"** — change to "phase 2, months 6–12."
- **Publishing the 3 YMYL pages (panel, GFCI, garage door opener)** without a credentialed reviewer arranged. Either arrange per-article review ($25–$75/review — much cheaper than a retainer) or swap them out.
- **The quiz-style decision tool** — replace with a data-backed, job-first version.

### What to CHANGE

- **Expert reviewer: not a full retainer, but yes for YMYL.** Per-article review at $25–$75/review from a licensed pro (find via LinkedIn or r/askanelectrician subreddits). Non-YMYL pages (cost guides, garbage disposal, drywall, contractor vetting) don't need it. This is the compromise: keep launch lean, don't compromise YMYL trust.
- **Scale first wave from 10 → 15 pages.** Same topics, more coverage per pillar. 5 decision / 5 cost / 5 consumer-advocate.
- **Homepage explainer should include a number.** "Know what to fix, what to buy, and when to hire help" is fine but bland. Try: "Real costs. Honest decisions. From $120 faucet fixes to $12,000 roof replacements."

## B. Is this realistic for a solo founder?

**Yes. Blunt: far more realistic than the original memo.**

The original memo was a venture-backed playbook dressed up as a solo one. The revised plan is a *solo* plan. It will survive 90 days without burnout. The original would have produced a half-built site and a burned-out founder by month 3.

The only caveat: **a solo founder who cuts the expert-reviewer step to zero on YMYL topics is saving the wrong dollar.** $25–$75 per YMYL article is the single highest-ROI spend in the entire plan — it's what separates the survivors of the HCU era from the dead.

## C. Best Final Version of the Strategy

### Core positioning (tightened)

> **FixItReal is a consumer advocate for home repair. We tell you honestly what a repair should cost, whether to DIY or hire, and who's worth your money — so you fix it right, not twice.**

### Brand stack
- **Tagline:** "Fix it right, not twice."
- **Homepage explainer:** "Real costs. Honest decisions. From $120 faucet fixes to $12,000 roof replacements."
- **Brand angle:** Consumer advocate (primary), with data-desk rigor (secondary). Not "helpful blogger."

### Pillars (keep the 3, reordered)

1. **DIY or Hire** — the wedge. Central to brand.
2. **Cost Guides** — the money pages. Lead-gen and Mediavine both live here.
3. **Consumer-Advocacy Editorial** — contractor vetting, home-warranty exposés, scam red flags. Underweight in the revised version. This is what NO competitor can copy. Expand it.

**Product reviews** demoted to "context only" — referenced inside cost guides and decision pages, never standalone reviews.

### Scope on launch

- Homepage + 5 trust pages (about, editorial standards, methodology, disclosure, contact)
- **15 launch pages** (not 10): 5 DIY-or-Hire, 5 Cost, 5 Consumer-Advocacy
- **1 tool** at launch: DIY-or-Hire (job-first, data-backed)
- **1 tool** in month 2–3: Cost estimator (10-job MVP)
- Email list active from day 1
- Credentialed reviewer arrangement for YMYL content **before** any YMYL page publishes (per-article basis, not retainer)

### What doesn't get built (yet)

- No appliance error-code database (phase 2, months 6–12)
- No tool-review section
- No generic tutorial library
- No YouTube yet (phase 3)
- No paid ads
- No home warranty affiliates, ever

## D. Launch Roadmap (First 90 Days)

### What to build FIRST (week 1–3)

1. **Homepage + 5 trust pages** (about, editorial standards, methodology, affiliate disclosure, contact). Ship with real content, not placeholders. The methodology page especially — it should read like journalism.
2. **Email capture** — one lead magnet: *"The Home Repair Cost Calendar: what to check (and what it costs) each month of 2026."* PDF.
3. **Draft first 15 pages** in Notion/Docs before publishing. Quality first, SEO second.
4. **Arrange per-article expert review** for the YMYL topics. Budget: $75–$150 total for the launch wave. LinkedIn outreach or r/askelectricians.

### What tool to launch FIRST (week 3–4)

**DIY-or-Hire Decision Tool — job-first, not quiz-first.**

Don't ask the user to self-assess safety and skill. Ask them which job they're considering. The tool maps that job to:

- Safety classification (electrical/gas = YMYL; plumbing = moderate; cosmetic = low)
- Permit required (by state, sourced from ICC/IRC references)
- Typical DIY time (from contractor-forum and homeowner-survey data)
- DIY cost estimate (parts + tools if not owned)
- Pro cost estimate (from cost-guide data)
- Verdict: DIY Recommended / Maybe DIY (with conditions) / Hire a Pro
- *One-line reasoning* ("hire — panel work requires permit in 47 states and mis-wiring causes house fires; typical pro cost is $1,500–$3,500.")

Launch with 10 jobs in the tool. Same 10 that map to the first 10 pages. Tool and articles cross-link.

### What pages to publish FIRST (week 4–6)

Publish 15 pages in two waves. Wave 1 = 8 pages in week 4–5 (non-YMYL). Wave 2 = 7 pages in week 5–6 (includes YMYL, after expert review).

### What to DELAY

- Cost estimator tool → month 2–3 (5 jobs at first, then scale)
- Second tool iteration → month 3
- Lead-gen partnerships (Modernize, Networx) → when traffic hits 5K monthly uniques, likely month 4–6
- Mediavine Journey → 1K sessions, likely month 3–4
- YouTube channel → month 6+
- Appliance error-code database → month 6–12
- Digital product → when email list crosses 3K subscribers
- Paid writers → when there's $2K+/mo recurring revenue

### What to AVOID in the first 90 days

1. Building more than 1 tool.
2. Writing more than 15–20 articles. Quality, not volume.
3. Publishing any YMYL page without expert review.
4. Using Amazon links in every article out of habit (kills newsletter CTR later).
5. Hiring a writer. Too early. Writers need a functioning system to plug into.
6. Redesigning the site. Ship. Iterate later.
7. Chasing Reddit marketing aggressively. Contribute genuinely; don't spam.
8. Reading another strategy memo. Execute.

## F. Final Answer: Lean or Broader?

**Lean. Not close.**

If this were my site, I'd launch the revised version — with two amendments:

1. **Raise launch page count to 15 and include consumer-advocacy content in the first wave.** The "here's why home warranties are a bad deal" post is the article that builds the brand in one piece. Ship it in week 6.
2. **Arrange per-article expert review before any YMYL page publishes.** Non-negotiable. Cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.

The original memo was an ideal-world plan. The revised plan is what a real solo founder actually executes. Ideal-world plans die in month 2 when the founder realizes they've committed to $2K/mo in expert retainers, three tool builds, and a content pipeline they can't feed.

The lean version wins because **it ships**. A shipped site at month 3, iterating weekly, will outperform a perfect-on-paper site that's still in build at month 6. Every time.

Ship the lean version. Add the expert-review line item. Keep the appliance database on a post-it for phase 2. Go.
