This is a room-by-room safety checklist covering the modifications that may reduce fall risk and make a home easier to navigate as parents age. Items are ordered from highest-impact, lowest-cost to larger investments. **Safety note: this is a general checklist; for personalized mobility, balance, or fall-risk concerns, talk to an occupational therapist or your parent&apos;s physician.**

## Quick-win checklist (under $300 total)

If you only do these eight things, you cover the highest-impact, lowest-cost safety modifications:

- [ ] Install grab bars in the shower (entry vertical + long-wall horizontal)
- [ ] Install a grab bar beside the toilet
- [ ] Add a non-slip bath mat or non-slip treads to the tub/shower floor
- [ ] Add motion-activated night lights to bathroom, hallway, and bedroom
- [ ] Replace dim bulbs with brighter LED equivalents (target 800–1,100 lumens for general lighting)
- [ ] Remove or secure trip-hazard rugs (especially throw rugs without rubber backing)
- [ ] Check that all smoke alarms and CO detectors are functional and less than 10 years old (see our [smoke detector replacement guide](/diy-or-hire/smoke-detector))
- [ ] Add a sturdy handrail to every staircase, both sides if missing

Total parts cost: $150–$300. Install time: a weekend.

## Bathroom

This is where the largest share of older-adult home falls happen. Prioritize this room first.

- [ ] **Grab bars:** vertical at shower entry, horizontal along shower wall, horizontal beside toilet. Anchored into studs or grab-bar-rated toggle anchors. See our [grab bar selection guide](/senior-home-safety/best-grab-bars-for-showers).
- [ ] **Non-slip surface:** rubber bath mat or applied non-slip treads on the tub/shower floor.
- [ ] **Adequate lighting:** brighter overhead, plus motion-activated night light.
- [ ] **Handheld shower head:** allows seated showering and easier rinsing.
- [ ] **Raised toilet seat or comfort-height toilet:** standard 14–15 inch toilet seats are too low for many older adults.
- [ ] **Lever-handle faucets:** easier to operate than knob handles for arthritic hands.
- [ ] **Thermostatic shower valve:** scald-prevention. Basic versions $50–$150 installed.
- [ ] **Shower seat or transfer bench:** if standing in the shower is becoming difficult.

Larger investment ($3,000–$18,000): converting a tub-shower to a curbless walk-in shower. See [bathroom safety for seniors](/senior-home-safety/bathroom-safety) for the full reasoning.

## Stairs

The second-highest fall-risk area in most homes.

- [ ] **Handrails on both sides** of every staircase.
- [ ] **Solid, ungapped treads.** Open-riser stairs (where you can see through to the next step) are harder to navigate with depth-perception changes.
- [ ] **Non-slip stair treads** on carpeted stairs that are slippery, or self-adhesive non-slip strips on wood stairs.
- [ ] **High-contrast trim** on the nose of each tread, especially the top and bottom step.
- [ ] **Switch-accessible lighting** at both top and bottom — never make the user navigate the stairs to reach a light switch.
- [ ] **Stair lift** for major mobility limitations (typical installed cost $3,500–$8,000 for a straight staircase; curved stairs run higher).

See [how to make stairs safer for seniors](/senior-home-safety/how-to-make-stairs-safer-for-seniors) for the full deep dive.

## Bedroom

- [ ] **Clear path from bed to bathroom**, with night-light coverage along the route.
- [ ] **Bed at appropriate height** — most older adults are safest at 20–23 inches from floor to top of mattress (sit-to-stand height). A bed too high or too low increases fall risk.
- [ ] **Bedside lamp with easy-to-reach switch**, or a smart-bulb that responds to voice or a touch lamp.
- [ ] **Phone within reach** of the bed.
- [ ] **Remove trip hazards** — clear floor of throw rugs, dog beds, electrical cords across walking paths.
- [ ] **Bed rail** for support sitting up and getting in/out of bed, if needed.
- [ ] **Closet rod at accessible height** (around 48 inches) if reaching overhead is becoming difficult.

## Kitchen

- [ ] **Frequently-used items at counter height** — coffee maker, plates, daily mugs. Avoid the &ldquo;step on a stool to reach the top shelf&rdquo; pattern.
- [ ] **Lever-handle faucet** for easier operation.
- [ ] **Adequate task lighting** under cabinets so the counter is bright when prepping food.
- [ ] **Non-slip floor mat** in front of the sink, with rubber backing.
- [ ] **Smoke alarm in or near the kitchen** (within 10 feet of the door, per code).
- [ ] **Fire extinguisher accessible** but not stored above the stove.
- [ ] **Stove with timer or automatic shutoff** for users who sometimes forget to turn off the burner.
- [ ] **Easy-to-read stove dials** (some older stoves have unclear markings — replacement knobs are cheap).

## Living areas

- [ ] **Clear walking paths** with no electrical cords or furniture obstacles.
- [ ] **Rugs secured** with rubber backing or removed entirely — throw rugs are a major fall trigger.
- [ ] **Furniture with sturdy arms** for sit-to-stand support, at appropriate seat height (16–19 inches for most adults).
- [ ] **Switch-accessible lamps** at every seating location.
- [ ] **Smoke alarm** in every living space and hallway.

## Entry / outdoor

- [ ] **Handrail on entry steps**, even just two or three steps.
- [ ] **Non-slip entry mat** with rubber backing.
- [ ] **Adequate exterior lighting** at the door, ideally motion-activated.
- [ ] **Threshold ramps** to eliminate doorway lips that become trip hazards.
- [ ] **Doorbell visible from inside** so the user can verify visitors without opening the door.

## Whole-house

- [ ] **Smoke alarms** in every required location, less than 10 years old, tested monthly.
- [ ] **Carbon monoxide detectors** in any home with gas appliances, oil, propane, fuel-burning fireplace, or attached garage.
- [ ] **Fire extinguisher** accessible in or near the kitchen.
- [ ] **Phone numbers visible** — primary care doctor, family contacts, poison control, local non-emergency police.
- [ ] **Emergency response system** (Life Alert / similar) considered if the user lives alone.
- [ ] **Water heater set to 120°F** — higher settings scald older skin faster.
- [ ] **Lever door handles** instead of knobs.
- [ ] **Programmable thermostat** to avoid frequent walks to a wall thermostat.

## When to bring in an occupational therapist

If your parent has specific mobility, balance, or cognitive concerns, a one-time home assessment by an occupational therapist gives much better-targeted recommendations than any generic checklist. OT home assessments are often covered by Medicare for qualifying patients — talk to the physician.

## When to hire a CAPS contractor

For larger modifications — bathroom remodels, doorway widening, stair lifts, full universal-design upgrades — a Certified Aging-in-Place Specialist (CAPS) contractor is worth the search. They&apos;ve completed specialty training on aging-in-place principles. Find one through the NAHB directory.

For small jobs (single grab bar, raised toilet seat, motion-activated night light), any reasonably skilled handyman works.

## Avoid this mistake

The most common pattern in failed home-safety projects: buying lots of products before installing any of them. A box of grab bars in the garage helps no one. Start with one room, finish it, then move to the next. Bathroom first.
