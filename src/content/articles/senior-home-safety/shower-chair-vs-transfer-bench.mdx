**A shower chair sits inside the shower; a transfer bench bridges the tub edge.** Pick a shower chair when the user can safely step in. Pick a transfer bench when stepping over the tub wall is a fall risk. For users on the borderline, the transfer bench is the safer choice — it solves the highest-risk part of tub bathing without requiring the user to commit to stepping over the wall on a given day. Either way, **always use one alongside grab bars** — the two work together, not as substitutes.

**Safety note:** This compares home equipment for general use. For personalized mobility, balance, or post-surgery needs, consult an occupational therapist for the right fit.

## What each one is

### Shower chair

A four-legged stool or chair, sized to fit inside a tub or shower stall. The user enters the shower normally (steps over the tub edge or shower curb), sits down on the chair, and bathes seated. Common features:

- 4 anti-slip rubber feet
- Plastic seat (often perforated for drainage)
- Optional: backrest, armrests, padded seat
- Height typically adjustable from 17–21 inches

**Footprint:** Sits entirely inside the bathing area.

### Transfer bench

Wider than a shower chair, designed to span the edge of a bathtub. **Two legs inside the tub, two legs outside.** The user sits down on the outside section (no leg-lifting required), slides sideways across the bench, and ends up seated inside the tub. Common features:

- 4 anti-slip rubber feet
- Wider seat (typically 28+ inches)
- Backrest and one or both armrests on the outside section
- Optional: sliding / rotating seat for easier transfer
- A removable bench section (some models)

**Footprint:** Spans the tub wall.

## Which one fits which user

| User situation | Recommended |
|---|---|
| Can step into shower / over tub edge with mild support | Shower chair |
| Walk-in shower (no curb) | Shower chair |
| Difficulty lifting one leg while balancing on the other | Transfer bench |
| History of falls during tub transfers | Transfer bench |
| Post-surgery hip/knee replacement | Transfer bench (often prescribed) |
| Stroke recovery affecting balance | Transfer bench |
| Wheelchair-bound for transfers | Transfer bench with sliding/rotating seat |
| Tub-shower combo, average mobility | Either; bench is safer |
| Shower stall only, average mobility | Shower chair |

When in doubt, go transfer bench. The cost premium is $30–$60 and the additional safety margin is meaningful.

Avoid the cheapest tier ($20–30 shower chairs, $40–60 transfer benches): leg rubber wears out fast, welds fail under repeated use, and seat plastic cracks. Mid-range from established brands (Drive Medical, Carex, NOVA, Vive, Medline) is the value tier.

## What to look for

**1. Weight capacity.**
Listed on every product. Standard is 250 lb; heavy-duty is 300–500 lb. Pick a unit rated comfortably above the user&apos;s weight (20% margin minimum).

**2. Anti-slip feet.**
Rubber, not plastic. Should grip wet ceramic, fiberglass, and tile. Look for &ldquo;suction&rdquo; feet only as a supplement — primary grip should be rubber friction.

**3. Adjustable height.**
17–21 inch range covers most users. Lower for shorter users; higher for taller users or those who struggle to stand from a low seat.

**4. Backrest.**
Strongly recommended for users with balance issues. A simple back keeps the user from tipping backward if they lose orientation.

**5. Armrests.**
Help with sit-to-stand. Optional for stronger users; essential for users who need to push up with their arms.

**6. Sliding or rotating seat (transfer bench only).**
A non-sliding transfer bench requires the user to slide across the bench themselves. A sliding seat moves with them, reducing the muscle effort required. Worth the premium for users with weakness or significant joint pain.

**7. Removable or foldable.**
For sharing the bathroom with other family members, a unit that can be removed or folded is more livable. Most quality transfer benches can be lifted out without tools.

## Common mistakes

- **Buying the cheapest model.** Rubber feet wear, plastic seats crack, welds fail. A $30 shower chair that fails after 6 months is more expensive than an $80 one that lasts 5 years.
- **Skipping grab bars.** A bench or chair doesn&apos;t solve the standing-balance problem when reaching for soap or shampoo. Pair with at least one grab bar in the shower. See [best grab bars for showers](/senior-home-safety/best-grab-bars-for-showers).
- **Putting the bench in the wrong direction.** The longer leg-extension should be on the side the user transfers from. Don&apos;t guess — set it up with the user present.
- **Trying to transfer over the bench instead of through it.** A transfer bench is designed to slide on. Stepping over it defeats the purpose and is a fall risk.
- **Using a shower chair on a tub edge.** Shower chairs aren&apos;t engineered to span the tub wall. Use a transfer bench for that geometry.

## Best first upgrade

For most older adults still using a tub-shower combo: **a quality transfer bench ($120-$200) plus one grab bar inside the shower ($30-$60) plus a non-slip bath mat ($15-$30) is the highest-leverage $200 you can spend in the bathroom.** Together they address the three biggest fall-risk factors: the transfer, the standing-while-wet motion, and the slippery floor.

## When to involve an occupational therapist

For users with specific medical situations — post-surgery, stroke recovery, significant balance impairment, multiple medications affecting balance — a one-time occupational therapist home assessment ($150-$400, sometimes Medicare-covered for qualifying patients) tells you which equipment fits the actual user, not just the typical user. OTs aren&apos;t selling anything; their recommendation is mobility-based.
