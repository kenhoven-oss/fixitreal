A real grab bar supports at least 250 pounds of load, anchors into studs or grab-bar-rated toggle anchors, resists corrosion in a wet environment, and has a textured (knurled) finish for grip when wet. These specs are well-met by mid-priced bars from Moen, Delta, Kohler, and a few specialty makers. The bar you choose matters less than the install — a $25 bar properly anchored beats a $90 bar mounted in plain drywall anchors.

## The specs that actually matter

When evaluating a grab bar, check these four things in this order:

1. **Load rating.** 250 pounds or more, ideally with a published spec or stamped on the bar. ADA-rated bars meet this by definition. Any bar without a published load rating is suspect.
2. **Diameter.** 1.25–1.5 inches is the usability sweet spot. Thinner bars (1 inch) are harder to grip in a real loss-of-balance event. Thicker bars (1.75+ inches) are uncomfortable for smaller hands.
3. **Wall clearance.** 1.5 inches between bar and wall. Closer is unsafe (wrist can&apos;t fit behind for a full grip); farther moves the leverage further from the wall and stresses the anchors.
4. **Texture.** Knurled, peened, or otherwise textured surface for grip when wet. Smooth finishes are slipperier under soapy hands.

Beyond those four: corrosion-resistant materials (304 stainless steel, brass, powder-coated steel) and a flange large enough to hide imperfect drill holes are useful but secondary.

## What we look for in a grab bar

We pick bars based on these criteria — not on Amazon star count or affiliate margin:

- Load rating clearly published (250+ pounds)
- ANSI A117.1 or ADA dimensional compliance, or close to it
- 1.25–1.5 inch diameter
- 1.5 inch wall clearance
- Textured finish on shower bars
- Concealed-screw flange design (more secure than exposed-screw)
- Sold at major retailers with reliable stock and warranty access

## Categories that work well

For most homeowners, the three categories below cover almost every install:

**Concealed-screw stainless steel bars (Moen, Delta, Kohler "Designer" lines).**
These hide the mounting screws behind a decorative flange. Slightly higher cost ($35–$70) but cleaner look and arguably more secure mount than exposed-screw bars. The mainstream big-brand versions are sold at all major home-improvement retailers.

**Standard exposed-screw ADA bars (Moen Home Care, Delta Decor Assist).**
The classic safety-bar look. Functional, well-rated, and cheap ($20–$45). Good for utility installs where appearance isn&apos;t the priority — laundry room, garage access, basement stairs.

**Decorative grab bars with integrated towel hooks (Moen Home Care SecureMount, Delta SureMount).**
Look like towel bars but rated as grab bars (load-tested 250+ pounds). Higher cost ($45–$120) but useful in primary bathrooms where guests might balk at obvious "safety" hardware. Just make sure the version you buy explicitly publishes the load rating — some decorative bars look similar but aren&apos;t rated.

## Categories to avoid

- **Suction-cup "grab bars" ($15–$30).** Marketed as portable grab bars; not actually rated for fall loads. The suction can release without warning when the wall is wet or soapy. These are useful as a balance hand-rail for a fully-balanced user — they are not a substitute for an anchored bar.
- **Bars without published load ratings.** If the listing doesn&apos;t state pound load, don&apos;t buy. Marketplace third-party sellers often list bars that look like name-brand units but lack the spec.
- **"Universal anchor" plastic kits.** Plastic drywall anchors aren&apos;t rated for grab-bar loads. Use steel grab-bar-rated toggle anchors (WingIts, Cobra) or screw into a stud.

## How to choose by wall type

**Drywall with studs.** Use a stud finder. Pick a bar length that spans two studs (16 inches apart in most homes; verify with the finder). Standard 1.25–1.5 inch wood screws into the stud are usually sufficient.

**Tile over drywall.** Same as above but drill through tile with a tile bit. Tape the tile, drill slowly, transition to wood-bit once through the tile.

**Tile over cement board.** The strongest install. Anchor into the cement board with proper masonry/concrete anchors.

**Fiberglass or acrylic tub surround.** Less ideal — the panel itself isn&apos;t structural. Use grab-bar-rated toggle anchors that grip the back of the panel, or install backer-board reinforcement behind it (best done during a remodel).

## Best first upgrade

For most homes: a 12-inch vertical bar at the shower entry, anchored into a stud, with a 24-inch horizontal bar along the long shower wall, also anchored into studs. Total parts cost $40–$120 for two solid bars. Install: 30–60 minutes DIY, or $100–$300 hired. This pair covers the two highest-risk transitions in the bathroom.

## Avoid this mistake

Don&apos;t buy decorative grab bars that don&apos;t publish a load rating. Specifically: anything marketed as a &ldquo;towel bar that doubles as a grab bar&rdquo; without a 250+ pound spec is a towel bar, period. Some makers (Moen Home Care SecureMount, Delta SureMount) make decorative bars that ARE rated; verify the spec before you buy.
