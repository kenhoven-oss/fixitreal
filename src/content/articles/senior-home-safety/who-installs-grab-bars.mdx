For a straightforward grab-bar install, a handyman is the right hire — $60–$200 per bar, no licensing concerns, and they can knock out a 3-bar bathroom package in an afternoon. For users with specific medical mobility needs, an occupational therapist evaluation first ($150–$400, often Medicare-covered) gets the bar placement right, then a handyman or CAPS-certified contractor installs to the OT&apos;s spec. For full bathroom remodels with aging-in-place focus, a CAPS contractor manages the whole project at premium rates.

## Who installs grab bars — full options

### Handyman ($60–$200 per bar)

The right hire for most homeowners. Grab bars don&apos;t require licensed-trade work in most jurisdictions; any competent handyman can install. Key skills:

- Finding wall studs reliably (stud finder, sometimes confirmed by drilling a small test hole)
- Using grab-bar-rated anchors when studs don&apos;t cooperate (WingIts, Cobra, or equivalent toggle anchors)
- Verifying the installed bar holds 250+ pounds before declaring done

Total typical cost: $60–$200 per bar including parts markup and labor. For a 3-bar bathroom package: $240–$600.

**When to pick this option:**
- You&apos;ve already picked the bars yourself
- You know roughly where you want them
- Your bathroom is standard residential layout (no unusual structural complications)
- You&apos;re budget-conscious

### Plumber ($120–$280 per bar)

Plumbers will install grab bars but it&apos;s usually overkill — and overpriced. Their hourly rate is higher than a handyman&apos;s, their trip fee is higher, and the work isn&apos;t plumbing.

**When a plumber makes sense:**
- You&apos;re already having a plumber out for another job and want to bundle
- Your install requires removing or working around plumbing fixtures
- Your bathroom has unusual layout that requires re-routing supply lines

For pure grab-bar work, hire a handyman instead.

### Occupational therapist consultation ($150–$400, often Medicare-covered)

OTs don&apos;t install — they assess. For users with specific medical mobility limitations (post-stroke, post-hip-replacement, significant arthritis, balance disorders, dementia), the OT&apos;s recommended bar locations + heights are personalized to the actual user, not just &ldquo;typical&rdquo;.

Workflow:
1. OT visits the home, observes the user in the bathroom environment, documents recommendations
2. Homeowner buys the recommended bars
3. Handyman or CAPS contractor installs to spec

Medicare Part B covers OT home assessments for qualifying patients with a doctor&apos;s order. Even out of pocket, $150–$400 is small money relative to a fall-prevention outcome that actually works for the user&apos;s specific situation.

### Certified Aging-in-Place Specialist (CAPS) contractor ($130–$280 per bar)

CAPS is a National Association of Home Builders credential. CAPS contractors have completed training in universal-design principles, common aging-in-place modifications, and the broader process of adapting homes for seniors.

For single-bar installs, a CAPS contractor is usually overkill — their rates run higher than a generalist handyman for the same task. Where CAPS earns its premium:

- **Full bathroom remodels** for aging-in-place (curbless walk-in shower conversion, doorway widening, fixture replacements)
- **Multi-room modification projects** (bathroom + kitchen + stair safety)
- **Coordinated work** that needs an aging-in-place lens across multiple trades

For a single grab-bar install, save the premium and hire a handyman. For a $15,000+ bathroom remodel aimed at the user&apos;s long-term needs, hire CAPS.

### DIY ($20–$60 per bar)

Most physically-able homeowners can install grab bars themselves with:
- A grab bar ($20–$60)
- A drill
- A stud finder
- Wall anchors rated for grab-bar loads (only if you can&apos;t hit studs)
- 30–60 minutes of patience per bar

The skill ceiling is real but reasonable. The critical thing is: anchor into studs whenever possible, use grab-bar-rated anchors (never standard drywall anchors) when studs don&apos;t cooperate, and load-test the installed bar with body weight before relying on it.

See our [grab bar installation cost guide](/senior-home-safety/grab-bar-installation-cost) for the full DIY procedure.

### Medicare or VA benefit programs

For qualifying users, some installation costs may be covered:

- **Medicare:** Original Medicare typically doesn&apos;t cover home modifications or grab-bar installation. Medicare Advantage plans sometimes include limited home-modification benefits.
- **Medicaid:** State Medicaid waivers sometimes cover home modifications for qualifying participants — varies by state.
- **VA HISA grant:** Veterans can apply for the Home Improvements and Structural Alterations grant for medically-necessary modifications.
- **Local Area Agency on Aging:** Often runs grant programs for low-income seniors needing home safety modifications.

Eligibility varies by program. Start with your local Area Agency on Aging (call 1-800-677-1116) — they know what&apos;s available in your area.

## What to ask before hiring

For any installer:

1. **&ldquo;How do you locate studs and anchor when you can&apos;t hit one?&rdquo;** The answer should mention stud finder, sometimes test-drilling, and grab-bar-rated toggle anchors (WingIts, Cobra). Anyone who says &ldquo;just drywall anchors&rdquo; — find someone else.
2. **&ldquo;Will you load-test the bar after install?&rdquo;** A 5-second pull with body weight verifies the install. Reputable installers do this without being asked.
3. **&ldquo;Are you insured?&rdquo;** Especially for home modifications, liability insurance matters. Ask to see proof.
4. **&ldquo;Do you supply or do I supply the bars?&rdquo;** Either is fine, but settle it in writing. Markup on bars can be 30–50%.
5. **&ldquo;What&apos;s the total quote for [X] bars including parts and labor?&rdquo;** Get it in writing before the visit.

## When NOT to hire just anyone

Some situations warrant the OT-then-CAPS workflow even at higher cost:

- User has had a fall in the past 12 months
- User has been hospitalized recently and is returning home with new mobility limitations
- User has progressive mobility decline (Parkinson&apos;s, MS, etc.)
- Bathroom is small and requires careful placement to avoid creating new hazards
- Standard placements don&apos;t fit the user (very tall, very short, wheelchair user)

Cheapest install isn&apos;t always best when the user&apos;s needs are specific.

## What I&apos;d do

For most users — basic aging-in-place safety upgrade, no specific medical complications:
1. Pick the bars yourself (see [best grab bars for showers](/senior-home-safety/best-grab-bars-for-showers))
2. Hire a local handyman with good reviews
3. Get a written quote for installing 3 bars in one visit ($240–$500 typical)
4. Verify install with a body-weight pull test before leaving

For users with specific mobility limitations:
1. Get a physician referral for an OT home evaluation (Medicare-eligible for qualifying patients)
2. Buy the bars per the OT&apos;s spec
3. Hire a CAPS contractor or a recommended handyman for install

The OT step is the highest-value single addition to a senior safety project — $150–$400 of personalized expertise that no generic install can match.
