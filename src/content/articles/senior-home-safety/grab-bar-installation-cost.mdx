A single grab bar professionally installed in a bathroom typically runs $60–$200, depending on parts and whether the install lands cleanly on a wall stud. DIY install with the right anchors and a drill runs $20–$60 in parts. For a full bathroom safety package (3 bars — shower entry, shower wall, beside toilet), expect $180–$600 installed, or $60–$180 DIY.

## What you&apos;re paying for

Three cost components:

1. **The bar itself.** $20–$60 for a standard 12–24 inch grab bar from a major brand (Moen, Delta, Kohler). Decorative or designer finishes (oil-rubbed bronze, brushed gold, integrated towel-bar combos) run $50–$120.
2. **Anchor hardware.** $5–$20 if you need toggle anchors when the install doesn&apos;t land on a stud. Standard wood screws into a stud are typically included with the bar.
3. **Labor.** $40–$140 per bar for a handyman, depending on local rates and whether tile drilling is involved.

## What changes the price

**Stud location.** A bar landing cleanly on two studs is the cheapest install — wood screws, 15 minutes. A bar landing in drywall between studs needs grab-bar-rated toggle anchors, which adds 10–15 minutes per anchor point and $5–$10 in hardware.

**Tile.** Drilling through tile requires the right bit (diamond or carbide tile bit), painters tape on the spot to prevent the bit from wandering, and patience. Average handyman charges $20–$60 more per bar for a tile install vs. drywall.

**Bar finish.** Plain stainless or chrome is the cheapest. Oil-rubbed bronze, brushed gold, white powder-coat run $20–$60 more.

**Length.** 12-inch and 18-inch bars are stocked everywhere. 24-inch and 36-inch are usually special-order, adding $10–$30.

**Tile age and condition.** Older tile (1960s–1970s) is more likely to crack on drill. A pro might quote slightly higher to account for the small chance of needing tile repair.

## When DIY makes sense

- You&apos;re comfortable with a drill, a stud finder, and a level.
- The install is into drywall or new (2000s+) ceramic tile.
- You can locate studs reliably.
- You&apos;re willing to use toggle anchors rated for grab-bar loads ("WingIts" or equivalent) when studs don&apos;t cooperate.

The skill ceiling is real but reasonable. Reading the bar&apos;s install instructions, watching one good YouTube install video, and taking your time gets you to a safe install for most homes.

## When to hire

- You&apos;re uncomfortable drilling tile, especially older tile that&apos;s more brittle.
- You can&apos;t locate studs reliably with a basic stud finder.
- The bar location requires routing around plumbing in the wall.
- You want a CAPS-certified contractor for a larger aging-in-place project.

## Avoid this mistake

**Don&apos;t use suction-cup "grab bars."** These are hand-rail aids for fully-balanced users — they fail under real load. They&apos;re a common cause of injury when a user grabs one during an actual loss-of-balance event.

**Don&apos;t use standard drywall anchors.** Plastic drywall anchors and butterfly anchors aren&apos;t rated for grab-bar loads. Use grab-bar-rated toggle anchors (WingIts, Cobra, or equivalent) or screw into studs.

**Don&apos;t skip the load test.** After install, pull on the bar with full body weight while standing safely. If anything moves or creaks, re-anchor before using.

## What to ask for in writing

If hiring out, the quote should specify:

- Bar brand and model
- Length and finish
- Mounting location (in inches from floor and from wall corner)
- Anchor type (into stud or with rated toggle anchors)
- That the installed bar will support at least 250 pounds of load
