For most aging-in-place situations, a **curbless walk-in shower is safer and cheaper than a walk-in tub**. Walk-in tubs sound appealing in the ads but introduce real problems: sitting inside while the tub fills (10-15 minutes of cold), door seals that can fail, exit-while-wet difficulty if balance is lost. Curbless walk-in showers with a fold-down seat, handheld sprayer, and grab bars deliver the same therapeutic benefit (warm bathing) with fewer risks. The remodel costs are typically similar or favor the shower.

**Safety note:** This is a home-modification comparison, not medical advice. For users with specific therapeutic bathing needs (joint conditions, hydrotherapy prescriptions), consult an occupational therapist or physical therapist before deciding.

## What each one is

**Walk-in tub.**
A bathtub with a hinged side door, raised seat, and handheld sprayer. User opens the door, steps in (still over a low threshold), closes the door, sits on the seat, fills the tub around themselves, bathes, drains, and exits.

**Curbless walk-in shower.**
A shower with no curb or threshold — the floor of the shower slopes gently to a drain at the same level as the bathroom floor. User walks (or rolls) in, optionally sits on a fold-down seat or transfer bench, uses a handheld sprayer or fixed showerhead, exits.

## The cost comparison

| Option | Typical cost installed |
| --- | --- |
| Walk-in tub (basic, no jets) | $4,000–$8,000 |
| Walk-in tub (with hydrotherapy jets) | $8,000–$15,000 |
| Tub-to-shower conversion (insert, no remodel) | $3,000–$6,000 |
| Curbless walk-in shower (full remodel, mid-range) | $7,000–$14,000 |
| Curbless walk-in shower (full remodel, high-end) | $14,000–$25,000 |
| Tub-step + transfer bench (interim solution) | $150–$400 (DIY) |

For comparable build quality and features, walk-in showers usually win on cost or break even — and they avoid the resale-value problem walk-in tubs create.

## Where the walk-in tub falls short

**1. Filling time = cold time.**
You sit inside the tub while it fills. Even with a fast-fill faucet (15-20 GPM), filling a tub takes 8-15 minutes. During that time you're wet, seated, and getting cold. Some models include in-line water heaters or pre-heat features ($500-$1,500 add-on); none fully solve the problem.

**2. Drain time = trapped time.**
Same issue in reverse. You sit in the tub while it drains. Drain time runs 5-10 minutes depending on the tub and drain configuration. During that time you cannot exit — the door is sealed shut by water pressure.

**3. Door seal failures.**
The hinged door has a rubber seal that's subject to repeated wet-dry cycles. Seal failures are the most common service issue with walk-in tubs and lead to water leaking onto the bathroom floor. Some seal repairs require returning the tub to the manufacturer — meaning weeks without bathing.

**4. Mid-bath emergency = trapped user.**
If something goes wrong mid-bath (medical event, dizziness, loss of consciousness), the user can't open the door — the water pressure holds it sealed shut until the tub drains. Some models have emergency-release mechanisms; some don't. Always verify before buying.

**5. Resale value.**
Walk-in tubs are widely viewed by buyers as a negative feature, signaling either aging-related accommodation or unusual buyer preferences. A bathroom with a walk-in tub typically requires removal and standard-tub install before sale, costing $2,000-$5,000 to undo. Curbless walk-in showers, in contrast, are increasingly seen as a positive feature by all age groups (universal design appeal).

## Where the walk-in shower wins

**1. No filling / no draining wait.**
Hot water on demand. Start washing immediately, leave when done.

**2. Easier exit.**
A fold-down seat lets you sit for stability; a curbless floor means no step required to exit. Even with mobility limitations, exiting a curbless shower is more controlled than a walk-in tub.

**3. Wheelchair accessible (with planning).**
Curbless showers can be designed for direct wheelchair roll-in. Walk-in tubs cannot accommodate users who can't transfer to the seat.

**4. Resale-neutral or resale-positive.**
Newer buyers actively look for curbless showers. Universal design is a growing real-estate selling point.

**5. Lower long-term maintenance.**
No door seal to fail. No tub-fill mechanism to break. Standard shower fixtures that any plumber services.

## When a walk-in tub IS the right call

Narrow use cases where a walk-in tub may make sense:

- **Therapeutic hydrotherapy** prescribed for arthritis, fibromyalgia, or post-injury rehabilitation, where full-body submersion in warm water matters.
- **A user who specifically wants to soak** and isn't satisfied with a shower-only option.
- **Space constraints** that don't accommodate a curbless shower remodel — usually due to floor-joist routing or limited drainage options.
- **Renters or short-term occupants** where a remodel isn't feasible.

If any of those apply, evaluate walk-in tub models carefully: verify emergency-release door function, verify door-seal warranty terms (real coverage, not "lifetime" marketing claims that exclude seals), and get at least three quotes from non-affiliated installers.

## Best first upgrade if you can't remodel

For users who need safety improvement now but can't do a full remodel:

1. **Add grab bars** at shower entry and along the tub wall (see [best grab bars for showers](/senior-home-safety/best-grab-bars-for-showers)).
2. **Add a transfer bench** that spans the tub edge — user sits, slides over the tub wall, slides into the tub.
3. **Add a handheld sprayer.**
4. **Add a non-slip bath mat or applied non-slip treads.**

Total cost: $200-$500. Time: an afternoon. Addresses 70-80% of the safety gap a full remodel solves, for 5% of the cost.

## Avoid this mistake

**Don't buy a walk-in tub from a high-pressure sales visit.** The pattern: salesperson visits the home, quotes $12,000-$18,000, offers a "today only" discount to $8,000, pressures signature. Real contractors don't use this script. Get at least three quotes, take 48 hours minimum to decide, and verify all advertised features (Medicare coverage claims, lifetime warranties, FDA approval) in writing before signing.

For more on this pattern, see [contractor red flags](/contractor-red-flags).

## When to consult an occupational therapist

For users with specific mobility, balance, or therapeutic needs, a one-time OT home assessment ($150-$400, sometimes covered by Medicare for qualifying patients) tells you which modification fits the actual user — not the salesperson's commission target. OTs aren't selling anything; their recommendation is based on the user's ability and home layout. This is the single most valuable consultation for a major bathroom remodel.
