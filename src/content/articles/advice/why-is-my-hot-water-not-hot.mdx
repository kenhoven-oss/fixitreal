<QuickAnswer>
**6 causes of no/lukewarm hot water, in diagnostic order:**

1. **Thermostat set too low** — adjust to 120°F (free fix).
2. **Tripped breaker / out pilot light** — reset breaker or relight pilot (free).
3. **Failed heating element** (electric) — $25 part + $100–$200 labor.
4. **Failed thermocouple** (gas) — $25 part + $100–$200 labor.
5. **Broken dip tube** — $20 part + $100–$200 labor, often a "fast-recovery" symptom.
6. **Sediment buildup** — flush the tank (free) or full replacement if old.

**Quickest diagnostic:** electric units have a red RESET button on the upper thermostat. Push it. If it restores hot water, the element or thermostat is degrading — schedule service. If RESET trips again within hours, you need a service call today.
</QuickAnswer>

<SafetyWarning>
**Gas water heaters produce carbon monoxide** during combustion. If you smell gas, hear a sustained hiss, or see soot near the burner area, **leave the house and call the gas utility** — not a plumber. Test CO detectors twice a year on every floor.
</SafetyWarning>

## Step 1: Check the easy stuff (5 minutes)

### Thermostat setting

- **Gas tank:** the dial is on the front of the gas control valve. Should read "A" or 120°F. Some units have a numeric dial 1–10; mid-range (5–7) is roughly 120°F.
- **Electric tank:** behind access panels on the side. Two thermostats (upper and lower) — both should be set to 120°F. Set with a flat screwdriver, slot in the thermostat dial.
- **Tankless:** digital display on the front, set via buttons. Default 120°F.

**Always shut power off before opening any access panel.**

### Breaker / pilot

- **Electric:** check the breaker panel. Water heater is typically a dedicated 240V double-pole breaker (often labeled "WH" or "Water Heater"). If tripped, reset once.
- **Gas:** look at the burner area. The pilot or igniter should be lit / sparking. If pilot out, follow the lighting instructions on the unit label.

## Step 2: Hit the RESET button (electric units)

Electric water heaters have a red RESET button on the upper thermostat (behind the upper access panel on the side of the tank).

- Shut off power at the breaker (safety first).
- Pop the upper access panel.
- Push the red button — should click in.
- Restore power.
- Wait 30–45 minutes for recovery.

If the reset resolves it: the upper element or thermostat is degrading. Plan for service.

If the reset trips again within hours: the lower element has likely shorted to the tank, drawing excessive current. Call a plumber/electrician.

## Step 3: Diagnose by symptom

### Symptom A: No hot water at all (cold only)

**Gas:**
- Pilot out → relight per label instructions.
- Pilot won't stay lit → failed thermocouple ($25 part + $150–$250 labor).
- Gas valve clicks but no flame → failed gas valve ($150–$300 part + labor).

**Electric:**
- Breaker tripped → reset once.
- RESET button trips → upper or lower element shorted.
- Both elements failed simultaneously → unlikely; check for thermostat failure.

### Symptom B: Hot water runs out fast (within 5–10 minutes)

For a 40–50 gallon tank, hot water should last 30–45 minutes of continuous use.

If less:
- **Broken dip tube** — cold water mixes with hot at the top instead of bottom. $20 part, 1-hour repair.
- **Lower heating element failed (electric)** — only the top element heats, capacity halved. $50 part + $150 labor.
- **Heavy sediment** — usable tank volume reduced 20–40%. Flush the tank.

### Symptom C: Lukewarm water (warm but not hot)

- **Thermostat set too low** — adjust.
- **Burner partially clogged with sediment (gas)** — flush; persistent → service call.
- **One element failed (electric)** — replace failed element.
- **Mixed line problem** — a faulty mixing valve at a fixture is sending cold water into the hot line. Check fixtures individually to isolate.

### Symptom D: Hot water then sudden cold

Same as Symptom B (runs out fast) — usually a dip tube or lower element issue.

### Symptom E: Pilot won't stay lit (gas)

- Most common cause: thermocouple failure. $25 part + $150 labor.
- Less common: gas valve failure, blocked flue (CO hazard — call utility immediately if suspected).
- Newer high-efficiency condensing units have electronic ignition + flame sensors. Replace flame sensor or call service.

## Step 4: Flush the tank (annual maintenance)

If your unit is over 3 years old and you've never flushed it, sediment buildup may be the primary cause of poor performance.

1. Turn off the water heater (gas → off; electric → breaker off).
2. Connect a hose to the drain valve at the bottom of the tank.
3. Run the hose to a floor drain, outside, or large bucket.
4. Open the drain valve and the T&P relief valve at the top.
5. Let water flow until it runs clear (5–20 minutes depending on sediment).
6. Close drain, close T&P, refill, restore power.
7. Test in 30–60 minutes.

This single maintenance task can restore lost hot-water capacity dramatically. Do it annually.

## When to call a plumber

- Pilot relight, breaker reset, thermostat adjustment all fail.
- You smell gas — call the utility first, not a plumber.
- Water leaking from the tank — see [water heater leaking](/advice/water-heater-leaking).
- Anode rod hasn't been checked in 5+ years.
- Unit is 10+ years old and has multiple symptoms.

Expect a typical service call in the $150–$300 range for trip + diagnostic. Element replacement: $200–$400 all-in. Thermocouple: $150–$250.

## When to replace instead of repair

For a tank under 8 years old, repair most things.

For a tank 10+ years old with any major repair quote over $400: get a [replacement quote](/costs/water-heater-replacement) for comparison. New units have federal tax credits (especially heat pump water heaters — up to $2,000 through 2032) that often make replacement the smart move.
