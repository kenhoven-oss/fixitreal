A hidden water leak rarely announces itself. By the time you see the stain or hear the drip, the water bill has been climbing for months and the drywall behind the cabinet is already soft. The good news: most household leaks can be found with tools you already own, a quiet house, and ninety minutes. Here is the order I'd work through, from the cheapest checks to the calls worth making.

<QuickAnswer>
The fastest way to confirm a hidden water leak is the **water-meter leak test**: shut off every fixture and appliance, write down the meter reading, wait two hours without using water, and check the meter again. Any movement = a leak on your side of the meter. From there, common culprits in order of frequency are **toilet flapper valves, dripping faucets, supply-line connections under sinks, water heaters, irrigation systems, and slab or wall pipes**. Most homes can be cleared in under two hours of homeowner work. Professional leak detection runs roughly **$200–$600** for diagnostics and is worth it once you know there's a leak but can't see it.
</QuickAnswer>

<SafetyWarning>
**Stop and shut off the water main if any of these are true:**
water is actively flowing or pooling, drywall is sagging or bulging, water is near outlets or light fixtures, you smell anything burning, or you see a hot-water line dripping near electrical. Active water plus live electrical is one of the most dangerous combinations in home emergencies — call your utility or the fire department before touching anything if it's near the panel. For the first ten minutes of a real incident, follow the [pipe-burst playbook](/emergency-repairs/pipe-burst-first-10-minutes) or [water-leaking-from-ceiling guide](/emergency-repairs/water-leaking-from-ceiling).
</SafetyWarning>

## Signs of a hidden water leak

Most hidden leaks announce themselves indirectly long before they're visible. The patterns to watch for:

- **A water bill that climbs without any change in use.** A 20–40% jump month-over-month with no new occupants or new fixtures is the single clearest signal. EPA estimates the average household wastes roughly 10,000 gallons a year to fixable leaks — a slow leak shows up on the bill before it shows up on the floor.
- **The meter dial creeps when nothing is running.** Pop the meter cover on a quiet evening and watch for 30 seconds. Any movement of the small "flow indicator" (often a triangle or star) with every fixture off means water is moving somewhere it shouldn't.
- **A faint sound of running water in quiet rooms.** Press an ear to walls, baseboards, and floors with the house quiet. A hiss, tick, or whisper near a wall plate or under a cabinet often gives away a slow line leak.
- **Warm or damp spots on floors.** A warm patch on tile, hardwood, or carpet — especially in a hallway near a bathroom — suggests a hot-water slab or wall-line leak.
- **Musty smell with no obvious source.** Drywall and framing that has been wet for a few days starts to smell before it shows visible damage.
- **Bubbling paint, peeling wallpaper, or warped baseboards.** Any of these on an exterior or wet-wall plumbing wall is suspect.
- **Mold or stains that come back after you paint.** A stain that bleeds through fresh paint is telling you the source is still active.
- **Toilets that refill on their own** — the "phantom flush." That's usually a worn flapper letting tank water seep into the bowl.
- **Unexplained mildew on the ceiling below a bathroom.** A slow supply or drain leak above can produce a stain so faint you'd miss it without looking for it.

One of these alone could be coincidence. Two or more on the same wall, room, or floor is enough to start investigating.

## How to perform a water-meter leak test

This is the single best 15-minute test in plumbing. It works because your meter records every gallon of water entering the house, whether you used it or not.

<KeyTakeaway title="Water-meter leak test in 5 steps">
1. **Stop all water use.** Turn off every faucet, the dishwasher, the washing machine, ice makers, irrigation, and any humidifier. Anyone home should not flush.
2. **Locate the meter.** Most U.S. homes have the meter at the curb in a concrete or plastic box, or in the basement near the front foundation. Pop the lid.
3. **Note the reading.** Write down the full number, or photograph the dial. Note the position of any small leak-indicator triangle or star.
4. **Wait at least two hours** with no water use. Overnight (8 hours) is even better and catches slower leaks.
5. **Check the meter again.** Any movement of the main reading or the leak indicator means water passed through with nothing in use. You have a leak.
</KeyTakeaway>

If the meter moved, the leak is somewhere between the meter and the inside of the house. To narrow it further: shut the **main shutoff valve at the house** (not at the meter). Run the test again. If the meter still moves, the leak is in the line between meter and house — usually a buried service-line leak, which is plumber work. If the meter is now still, the leak is inside the house, and you'll work through the fixture checks below.

## How to check the obvious sources first

Before you start opening walls, work through the cheap fixes. The order matters — toilets and outdoor lines waste the most water for the least visibility.

### Toilets

Toilets are the #1 source of "phantom" household water loss. A worn flapper can leak 200+ gallons a day silently.

**The dye test:**
1. Remove the tank lid and put a few drops of food coloring or a [toilet leak dye tablet](https://amzn.to/) into the tank water.
2. Don't flush. Wait 15 minutes.
3. Look at the bowl. Any color in the bowl means the flapper is leaking. A new flapper is $5–$15 and takes ten minutes.

Also check: water dripping from the fill valve, water at the floor base, or a tank that "phantom refills" every few minutes. If the base is wet, see our guide to a [toilet leaking at the base](/advice/toilet-leaking-at-the-base) — wax-ring failure is common and DIY-friendly.

### Faucets and showerheads

Every faucet, run hot and cold, then watch for 30 seconds after shutting off. A slow drip looks small but adds up — EPA estimates a faucet leaking one drip per second wastes more than 3,000 gallons a year. If a [faucet is dripping](/advice/faucet-dripping), the fix is usually a $5 cartridge or washer.

### Under-sink supply lines and shutoffs

Open every base cabinet under a sink. Run your finger along the supply lines and around each shutoff valve. Catch wet, mineral crust, or corrosion. Common culprits: braided supply lines past their service life (replace every 5–8 years), compression fittings under shutoff valves, and slow seeps at the trap or the disposal connection. If [water shows up under the bathroom sink](/advice/water-under-the-bathroom-sink) or the [kitchen sink drain is leaking](/advice/sink-drain-leaking-under-kitchen-sink), the source is usually visible once you clear the cabinet.

### Water heater

Look at the floor under the tank and inside the drain pan. Look at the cold-supply connection, the hot-out connection, the T&P discharge tube, and the tank base seam. A drip from the base seam is end-of-life — the tank is corroding through. See our guide on what to do when a [water heater is leaking](/advice/water-heater-leaking) and the realistic [water heater replacement cost](/costs/water-heater-replacement) so you know what's fair when the quote comes in.

### Appliance hoses

Pull the washing machine forward enough to see the hoses. Look at the dishwasher supply line and drain hose where they connect under the sink. Check the refrigerator ice-maker line where it tees off the cold-water supply. Rubber hoses harden and split with age — braided stainless replacements are ~$15 and worth the swap on washers and dishwashers before you have to call the plumber.

### Outdoor faucets and irrigation

Hose bibs that drip even when "off" usually have a worn washer. Wet ground around a sprinkler head when the system isn't running is a stuck valve or a broken line. A surprisingly green patch in an otherwise dry lawn is often an underground irrigation leak.

### Ceilings, floors, and walls

If the meter test is still showing flow with the obvious sources clear, walk the house slowly:

- Press a dry paper towel against any suspect ceiling stain — if it comes away damp, the leak is active. Our guide to [ceiling water stains](/advice/ceiling-water-stain) covers how to read the pattern.
- Bare-foot walk every floor. Note any warm spots (hot-water slab leak), cold spots (cold-line leak), or [soft spots in laminate or wood floor](/advice/soft-spot-in-laminate-or-wood-floor).
- Look at the bottom 6 inches of drywall on every wet-wall. Bubbling paint, soft drywall, or a darker baseboard says water is escaping behind it.

## How to find a leak behind a wall

This is the hardest one for homeowners. The leak isn't visible, and exploratory drywall cutting is the kind of work people regret.

**Step 1 — Confirm the wall is wet, don't guess.** A pinless moisture meter (no holes needed) on drywall reads ~12% in a dry room and 17%+ when there's water behind. A handheld meter is around $30 — see our [moisture meter buying guide](/tools/best-moisture-meters-for-homeowners). Move it across the wall and floor in a grid. Map the highest readings.

**Step 2 — Narrow with thermal imaging.** A phone-attachable thermal camera (~$200) shows cold or warm spots through drywall on hot- and cold-water lines. Not magic, but it dramatically narrows where to cut.

**Step 3 — Cut only where the readings are highest.** A 4-inch hole in the right place beats a 12-inch hole in the wrong place. Cut at the bottom of the suspect area first — water flows down, leaks usually appear higher than the wet spot.

**Step 4 — If readings are widespread or you can't localize, hire it out.** A plumber with acoustic leak-detection gear can pinpoint a wall leak in under an hour. That's $200–$400 well spent versus a half-day of homeowner demolition.

Once you've found and fixed the leak, the wall isn't done. Read [drywall damage after a leak](/advice/drywall-damage-after-a-leak) before you patch — the worst mistake is closing the wall too soon and trapping moisture, which becomes mold.

## How to find a slab leak

A "slab leak" is a leak in the pressurized water lines that run through or under the concrete slab foundation. Classic signs:

- **A warm spot on the floor** in tile, vinyl, or hardwood — usually a hot-water line.
- **Damp carpet** with no obvious source, near a plumbing wall.
- **The sound of running water under the floor** when everything is off.
- **A cracking foundation or unexplained settling** where pressurized water has been undermining the slab for weeks.

A confirmed slab leak isn't a DIY job. Repair options are pipe rerouting (running new line through the attic to bypass the slab), spot repair (jackhammer through the slab to the line), or full repipe. Pricing varies hugely — get two written quotes before agreeing to any approach, especially the "rip up the floor" option. A non-invasive electronic detection visit ($300–$600) is almost always worth paying for before authorizing any repair.

## How to find an underground water leak

If the meter is moving with the house main valve shut, the leak is between the meter and the house — the service line. This is the homeowner's responsibility on most utilities (the line from the meter to the house, not from the street to the meter).

Signs:

- A wet, soft, or unusually green strip of ground between the meter and the house.
- Reduced water pressure inside.
- Sinking pavers, asphalt, or settled walkway over the service-line path.
- Water bill spikes with no indoor change.

This is plumber-with-equipment work. Acoustic correlators and ground microphones can pinpoint a buried leak within a few feet without digging the entire yard.

## How water leak detection tools actually work

There are two completely different product categories. They don't replace each other.

**Sensors (leak detectors).** Battery or Wi-Fi pucks, cable sensors, and whole-home shutoff valves. These detect water *after* it has escaped a fixture — they sit on the floor under the water heater, behind the washer, under sinks. The cheap puck sirens when water touches it; the Wi-Fi sensor pushes a phone alert; the whole-home valve cuts off the entire house automatically. **They don't locate hidden leaks** — they catch escapes the moment they start. Our [best water leak detectors](/tools/best-water-leak-detectors) buying guide breaks down which type fits which home.

**Locators (detection tools).** Used to find a leak that's already happening somewhere hidden:

- **Moisture meters** (pin and pinless) read the moisture content of drywall, wood, and flooring. The cheapest accurate way to confirm a wet wall without cutting it.
- **Acoustic listening devices** amplify the high-frequency sound of pressurized water escaping a pipe. Used by plumbers to localize wall and slab leaks; consumer versions exist but the skill curve is steep.
- **Infrared / thermal cameras** show temperature differentials through walls. A cold streak running down a wall reveals a cold-line leak; a warm patch on a slab reveals a hot-line leak.
- **Tracer-gas equipment.** Pro-level. Pressurizes a depressurized line with helium or hydrogen mix; a surface sensor finds where the gas escapes. Used on hard-to-find service-line leaks.

If you're a homeowner doing your own diagnostics, a moisture meter is the single highest-value tool. If you're calling a pro, ask which tools they bring. A leak-detection company with thermal + acoustic + tracer gas is a different category from a plumber who just opens walls until he finds it.

## DIY vs. professional leak detection

<PullQuote>The right question is not "can I do this myself," it's "do I know enough about where the leak is to point a tool at it?"</PullQuote>

**DIY makes sense when:** you've passed the meter test, you can hear or see the source, the suspected location is a fixture, supply line, water heater, toilet, or visible appliance hose. Most household leaks fall here.

**Hire a leak-detection specialist when:** the meter test confirmed a leak, you can't localize it after working through the fixture checks, the suspected source is inside a wall, under a ceiling, in the slab, or buried in the yard. You're paying for the tools and the experience, not just the labor. Expect $200–$600 for diagnostics, often credited toward repair if you hire the same company.

## What to do immediately after finding a leak

The first hour after confirming a leak matters more than the next week.

1. **Shut off water at the closest valve.** Local fixture shutoff if you can — fixtures isolate cleanly. If not, the main shutoff. Know where it is *before* you need it.
2. **Move anything wet or near-wet.** Rugs, cardboard, electronics, papers, kids' books. Anything cardboard wicks water fast.
3. **Dry what you can.** Towels, then a [shop vac](/tools/best-shop-vacs-for-water-cleanup) for standing water, then fans pointed at the wet area. A rental dehumidifier ($30–$50/day at any hardware store) is the highest-impact tool against mold for a wet wall or floor.
4. **Document with photos and video** before any cleanup. Wide shots and close-ups. Note the time. If you end up filing a claim, this is the difference between an easy claim and a fight.
5. **Take moisture readings** before and after drying. A meter reading of "still wet at hour 48" tells you to keep drying; a "dry" reading lets you safely close the wall.
6. **Don't paint over a fresh stain.** Stain-blocking primer covers the color, which feels like progress, but if the wall is still wet you've just trapped the moisture. Confirm dry first.
7. **Call your insurer if the damage is more than cosmetic.** Most "sudden and accidental" water damage is covered; gradual seepage usually isn't. The Insurance Information Institute notes that timely notification — within 24–48 hours — is part of most policies.

## When to shut off the main water supply

**Shut the main immediately when:**

- Water is actively pouring, spraying, or pooling.
- A pipe has burst (any age, any room).
- A water heater is failing and water is reaching electrical or seeping under a wall.
- A toilet is overflowing and the local shutoff won't stop it.
- Drywall is sagging with water trapped above.
- You smell sewage with standing water.

**You can usually wait when:**

- A confirmed slow leak you can localize is in a fixture you've shut off locally.
- A small drip in a non-finished area you can contain with a bucket.

If you've never used the main shutoff, find it today and test that it closes. The most common failure during a real leak is the gate-valve handle that hasn't turned in 20 years and won't turn now. If it's stiff, don't force — call a plumber to replace it on a calm afternoon rather than at midnight in a flood.

## When to call a plumber

- Water meter shows leak, you can't find the source after the fixture checks.
- Wet wall, ceiling, or floor with no obvious supply line in reach.
- Slab leak signs — warm or wet spots in floor, sound of water under the slab.
- Underground service-line leak (the meter moves with the house main shut).
- Active leak near electrical you can't safely isolate.
- Mold or wet framing exposed and you're not equipped for remediation.

## A practical homeowner checklist

Run this every six months. Most takes less than an hour:

- **Meter test** — write down the meter reading, wait two hours with no water use, confirm no movement.
- **Toilet dye test** every toilet — confirm flappers seal.
- **Walk every faucet** — check for drips, slow flow, and water at the base.
- **Open every base cabinet** — feel supply lines, look for crust or wet.
- **Inspect water heater base and drain pan** — look for staining and rust.
- **Check washing machine hoses** — replace rubber with braided stainless every 5–8 years.
- **Check refrigerator ice maker line** — wet floor behind the fridge usually means a slow seep.
- **Walk the yard** — look for wet patches with no irrigation running, unusually green strips, settled walkways.
- **Test the main shutoff valve** — close it and re-open it. Confirm it actually works.
- **Replace puck-style leak sensor batteries** — most last 1–2 years, replace on a schedule.
- **Document the readings.** Write down what you found and the date. A history of "all clear" reads makes the first abnormal reading obvious.

A homeowner who does this routine catches most leaks in the cheap-to-fix stage. Skipping it is how a $15 flapper turns into a $4,000 drywall job.
