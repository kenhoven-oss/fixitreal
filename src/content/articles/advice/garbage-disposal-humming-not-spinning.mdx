A humming garbage disposal is jammed, not broken. The motor is trying to spin the flywheel but something — a bone, a pit, a utensil — is wedged in the grind chamber. The fix is fast: turn off the switch, clear the jam with the hex wrench that came with the disposal, hit the reset button on the bottom, and you&apos;re back in business in under 10 minutes. The whole repair is well within DIY range.

**Turn off the disposal switch immediately if it&apos;s still humming.** A locked motor pulling stall current overheats fast.

## What this usually means

Modern disposals have three layers of protection: a flywheel that spins, a motor that drives it, and a thermal-protection circuit that shuts the motor down if it stalls for too long. When something gets stuck between the flywheel and the grind chamber wall, the motor tries to spin, can&apos;t, and either trips the thermal protector or burns out if you leave it on.

The hum is the motor pulling locked-rotor current — the same sound you&apos;d hear if you held a power drill chuck so it couldn&apos;t turn. It&apos;s not normal operation; it&apos;s the disposal asking for help.

## The 10-minute fix

**Step 1: Turn off the switch.** Most disposals have a switch on the wall by the sink. If yours doesn&apos;t, kill the breaker. Don&apos;t skip this — you&apos;re about to put a wrench inside a grinder.

**Step 2: Find the hex wrench.** Almost every disposal ships with a 1/4-inch hex wrench, and a clip on the bottom of the unit to store it. If you&apos;ve lost yours, any 1/4-inch hex wrench from a hardware store will do.

**Step 3: Unjam the flywheel.** Look at the bottom of the disposal — there&apos;s a hex-shaped hole in the center. Insert the wrench, and turn it back and forth. You&apos;ll feel the flywheel resisting at first, then breaking free. Keep rocking it until you can spin the flywheel a full revolution in both directions.

**Step 4: Fish out what was stuck.** Look down into the disposal with a flashlight. Use **needle-nose pliers or tongs** — never your hand — to pull out whatever caused the jam. Common culprits: chicken bones, peach pits, cherry pits, avocado pits, plastic spoons that fell in unnoticed, twist ties, vegetable peel mats.

**Step 5: Hit the reset button.** On the bottom of the disposal, dead center, there&apos;s a small red or black reset button. Push it firmly until you feel a click. If it doesn&apos;t click, the thermal protector hadn&apos;t tripped — fine, the disposal is just waiting for its switch.

**Step 6: Run cold water and turn it on.** Cold water, not hot — cold solidifies any grease and lets it grind. With water running, flip the switch. The disposal should run clean. Let it run for 30 seconds with water to flush any debris.

## When it&apos;s safe to DIY (almost always)

A jammed disposal is one of the most common — and easiest — kitchen repairs. If you can find the wrench and follow the steps above, you&apos;re done. No plumbing changes, no electrical work, no skill requirements beyond reading directions.

## When to call a plumber or replace the unit

A small number of cases need professional help or replacement:

- **The flywheel won&apos;t budge with the wrench or with a wooden spoon used on the impellers from inside.** Severe jams sometimes mean the motor itself has seized, or a foreign object is wedged so tightly the disposal needs to be removed and disassembled.
- **The reset button trips again within minutes** of restarting. The motor is failing — replacement, not repair.
- **You smell burning** from the disposal, or the motor sounds different than usual (grinding, scraping). The bearings or windings are damaged.
- **The disposal is also leaking** from the bottom (cracked housing) or the top (failing flange seal). Replacement.

A new mid-range disposal runs $80–$200 (parts); install is a 90-minute DIY for someone comfortable with sink plumbing, or $150–$250 hired. See our [DIY-vs-hire guide on garbage disposals](/diy-or-hire/garbage-disposal) for the full decision.

## Red flags

- A handyman or plumber telling you the disposal &ldquo;needs to be replaced&rdquo; without first attempting the wrench fix. A jam takes 60 seconds to test for. Anyone skipping that step is selling, not diagnosing.
- A service call that turns into a full replacement quote on an otherwise fine, 3–5 year old disposal.
- &ldquo;Special disposal cleaner&rdquo; or &ldquo;treatments&rdquo; sold as part of a service visit. Hot water, dish soap, and the occasional ice-cube grind are all the maintenance a disposal needs.

## What I&apos;d do

Always try the wrench fix first. Always. The wrench is in the bag of parts that came with your disposal — or under the sink in a small ziploc — or available at any hardware store for $2. Five minutes with that wrench saves a $150 service call about 90% of the time.

Run cold water for 10 seconds before and after each use, avoid putting in stringy vegetables (celery, asparagus, banana peels), starchy foods (potato peels, pasta, rice), and anything fibrous or hard (bones, pits, shells, eggshells over time). That habit alone prevents most jams.
