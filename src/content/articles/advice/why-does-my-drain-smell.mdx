<QuickAnswer>
**Smelly drain causes, in order of likelihood:**

1. **Dried P-trap** (40% of cases) — run water for 30 seconds.
2. **Biofilm buildup in the drain pipe** (30%) — enzymatic cleaner + snake.
3. **Garbage disposal residue** (15%) — ice + salt scrub, lemon peel deodorize.
4. **Vent stack issue** (10%) — plumber, $200–$600.
5. **Sewer line crack** (5%) — plumber + possibly camera inspection, $300–$5,000+.

**Quickest diagnosis:** if running water in the smelly drain stops the smell for hours but it returns, biofilm. If smell goes away permanently after running water, dried trap. If smell persists with water running, vent or sewer issue.
</QuickAnswer>

## Fix #1: Refill the P-trap (most common)

Every drain in your house has a U-shaped pipe under it called a P-trap. The U holds a small amount of water permanently — that water blocks sewer gas from rising up the pipe and into your room.

If a drain isn't used for weeks (guest bathroom, basement sink, laundry-room floor drain), the trap water evaporates. Sewer gas then has a clear path into the room.

**Fix:** run the faucet for 30 seconds. Done.

**For floor drains and rarely-used fixtures:** pour a cup of water down monthly. Or pour mineral oil in — it floats on top of the trap water and slows evaporation dramatically.

## Fix #2: Clean drain biofilm

Biofilm is a colony of bacteria, fungi, and trapped organic material (hair, soap scum, food residue) that grows on the inside of drain pipes. The smell is the colony's metabolic output — often sulfurous or musty.

**Mechanical removal:**
1. Use a small flexible drain brush (about $8) to physically scrub the inside of the drain.
2. Or use a [drain snake](/tools/best-drain-snakes-for-homeowners) on slow-flowing drains to pull out the worst of it.

**Chemical / biological treatment:**
- **Enzymatic drain cleaner** ($10–$15): pour in per the bottle's instructions, leave overnight. Bacterial enzymes specifically target biofilm. Apply weekly for maintenance.
- **Baking soda + vinegar**: pour 1/2 cup baking soda, then 1 cup white vinegar. Wait 30 minutes. Flush with hot water. Less effective than enzymatic but cheaper.

**Avoid:**
- Bleach (damages old pipes, can mix dangerously with later cleaners).
- Drano / Liquid-Plumr (corrosive, ineffective on biofilm specifically).

## Fix #3: Garbage disposal deep-clean

Most "smelly kitchen drain" calls are actually a smelly disposal. The residue builds up under the rubber baffle at the top of the disposal — a place most homeowners never look.

**Monthly disposal-clean routine:**
1. Cold water on, disposal running.
2. Drop in a tray of ice cubes + 1/2 cup coarse rock salt or kosher salt. Run 30 seconds. The ice + salt scours the inside walls.
3. Cut a lemon or orange in half, drop in, run 30 seconds. Citric acid + oils deodorize.
4. Turn disposal off. Lift the rubber baffle (the splash guard at the top). Scrub the underside with a stiff brush + dish soap. This is where most odor lives.
5. Run cold water for 15 seconds to flush.

Don't pour bleach into a disposal — damages the rubber components and can splash back.

## Fix #4: Check the vent stack

Every plumbing system has a vent stack — a vertical pipe that runs up through the roof. The vent lets sewer gas escape outside and prevents pressure buildup that would otherwise suck water out of P-traps when fixtures drain.

Vent stack problems:
- **Blockage** (leaves, bird nest, ice plug in winter) — sewer gas backs into the house through traps.
- **Cracked vent** in attic or wall cavity — sewer gas leaks into framing.
- **Wrong configuration** in older homes (pre-1960s plumbing).

Symptoms:
- Drain gurgling sounds when other drains run.
- Sewer smell that doesn't go away with water running.
- P-trap water disappearing without cause (siphoned out by improper venting).

A plumber diagnoses with a camera or smoke test. Fix: clear the blockage from the roof, or repair the vent run. $200–$600 typical.

## Fix #5: Sewer line crack

The most serious cause. A cracked sewer line under the house or yard releases gas into the surrounding soil — eventually leaking into basement, crawlspace, or living areas.

Symptoms:
- Persistent sewer smell with no fixture explanation.
- Soft / sunken patches in the yard above the sewer line route.
- Slow drains throughout the house.
- Multiple drains backing up at once.

Diagnosis: plumber runs a camera down the main cleanout. Sees the crack on a monitor.

Repair varies widely:
- **Spot repair** (dig and replace one section): $1,500–$3,500.
- **Cured-in-place pipe lining** (no-dig): $4,000–$8,000.
- **Full sewer line replacement**: $5,000–$15,000+.

## When to call a plumber

- You've refreshed all P-traps, cleaned the disposal, and used enzymatic cleaner — smell persists.
- Smell is in multiple rooms simultaneously.
- Drains are gurgling and slow throughout the house.
- You see yard wetness or sinking soil near the sewer line.

Expect a typical service call at [$150–$400](/costs/plumber-service-call) for trip + diagnostic. A camera inspection adds $150–$400.

## Prevention

- Run every drain (including rarely-used ones) once a month for 30 seconds.
- Use enzymatic drain cleaner monthly in kitchen + main bathroom drains.
- Clean the disposal monthly (ice/salt + lemon + brush under the baffle).
- Don't pour grease, coffee grounds, or starchy food (rice, pasta) down kitchen drains — biofilm food.
- Use a hair-catcher in shower drains.

The combined cost is under $30/year and prevents the most common $150+ plumber calls.
