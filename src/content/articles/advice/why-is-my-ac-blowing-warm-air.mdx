<QuickAnswer>
**The 6 causes of AC blowing warm air, ranked by likelihood:**

1. **Thermostat set wrong** — set to AUTO not ON, set to COOL not HEAT, target temp below room temp.
2. **Dirty air filter** — restricting airflow, causing the coil to ice up. Change it.
3. **Outdoor condenser not running** — capacitor or contactor failure ($200–$450 HVAC visit).
4. **Frozen evaporator coil** — turn off for 2–4 hours, change filter, restart.
5. **Low refrigerant (leak)** — needs HVAC tech, refrigerant + leak repair ($400–$1,200).
6. **Failed compressor** — most expensive ($1,500–$3,500 repair or full replacement).

**Quickest diagnosis:** walk outside while the thermostat calls for cooling. Is the outdoor unit fan spinning? If no → outdoor unit problem. If yes → indoor unit / refrigerant problem.
</QuickAnswer>

## Quick diagnostic flow

### Step 1: Check the thermostat (30 seconds)

- **Mode:** set to COOL (not HEAT, not OFF, not FAN ONLY).
- **Fan:** set to AUTO (not ON).
- **Target temp:** set 5°F below current room temp.
- **Power:** display lit? If not, replace batteries.

A surprisingly large fraction of "AC blowing warm air" calls resolve here. The "fan ON" mistake is the most common — fan circulates room-temp air through the system without cooling, which feels like warm air.

### Step 2: Walk outside (30 seconds)

While the thermostat is actively calling for cooling:

- **Listen** for the outdoor condenser. It should be running, fan spinning, hot air coming off the top.
- **If silent and not spinning** → outdoor unit problem (capacitor, contactor, or compressor).
- **If spinning normally** → indoor unit or refrigerant problem.

This split decides which of the next sections applies.

## If the outdoor unit isn't running

Three most common causes:

### 3a. Failed capacitor ($200–$450 HVAC visit)

The start capacitor gives the compressor and condenser fan a high-voltage kick to start spinning. When it fails, the unit hums (trying to start) but the fan doesn't spin, or the compressor doesn't engage.

- Diagnostic: HVAC tech tests with a multimeter, often finds in 5 minutes.
- Part cost: $25–$80. Labor: $150–$300.
- DIY discouraged: capacitors hold lethal voltage even when power is off. Pros only.

### 3b. Failed contactor ($150–$400 HVAC visit)

The contactor is the heavy-duty relay that switches power to the compressor. Pitted or burned contacts prevent the unit from starting.

- Part cost: $20–$70. Labor: $100–$250.
- HVAC tech work.

### 3c. Tripped breaker (free fix)

Outdoor AC units have a dedicated 240V breaker. If tripped, the unit gets no power.

- Reset once. If it trips again immediately → don't reset a second time. That's a short or major fault. Call HVAC.

### 3d. Failed compressor (replace the system)

The compressor is the heart of the outdoor unit. When it fails, the part itself runs $800–$1,800 + 4–6 hours of labor = $1,500–$3,500 repair. For a unit over 10 years old, replacing the system makes more sense than replacing the compressor.

## If the outdoor unit IS running but indoor air is warm

### 4. Frozen evaporator coil

The most common indoor-side problem. The coil (mounted above the furnace or in the air handler) ices up and air can't pass through it, or air passing through the ice barely cools.

Causes:
- **Dirty air filter** restricting airflow.
- **Low refrigerant** lowering coil temperature below freezing.
- **Failing blower motor** not moving enough air across the coil.
- **Closed/blocked supply vents** in too many rooms.

**Fix attempt:**
1. Turn off the AC at the thermostat.
2. Set fan to ON for 2–4 hours to thaw the coil with room-temperature airflow.
3. Change the air filter.
4. Open any closed supply vents (don't reduce airflow to "balance" rooms with a vent-closing strategy).
5. Restart in COOL mode.

If the coil freezes again within 24 hours → call HVAC. The refrigerant charge or blower is the cause.

### 5. Low refrigerant (leak)

A refrigerant leak doesn't make the AC "low" in the sense of needing top-off — it means there's a leak. Adding refrigerant without fixing the leak is a temporary patch.

Symptoms:
- Cool air at first, gets warmer as the run goes on.
- Coil freezes (see above).
- Hissing sound near the indoor coil or outdoor lineset.
- AC runs constantly without reaching setpoint.

Diagnostic: HVAC tech with an electronic leak detector finds the leak in 30–60 minutes. Repair varies widely:
- **Schrader valve leak** (the access port): $150–$250.
- **Lineset leak**: $300–$600.
- **Coil leak (indoor)**: $800–$1,500. Coil under warranty, labor isn't.
- **Coil leak (outdoor)**: $700–$1,400.
- **Compressor leak**: usually a system replacement on units over 10 years.

## When to call HVAC

- You've checked thermostat, filter, and outdoor unit, and the issue persists.
- The outdoor fan isn't spinning when the thermostat calls for cool.
- Ice is visible on the indoor coil or refrigerant line.
- The unit hums but doesn't start.
- Warm air with no obvious cause and the unit is over 10 years old.

Expect a typical HVAC service call in the [$100–$250 range](/costs/hvac-service-call-cost) for trip + diagnostic, plus repair cost depending on cause.

## When to consider replacement instead

For units 10–15 years old with any major repair quote over $1,500: get a [replacement quote](/costs/ac-replacement-cost) for comparison. Modern SEER 16 units run dramatically more efficiently than 2010-era units; the operating-cost savings can pay back the upgrade in 4–8 years.

Also consider whether a heat pump replacement makes more sense than another AC + furnace combination. See [heat pump vs furnace](/advice/heat-pump-vs-furnace) for the math.
