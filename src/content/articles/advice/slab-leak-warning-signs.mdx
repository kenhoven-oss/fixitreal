A slab leak is the most expensive plumbing problem a homeowner can ignore. The line under your foundation is pressurized 24/7, and the water has nowhere to go but into the soil and concrete you're standing on. Catching it in the warning-sign stage costs hundreds. Catching it after the slab cracks costs tens of thousands.

<QuickAnswer>
**The 10 most common slab leak warning signs:**

1. A warm or hot spot on the floor (hot-water line leak)
2. Damp carpet, vinyl, or hardwood with no obvious source
3. The sound of running water under the floor when nothing is on
4. A water bill that jumps with no usage change
5. The water meter creeping with every fixture off
6. Reduced water pressure throughout the house
7. Mildew or musty smell at floor level
8. Cracked or shifted flooring tiles
9. Cracks in interior walls, especially at door frames
10. The water heater running more than usual (hot-line leak)

Any single sign is worth investigating. Two or more together is a slab leak until proven otherwise. Confirm with a [water-meter leak test](/advice/water-leak-detection#how-to-perform-a-water-meter-leak-test); call a leak-detection specialist before authorizing any repair.
</QuickAnswer>

<SafetyWarning>
A confirmed slab leak is not a wait-and-see problem. Active water escaping under a foundation undermines the soil that holds the slab up — over weeks and months, this can lead to differential settling, slab cracking, and structural damage to load-bearing walls. If you see new cracks in interior walls or door frames combined with any of the warning signs below, treat it as a structural-urgency item, not a plumbing inconvenience.
</SafetyWarning>

## The 10 warning signs, in order

### 1. A warm or hot spot on the floor

This is the most diagnostic single sign. A hot-water line leaking under a tile, vinyl, hardwood, or carpet floor warms the surface above the leak — often by 5–10°F more than the surrounding floor. The cleanest test: walk the house barefoot in the morning before HVAC has run, and note any patches that feel noticeably warmer.

False positives: a heated water line passing under a tile near a bathroom; an attic-mounted air handler dumping condensate; a south-facing window heating one section of floor. But if a hot patch is mid-room and persistent, that's a strong tell.

### 2. Damp carpet, vinyl, or hardwood

Carpet that stays damp when there's no humidity in the house, vinyl that's lifting at the edges, or hardwood that's cupping or warping with no rain in the forecast. Press a paper towel against the suspect spot — if it comes away damp, water is coming from below.

### 3. The sound of running water under the floor

Press an ear to the floor at night when the house is quiet. A continuous hiss, tick, or whisper is water escaping a pressurized line. Compare the sound at multiple spots; the loudest point is closest to the leak.

### 4. A water bill that jumps with no usage change

A 20–50% increase month-over-month without any new occupants, new appliances, or seasonal usage shift. The EPA estimates household leaks waste roughly a trillion gallons of water nationally each year — your share shows up on the bill before it shows up on the floor.

### 5. The water meter creeping with every fixture off

Run the [water-meter leak test from our detection guide](/advice/water-leak-detection#how-to-perform-a-water-meter-leak-test): shut off every fixture, write down the reading, wait two hours, check again. Movement = leak somewhere on your side of the meter. If the meter still moves after you shut the *house main* valve, the leak is in the buried service line between meter and house — not a slab leak but a related problem. If the meter stops only after you shut the house main, the leak is inside the house — which on a slab home often means a slab leak.

### 6. Reduced water pressure throughout the house

Especially noticeable on the hot side. A leak in the hot-water slab loop bleeds off pressure that should be reaching showers and fixtures. A single low-pressure fixture is usually a cartridge or aerator; pressure dropping at every hot fixture in the house is a system signal.

### 7. Mildew or musty smell at floor level

Get down to floor level and sniff baseboards, closet corners, and under cabinets in the room above the suspect leak. Mold needs moisture and food (drywall paper, carpet padding); a slab leak provides both. The EPA's mold guidance emphasizes that musty smells often precede visible growth.

### 8. Cracked or shifted flooring tiles

A grout line that suddenly cracks across multiple tiles, especially near a wall, can indicate slab movement caused by soil erosion under the slab. New cracks in previously stable tile floors are a structural signal, not just cosmetic.

### 9. Cracks in interior walls, especially at door frames

When a slab loses uniform support, parts of the foundation settle. Drywall cracks at the upper corners of door frames are a classic signal — the frame is no longer parallel because the slab beneath has shifted slightly. Stair-step cracks in tile or brick exterior siding are another. Any new interior wall crack within a month of new water-related symptoms is a slab leak red flag.

### 10. The water heater running more than usual

A hot-water slab leak makes the water heater work harder to maintain temperature. You may notice the burner cycling more often, the recovery time getting longer, or the gas/electric bill creeping up alongside the water bill. If the [water heater is leaking](/advice/water-heater-leaking) at the unit itself, that's a separate issue — but a heater that's working overtime with no visible leak at the tank is consistent with a hot slab leak somewhere downstream.

## What to do when you suspect a slab leak

1. **Run the meter test.** This is the cheapest possible confirmation. 15 minutes of setup, 2 hours of patience.
2. **Map the warm spots.** Walk the floor barefoot and mark with painter's tape any spots noticeably warmer than the surrounding floor.
3. **Shut off the water heater's cold supply** as a test. If the warm spot disappears within 30–60 minutes, the leak is on the hot side. If it persists, it may be a cold-line leak.
4. **Call a leak-detection specialist** — not just any plumber. You need acoustic and thermal imaging gear. See our [professional leak detection cost guide](/costs/professional-leak-detection-cost) for what a fair quote looks like ($350–$650 for slab-specific detection).
5. **Document everything** with photos, dates, and meter readings before any cutting starts. Insurance adjusters use this evidence; without it, claim outcomes get worse.
6. **Don't authorize repair until detection is complete.** A specialist with the right gear should localize the leak within a square foot. Anyone proposing to "open the slab and look" without acoustic confirmation is gambling with your foundation.

## Repair options — what specialists actually do

There are three accepted approaches. The right pick depends on the leak location, how many leaks exist, the pipe material, and your timeline.

### Spot repair

Jackhammer through the slab to the leak, replace the failed section, patch the concrete. Lowest dollar cost ($1,500–$3,500 typical), highest disruption (concrete dust, exposed subfloor for 1–3 days, flooring restoration after).

**Pick when:** the leak is a single isolated failure, the pipe is otherwise sound, you have many years left in the house, and the leak is in an accessible spot (not under a kitchen island or a load-bearing wall).

### Pipe rerouting (overhead)

Abandon the under-slab line. Run a new line through the attic to bypass the slab section. No concrete work. Cost: $2,000–$4,500 typical, depending on pipe length and attic access.

**Pick when:** the leak is in a difficult-to-access spot, you want zero concrete cutting, or you suspect more failures will follow on aging copper. The new line is fully accessible for future maintenance.

### Full repipe

Replace all under-slab supply lines, usually via overhead routing. Cost: $5,000–$15,000+ for a typical home. Often combined with a kitchen or bathroom remodel.

**Pick when:** multiple slab leaks have already occurred, the supply system is at end of life (40+ years on copper in aggressive soil, 30+ years on galvanized), or you're already opening walls for other work.

## What insurance typically covers

Standard U.S. homeowners policies vary widely on slab leaks. Common patterns:

- **Resulting water damage** (drywall, flooring, contents): usually covered under "sudden and accidental" provisions.
- **The pipe itself**: typically *not* covered (the leak is the cause of damage, not the damage).
- **Access cost** (cutting the slab to reach the pipe): coverage varies — some policies include it under "access" or "tear-out"; many exclude it.
- **Damage from "continuous or repeated seepage"**: typically excluded if the leak ran for weeks before detection.

This is why the warning-sign list matters. Insurers look for evidence the homeowner acted promptly. A documented timeline of "noticed warm spot Tuesday, ran meter test Wednesday, called specialist Thursday, claim filed Friday" is a different conversation than "the slab cracked and I think it's been leaking for a year."

Call your insurer before authorizing repair. The Insurance Information Institute notes that timely notification — typically within 24–48 hours of discovery — is part of most policies' covered-loss qualifications.

## What it costs to *not* fix it

A slab leak left active for 12 months typically results in:

- $5,000–$15,000 of water damage to flooring, baseboards, and drywall.
- $2,000–$8,000 in mold remediation if humidity stays high.
- Possible foundation repair if soil erodes enough to allow slab settling: $5,000–$30,000.
- Higher long-term insurance premiums or non-renewal if the claim shows up as preventable.

The math always favors early detection. The diagnostic visit is the cheapest part of the entire problem.

## A practical homeowner check

If you live on a slab foundation in a region with copper-pipe homes (most of the U.S. South, Southwest, and California), add these to your six-month homeowner check:

- Walk every floor barefoot — note warm or wet spots.
- Run the meter test — confirm no movement with all fixtures off.
- Look at water bills for trend changes.
- Inspect door-frame corners for new drywall cracks.
- Confirm the water heater recovery time hasn't changed.

A history of "all clear" reads makes the first abnormal one easy to spot. That's the whole game with hidden plumbing.
