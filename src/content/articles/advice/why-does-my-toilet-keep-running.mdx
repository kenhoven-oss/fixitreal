A constantly-running toilet is the single biggest source of silent household water waste. It's also one of the easiest fixes — most are $5 in parts and 10 minutes of work.

<QuickAnswer>
**The 5 causes of a running toilet, in order of frequency:**

1. **Worn flapper** (~70% of cases) — $5 part, 5-minute fix.
2. **Chain too long or tangled** (~15%) — free fix, 2 minutes.
3. **Fill valve failure** (~10%) — $15 part, 15-minute fix.
4. **Water level too high in tank** (~3%) — adjust float, free.
5. **Cracked toilet handle / flush lever** (~2%) — $8 part, 5-minute fix.

**Test which one:** Put food coloring in the tank water (don't flush). Wait 15 minutes. If color appears in the bowl → flapper. If tank water level keeps rising even after the fill valve should stop → fill valve. If the toilet only runs briefly every 5–10 minutes → also flapper.
</QuickAnswer>

## The dye test (do this first)

Before buying any parts:

1. Add 10 drops of food coloring or a leak-test dye tablet to the tank water.
2. Don't flush. Wait 15 minutes.
3. Look in the bowl.

**Color in the bowl** → the flapper isn't sealing. Replace it.

**No color in the bowl, but the toilet still runs** → not the flapper. Check chain, fill valve, water level.

The dye test takes 15 minutes and saves you from buying the wrong part.

## Fix #1: Replace the flapper

The flapper is the rubber disc at the bottom of the tank that lifts when you flush and seals the tank from the bowl between flushes. Over years of submersion and chlorinated water, rubber stiffens, warps, and loses its seal.

**Steps:**
1. Shut off the water supply to the toilet (valve under the tank, turn clockwise).
2. Flush to empty the tank.
3. Unhook the chain from the flush lever.
4. Lift the old flapper off the overflow tube hooks.
5. Take the old flapper to the hardware store for an exact match (universal flappers fit most toilets but not all).
6. Slide the new flapper onto the overflow tube, hook the chain back to the lever with about 1/2 inch slack.
7. Turn the water back on, let the tank refill, test flush.

Total time: 10 minutes. Cost: $5–$15 depending on toilet model.

## Fix #2: Adjust the chain

If the chain has too much slack, the flapper can shift sideways and not seat properly. If too tight, the flapper can't fully close. Test:

- With the handle at rest, the chain should have **about 1/2 inch of slack** before going taut.
- If your chain has multiple inches of dangling slack → shorten by hooking the chain higher on the flush lever.
- If your chain is so tight that it lifts the flapper at rest → lengthen one link.

Sometimes the chain itself is the problem — twisted, knotted, or kinked. Untangle, replace if needed (universal chain assemblies are $3).

## Fix #3: Replace the fill valve

The fill valve refills the tank after a flush. When it fails (often after 10+ years), it can either:
- Never fully shut off — toilet runs continuously.
- Shut off intermittently — toilet refills randomly.
- Whistle or hiss — wearing out.

**Steps:**
1. Shut off the water supply.
2. Flush to empty the tank.
3. Sponge out any remaining water in the tank.
4. Disconnect the supply line from the bottom of the fill valve.
5. Unscrew the lock nut underneath the tank (use channel-lock pliers).
6. Lift the old fill valve out.
7. Install the new fill valve (universal Fluidmaster-style is the standard).
8. Adjust the height per the included instructions (water level should be about 1 inch below the overflow tube).
9. Reconnect supply line, turn water on, test.

Total time: 20 minutes. Cost: $15–$25 for the part.

## Fix #4: Adjust the water level

If the water level in the tank rises high enough to spill into the overflow tube, the toilet will run constantly while the fill valve tries (and fails) to reach shutoff height.

**On modern fill valves:** there's a height adjustment screw or clip on the fill valve itself. Turn the screw or move the clip down to lower the water level.

**On older ball-float valves:** the float ball is on an adjustable arm. Bend the arm down slightly to lower the water level.

Target water level: about 1 inch below the top of the overflow tube. There's usually a line marked inside the tank.

## Fix #5: Replace a cracked handle

A cracked or stuck flush handle can hold the flapper open. Symptom: the toilet flushes, fills, and keeps running because the lever inside the tank stays partially raised.

**Steps:**
1. Reach inside the tank, hold the flush lever in the "rest" position.
2. Unscrew the handle nut from inside the tank (often left-hand threads — turn clockwise to loosen).
3. Remove the old handle.
4. Install the new handle, tighten the nut, reattach the chain.

Universal handles match most toilets. $8–$15 part, 5 minutes.

## When the running toilet is actually leaking outside the tank

If you see water on the floor near the toilet, the issue isn't the flapper or fill valve. See:
- [Toilet leaking at the base](/advice/toilet-leaking-at-the-base) — wax ring failure, supply line leak, tank-to-bowl bolt failure.

## What about the water bill?

A flapper that's been leaking for a year costs $150–$400 in extra water on the typical municipal bill. The fix is $5. The dye test once a year on every toilet in the house is the cheapest plumbing maintenance you can do.

## When to replace the whole toilet

- The porcelain is cracked.
- The toilet was installed before 1994 (pre-low-flow era; uses 3.5+ gallons per flush vs. modern 1.28).
- You've replaced the flapper and fill valve twice in 3 years.
