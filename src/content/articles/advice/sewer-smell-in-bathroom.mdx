A sewer smell in your bathroom almost always traces to one of three causes — and you can diagnose all three yourself in under 10 minutes before paying any plumber. The most common (a dried-out trap) is fixed by pouring water down the drain. The next most common (a failed wax ring under the toilet) is a $15 DIY repair. The third (a blocked vent stack) needs a plumber but is the rarest of the three. Walk through them in order before calling anyone.

## What sewer gas actually is

The drain system carries waste water out of your home and into the city sewer or septic. The same lines carry **sewer gas** — hydrogen sulfide (the rotten-egg smell), methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide. The whole drain system is sealed against gas escape by two mechanisms: **traps** (water reservoirs that block gas at every fixture) and **vents** (pipes that let gas safely exit through the roof).

When gas is getting into the house, one of those two systems is failing somewhere.

## Diagnose in order

### 1. Dried-out P-trap (the most common cause — fix in 30 seconds)

Every drain in your bathroom has a U-shaped P-trap below it (visible under sinks; hidden in the floor under tubs and toilets) that holds an inch or two of water. That water seal blocks sewer gas.

In rarely-used bathrooms — guest bathrooms, second homes, basement bathrooms, or after extended travel — the trap water can evaporate over weeks. Once empty, sewer gas comes straight up.

**Diagnostic + fix:** pour a quart of water down each drain in the affected bathroom, including the floor drain if present. Wait an hour. Smell gone? Trap was dry. Done.

If you live in the home full-time, the most common dried-trap culprits are:
- Floor drains that don&apos;t see water in normal use
- Showers used only by guests
- Sinks in basements or workshops

Pour a quart of water down each of these monthly to prevent the issue.

### 2. Failed wax ring under the toilet ($5–$15 DIY)

If the smell persists after fixing the traps, the next likely culprit is the [wax ring](/glossary/wax-ring) under the toilet. Every toilet sits on a wax ring that seals the toilet&apos;s outlet horn against the floor flange. When that seal fails, sewer gas escapes around the base of the toilet.

**Diagnostic:**
- Smell is strongest at the floor level, around the base of the toilet
- Toilet may rock slightly when sat on
- Sometimes a small water mark or stain at the floor around the toilet base

**Fix:** pull the toilet, scrape the old wax, install a new ring ($5–$15 at any hardware store), set the toilet back, retighten the closet bolts. 90-minute DIY job — see our [Should I fix a leaking toilet myself?](/diy-or-hire/toilet) guide for the full procedure.

If the toilet is rocking visibly, check the closet flange too — a cracked or rotted flange is sometimes the underlying cause. Replacement flange + new wax ring is still DIY for a confident homeowner, or $200–$400 with a plumber.

### 3. Blocked vent stack ($200–$500 hired)

The least common cause but the one that requires a plumber. Every drain system has a [vent stack](/glossary/vent-stack) — a vertical pipe that exits through the roof, letting sewer gas safely escape outside and admitting fresh air so drains flow properly.

If the vent stack is blocked at the roof — birds, ice, leaves, tennis balls, dead animals — gas can&apos;t exit normally and starts pushing back through trap seals (overcoming the water in the trap and bubbling up into the home).

**Diagnostic clues:**
- Drains gurgle when they empty
- Multiple fixtures smell of sewer at the same time
- Smell appears or gets worse after running other drains (a load of laundry, a flushed toilet)
- Slow drainage in addition to the smell

**Fix:** call a plumber. They&apos;ll inspect the roof vent (sometimes from inside via a camera) and clear the blockage. $200–$500 typical, including the trip and one-shot clearing.

### Rarer causes

- **S-trap instead of P-trap** (older homes, code violation). S-traps siphon themselves dry. See [P-trap vs S-trap](/glossary/p-vs-s-trap). Fix: re-pipe to P-trap with proper vent, $400–$1,500.
- **House trap clog** (older homes with a main-line trap). Symptom: smell appears when other parts of the house drain. Fix: $1,500–$5,000 for trap removal + re-pipe.
- **Cracked drain pipe inside a wall.** Rare. Symptom: smell strongest at a particular wall location, sometimes with damp drywall. Fix: drywall removal + plumber repair.

## What NOT to do

- **Don&apos;t pour bleach down drains repeatedly.** Bleach destroys trap seals over time and corrodes some pipe materials. One pour is fine; routine use isn&apos;t.
- **Don&apos;t use &ldquo;sewer-gas removal products.&rdquo;** Mostly perfume sprays. Doesn&apos;t fix the underlying gap.
- **Don&apos;t seal the toilet base with caulk** unless you&apos;ve actually replaced the wax ring. Caulk hides the smell temporarily; trap pressure builds inside and pushes through faster next time.
- **Don&apos;t ignore it.** Chronic sewer gas exposure isn&apos;t acutely dangerous in residential concentrations but the source is a real plumbing failure that escalates. Find and fix it.

## Red flags from a plumber visit

- **Quote includes &ldquo;sewer gas elimination treatment&rdquo;** without identifying the source. There&apos;s no such product. Either it&apos;s a dried trap, a wax ring, or a vent issue.
- **Recommendation to replace the entire toilet** when only the wax ring is suspect. The toilet itself almost never causes sewer-gas leaks; the seal under it does.
- **&ldquo;Whole-house drain inspection&rdquo;** quoted at $500+ for a single sewer-smell complaint without other symptoms. Disproportionate.

## What I&apos;d do

In order: pour water down every drain. Wait an hour. If still smelling, smell-test around the toilet base. If localized there, plan the wax-ring repair (90-minute DIY + $15 parts). If still generalized after both checks, call a plumber and specifically ask them to inspect the vent stack — that&apos;s the third-most-common cause and the one that requires their tools.

The vast majority of sewer-smell complaints resolve at step 1 (dried trap) or step 2 (wax ring). Don&apos;t pay for an expensive diagnostic before doing them yourself first.
