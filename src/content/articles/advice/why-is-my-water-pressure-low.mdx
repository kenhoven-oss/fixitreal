<QuickAnswer>
**Low water pressure causes, in order from cheapest to most expensive to fix:**

1. **Clogged aerator** ($0 — clean by hand) — if only one faucet is low.
2. **Partially closed shutoff valve** ($0 — open the valve) — at the main or under a sink.
3. **Sediment in water heater** ($0–$50 — drain and flush) — hot-only low pressure.
4. **Worn faucet cartridge** ($15–$40 — replace) — one faucet, hot AND cold low.
5. **Pressure regulator failure** ($300–$650 — plumber) — whole house, gradual decline.
6. **Water main partially closed** ($0 — call utility) — if you've done work or had a shutoff recently.
7. **Galvanized pipe corrosion** ($3,000–$8,000+ — repipe) — old homes, gradual decline over years.
8. **Service line leak** ($1,500–$5,000 — plumber) — confirm with water-meter leak test.

**Diagnostic key: is it ALL fixtures or ONE?** All = whole-house cause (regulator, main, line leak). One = local cause (aerator, cartridge, supply valve).
</QuickAnswer>

## The one-fixture vs. whole-house split

Walk the house. Test every fixture — kitchen, bathroom sinks, shower, tub, outdoor hose bibs. Note which are low.

- **Only one fixture low** → cause is at that fixture (aerator, cartridge, supply, shutoff valve).
- **All fixtures on one bathroom low** → cause is at the bathroom branch (often a shutoff valve or shared supply line).
- **All hot fixtures low, cold fine** → water heater or hot supply problem.
- **Every fixture in the house low** → whole-house cause (pressure regulator, main valve, service line, or utility).

This single test rules out 80% of possible causes within 5 minutes.

## The cheapest fixes (do these first)

### 1. Clean the aerator

If only one faucet is slow, the aerator (the small screen at the spout tip) is almost always the cause.

1. Unscrew the aerator counterclockwise by hand. If it won't budge, wrap a rag around it and use channel-lock pliers gently.
2. Rinse the screen in vinegar to dissolve mineral buildup. Use a toothbrush for stubborn deposits.
3. Reinstall and test.

A clogged aerator can drop a single faucet's flow rate to 25% of normal. Five minutes, no cost.

### 2. Check shutoff valves

Under-sink shutoffs and main shutoff valves sometimes get bumped or partially closed during cleaning or pest-control visits.

- Under every slow sink, confirm both supply shutoffs are fully open (counterclockwise to the stop).
- At the main water valve (basement, crawl space, or near the front foundation), confirm it's fully open.
- At the water heater, confirm both cold-in and hot-out valves are fully open.

A partially-closed main valve is the most common cause of "the whole house got worse after we had work done last month."

### 3. Flush the water heater (hot-only issue)

If only hot water is low:

1. Turn the water heater to "off" or "pilot" (gas) or trip the breaker (electric).
2. Connect a hose to the drain valve at the bottom of the tank.
3. Open the drain valve and the T&P relief valve at the top.
4. Let water flow until it runs clear (often 5–15 minutes).
5. Close drain, close T&P, refill, restore power.

Sediment buildup can reduce hot-water flow noticeably. Annual flush is recommended; many homeowners go years without doing it.

## The mid-cost fixes (DIY-friendly or one plumber visit)

### 4. Replace a faucet cartridge

If one faucet has low pressure on BOTH hot and cold (not just one side), the cartridge is worn. $15–$40 part, 30-minute DIY. See our [faucet dripping guide](/advice/faucet-dripping) for the procedure — same steps.

### 5. Test the pressure regulator

The PRV (pressure-reducing valve) is mounted on the main water line where it enters the house. A failed PRV usually causes gradually declining whole-house pressure.

Test:
- Attach a pressure gauge to the closest outdoor hose bib.
- Read the pressure. Normal: 40–60 psi.
- If below 40 psi everywhere AND the issue developed over months → likely PRV failure.

Replacement: $300–$650 plumber labor + part. The PRV is upstream of the main shutoff so it's a real plumbing job.

### 6. Call the utility (rule it out for free)

Before assuming the problem is on your side:
- Call the water utility's customer service line.
- Ask: "Is there a known pressure issue in my area? Has anyone reported low pressure?"
- Ask them to test pressure at your meter on the next available visit (often free).

Sometimes the answer is "we're flushing mains this week" or "your meter is partially clogged." Free fixes are the best fixes.

## The expensive fixes (the bad news)

### 7. Galvanized pipe corrosion

If your house was built before 1960 and still has galvanized supply lines, internal corrosion is a real probability. Galvanized pipes corrode from the inside, narrowing the effective diameter over decades. Pressure decline is gradual (years), affects everything, and gets worse over time.

The fix is repipe — replacing all the galvanized lines with PEX or copper. $3,000–$8,000 for a typical house. Some repipe projects can be done in stages.

### 8. Service-line leak

A leak in the buried water line between the meter and the house drops whole-house pressure. To confirm:

- Close the house main shutoff.
- Watch the meter (the small flow indicator triangle/star).
- If the meter is still moving with the house main closed → service line leak.

See our [water leak detection guide](/advice/water-leak-detection) for the full test procedure. A confirmed service-line leak runs $1,500–$5,000 to repair depending on depth, length, and excavation requirements.

## When to call a plumber

- You've checked aerators, shutoffs, water heater drain, and the issue persists.
- Whole-house pressure has gradually declined over months.
- A pressure gauge confirms below 40 psi.
- Visible water on the lawn between the meter and house.

Expect a typical diagnostic visit in the [$150–$400 range](/costs/plumber-service-call) for the trip + diagnostic, plus repair cost depending on cause.
