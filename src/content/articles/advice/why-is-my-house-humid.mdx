**6 causes of high indoor humidity:**

1. **Oversized AC** (40% of cases in cooling season) — AC short-cycles, doesn't dehumidify.
2. **Unvented bathroom/kitchen** (20%) — bath fans dump into attic instead of outside, moisture returns.
3. **Plumbing leak** (10%) — slow leak adds gallons of moisture per day.
4. **Basement / crawl space moisture** (15%) — groundwater + condensation.
5. **Lifestyle sources** (10%) — drying laundry indoors, many houseplants, long hot showers, big aquariums.
6. **No AC + humid climate** (5%) — straightforward fix is dehumidification or AC.

## Step 1: Measure (do this first)

You can't fix what you don't measure. A $15 hygrometer (small digital meter) tells you actual RH room by room. Targets:

- **Below 30% RH** — too dry. Dry sinuses, static, wood shrinkage.
- **30–50% RH** — ideal range. CDC recommendation.
- **50–60% RH** — borderline. Watch for mold-vulnerable areas.
- **Above 60% RH** — high mold risk. Address now.

Check multiple rooms: living room, kitchen, basement, bedrooms, bathroom. Basements and bathrooms typically read 5–15 points higher than main floors.

## Cause #1: Oversized AC (most common in cooling season)

An oversized AC cools the air faster than it dehumidifies. Cooling capacity is rated in BTUs; dehumidification happens as a byproduct of running the cooling coil. If the AC reaches setpoint and shuts off in 5–8 minutes, it removed minimal moisture.

Symptoms:
- AC cycles on and off frequently (3–5 cycles per hour).
- House feels cool but muggy.
- Hygrometer reads 60%+ even with AC running.

**Fix:** confirm sizing with a Manual J load calculation. Many homes have AC units sized 20–40% larger than actual cooling load (often inherited from previous installs). Right-sizing on next replacement fixes this — usually means a smaller tonnage.

Short-term workaround: run AC with the fan set to AUTO (not ON). The fan stops between cycles, letting moisture drain off the coil instead of re-evaporating into the house.

## Cause #2: Unvented bathroom or kitchen

A bathroom fan that vents into the attic (not outside the house) creates a moisture loop — humidity rises into the attic, then migrates back into living space. Same for kitchen range hoods that recirculate instead of venting outside.

Check:
- Pull the bath-fan cover. Look at the duct exiting — does it go to an exterior wall or roof cap (correct) or just into the attic (wrong)?
- Same for the range hood. If it has a charcoal filter and no exterior duct, it doesn't actually remove moisture.

Fix: a venting upgrade typically costs $200–$600 — running new ductwork to an exterior cap. Major reduction in indoor humidity and a significant building-code/insurance compliance fix.

## Cause #3: Plumbing leak

A slow plumbing leak can add 50+ gallons of moisture into the structure per month. Common culprits:
- Slow pipe leak under a slab.
- Water heater seepage at the base.
- Toilet wax-ring failure.
- Slab-leak in concrete-slab homes.

The leak source may be far from where humidity manifests. Run the [water-meter leak test](/advice/water-leak-detection) to confirm. If positive, find and fix before any dehumidification investment.

## Cause #4: Basement / crawl space moisture

Below-grade spaces are the highest-risk humidity zones:

**Groundwater intrusion:**
- Foundation cracks letting water through.
- Soil grade sloping toward house (water pooling against foundation).
- Failed waterproofing membrane.
- Failed gutter or downspout dumping water near foundation.

**Condensation:**
- Warm humid outdoor air contacting cool basement surfaces (especially cold-water pipes and concrete walls).
- Worse in summer than winter for basements.

Fix:
1. Address obvious water intrusion first (regrading, gutter repair, foundation crack repair).
2. Run a 50+ pint/day dehumidifier in the basement during humid months.
3. For finished basements with persistent issues, install a whole-house dehumidifier integrated with HVAC.

## Cause #5: Lifestyle sources

Surprising amounts of moisture come from daily activities:

- **Cooking and dishwashing:** 1–3 gallons/day.
- **Showers and baths:** 1–2 gallons/day per person.
- **Drying laundry indoors:** 3–5 gallons per load.
- **Houseplants:** 0.5–1 gallon/day for a heavily-planted home.
- **Aquariums:** 0.2–0.5 gallon/day per 50 gallons of tank water.
- **Cooking pasta, soup, anything in open pots:** measurable.

Solutions:
- Run bath fan for 20 minutes after every shower (not just during).
- Don't dry laundry indoors in humid weather. Outside or dryer.
- Cover pots when boiling. Use range hood that vents outside.
- Use an exhaust fan timer to ensure proper run-time.

## Cause #6: No AC + humid climate

In humid summer climates (Southeast US, Gulf Coast, parts of Midwest) without AC, humidity can run 70–80% RH indoors with no obvious "cause" — it's just outdoor humidity making it inside.

Solutions:
- **Portable dehumidifier:** $200–$400 for a 40–50 pint/day unit. Covers a typical floor of a home.
- **Whole-house dehumidifier:** $1,500–$3,000 installed. Integrates with HVAC.
- **Window AC unit:** dehumidifies as a side effect. $200–$500 for a basic unit.
- **Central AC install:** if you don't have it and live in a humid market, the long-term answer.

## When to call a pro

- AC is short-cycling and you suspect sizing — HVAC contractor with Manual J capability.
- Persistent basement humidity above 60% with no obvious source — foundation/waterproofing contractor for groundwater issues.
- Whole-house humidity above 60% with AC running and you've ruled out the above — HVAC for refrigerant check or dehumidifier install.
- Visible mold anywhere — see [drywall damage after a leak](/advice/drywall-damage-after-a-leak) for remediation guidance.
