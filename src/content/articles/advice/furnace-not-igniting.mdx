A furnace that won't light is the most stressful winter home failure. The good news: most of the time, the cause is something a homeowner can fix in 5–15 minutes. Here are the checks in the right order, from cheapest and safest to most likely to need a pro.

<QuickAnswer>
The top 3 causes of a furnace not igniting, in order of frequency:

1. **Dirty flame sensor** — a 5-minute DIY clean with a piece of fine sandpaper resolves a surprising share of "won't ignite" calls.
2. **Tripped breaker, blown fuse, or thermostat issue** — confirm power, set thermostat to heat, replace battery if applicable.
3. **Failed hot-surface ignitor** — needs replacement ($150–$300 HVAC visit) on most modern furnaces.

Work through the safe checks below. If you smell gas at any point, **stop, ventilate, and call the gas utility** — not HVAC.
</QuickAnswer>

<SafetyWarning>
**Stop and call the gas utility immediately if:**
- You smell gas anywhere in or near the furnace area.
- You hear a sustained hissing sound from gas lines.
- You see soot or black streaking near the furnace.

Do not flip switches or use anything electrical (including phones) near a suspected gas leak. Leave the house, open doors as you go, and call the gas utility's 24-hour emergency line from outside. CO poisoning is odorless — a working CO detector on every floor of the house is non-negotiable with any gas appliance.
</SafetyWarning>

## The 7 checks, in order

### 1. Confirm power and thermostat

Before anything else:

- **Furnace power switch** (looks like a regular light switch, usually mounted on or near the furnace): confirm it's ON.
- **Breaker panel**: find the furnace breaker, confirm it hasn't tripped. If tripped, reset once. If it trips again immediately, stop — that's a wiring or motor issue requiring a pro.
- **Thermostat**: confirm it's set to HEAT (not OFF or COOL) and the target temp is above current room temp. If battery-powered, replace the battery.

About 25% of "furnace won't start" calls resolve at this step.

### 2. Check the air filter

A severely clogged filter restricts airflow enough to trip the high-limit safety switch. The furnace tries to ignite, can't complete the airflow check, and locks out.

- Pull the filter. If you can't see light through it, it's restricting airflow.
- Install a clean filter the right way (arrow toward the furnace blower).
- Reset the furnace: turn the power switch off, wait 30 seconds, turn it back on.

If clogged-filter lockout was the issue, the furnace should ignite within 1–2 cycles.

### 3. Confirm gas supply

If the furnace is gas-fueled:

- Check that the gas shut-off valve at the furnace is OPEN (handle parallel to the gas pipe).
- Confirm other gas appliances in the house still work (gas range, water heater). If they don't, the utility supply is the issue — call the gas utility.
- Look outside at the gas meter — if you see frost on it during cold weather, the regulator may be freezing (utility issue).

### 4. Clean the flame sensor

This is the most common 5-minute fix. The flame sensor is a small thin rod (often porcelain-and-metal) that sits in the burner area. When the burner ignites, the sensor's electrical resistance changes, telling the control board "flame is present." When the sensor is dirty (oxidation, soot, dust buildup), it can't read the flame, so the control board shuts off gas as a safety response.

**To clean:**
1. Turn off the furnace power switch.
2. Open the burner-area access panel (one or two screws).
3. Find the flame sensor — a small rod, usually with a wire attached, mounted near the burners.
4. Remove the screw holding it (often one Phillips screw).
5. Wipe the sensor rod with very fine sandpaper (400-grit or finer) or fine steel wool. Light pressure. You're removing oxide, not metal.
6. Wipe with a clean cloth.
7. Reinstall in the same orientation.
8. Replace the access panel.
9. Restore power.

The next ignition cycle should run cleanly. If it still fails, the sensor itself may need replacement ($30 part + 30-min HVAC labor) — but cleaning fixes most cases.

### 5. Check the condensate drain (95% AFUE furnaces only)

High-efficiency condensing furnaces produce acidic condensate that drains through a small PVC line. If that drain clogs, a float switch shuts down the furnace as a safety response.

- Find the condensate line where it leaves the furnace (usually a 3/4" PVC).
- Check if water is pooling anywhere along the line.
- If pooling, clear the clog: disconnect at the cleanout, blow through with a shop vac in reverse, or use compressed air.
- For persistent clogs, a thin wire or pipe cleaner often clears the drain.

### 6. Reset the furnace

Modern furnaces lock out after multiple failed ignition attempts as a safety feature. To reset:

- Turn off the furnace power switch (or thermostat).
- Wait 5 minutes.
- Turn it back on.
- Set the thermostat to call for heat.

Watch the sequence: blower should start the inducer motor, then ignitor should glow orange (visible through the inspection port on most furnaces), then gas should fire. If any step fails, that's diagnostic information for the HVAC tech.

### 7. Check the pressure switch tubing

The pressure switch confirms the inducer motor is creating proper draft before allowing ignition. A small rubber or vinyl tube connects the pressure switch to the inducer assembly. If that tube is kinked, cracked, or full of water, the switch can't read pressure and the furnace won't ignite.

- Find the pressure switch (small disc-shaped device with a thin tube).
- Visually inspect the tube — should be clear, kink-free, and not full of liquid.
- If the tube has water in it (common in condensing furnaces), disconnect and blow it out.

## When to stop and call an HVAC tech

After working through these checks, call if:

- The ignitor doesn't glow orange when the furnace tries to start (likely failed hot-surface ignitor — $150–$300 repair).
- The flame ignites then dies within 5–10 seconds (likely flame sensor or control board — $200–$500).
- You hear a clicking gas valve but no ignition attempt (likely failed ignitor or gas valve — $200–$700).
- The blower runs but the burner never tries to fire (likely control board or wiring — $300–$700).
- The furnace short-cycles repeatedly (multiple causes — diagnostic visit needed).

Expect a typical HVAC service call in the **$100–$250 range** for the trip plus diagnostic, plus repair cost. See our [HVAC service call cost guide](/costs/hvac-service-call-cost) for what's fair.

## When to replace instead of repair

For a furnace under 12 years old: repair anything reasonable.

For a furnace 15+ years old: any repair over $1,000 puts you in replacement territory. The next major failure is statistically close, and modern 95% AFUE units pay back the upgrade within 4–6 winters. See our [furnace replacement cost guide](/costs/furnace-replacement-cost) for what fair pricing looks like.

For any furnace failure right before winter peak: get an emergency repair if needed, but plan replacement for the off-season when demand pricing drops 15–25%.

## Carbon monoxide — the non-negotiable

Every home with a gas furnace needs working CO detectors on every floor. Test them twice a year. After any furnace work — whether you cleaned the flame sensor yourself or had an HVAC tech do a major repair — verify the CO detector batteries are fresh and the units have not expired (most CO detectors expire 7–10 years from manufacture date).

A furnace that ignites but vents poorly produces CO that can build up to fatal levels overnight. The detector is the last line of defense.
