A refrigerator leak is one of the most common slow water problems — and one of the most likely to go unnoticed until the floor under the fridge has been wet for weeks. The good news: most causes are cheap DIY fixes.

<QuickAnswer>
**5 causes of a refrigerator leaking water:**

1. **Clogged defrost drain** (45% of cases) — clear with warm water; $0 fix.
2. **Ice-maker supply line leak** (25%) — tighten connection or replace line; $5–$20 part.
3. **Cracked or overflowing drain pan** (10%) — empty / replace; $20 part.
4. **Water filter not seated** (10%) — push to click; $0 fix.
5. **Refrigerant or compressor leak** (10%) — service call, $200–$600.

**First action: shut off the water supply behind the fridge.** Move the fridge out, pull the drip tray, and identify where the water is coming from.
</QuickAnswer>

## First steps (5 minutes)

1. **Shut off the water supply** to the fridge. Look behind it for a small shutoff valve on the water line. Turn perpendicular to close. This stops the active leak.
2. **Turn off the ice maker** at the switch (inside the freezer for most models).
3. **Pull the fridge out** about 12 inches from the wall. Look at:
   - The floor under the fridge — wet from defrost drain or appliance pan?
   - The water connection at the back — wet at the fitting?
   - The fill tube to the ice maker (top-rear of freezer) — wet/cracked?

## Fix #1: Defrost drain clog (most common)

Modern refrigerators defrost the cooling coil periodically. Water from the defrost cycle drips into a small drain at the back of the freezer floor, runs through a tube to a drain pan at the bottom of the fridge (under the compressor), and evaporates from the pan into the air.

When the defrost drain clogs (ice, food debris, mineral buildup), water backs up and overflows — often appearing on the kitchen floor in front of the fridge.

**Fix:**
1. Empty the freezer.
2. Find the drain opening — usually a small hole at the rear of the freezer floor or under the bottom shelf.
3. Melt any ice plug with warm water (turkey baster or syringe).
4. Push a pipe cleaner or wire (from a coat hanger) through to clear debris.
5. Pour warm water through to confirm flow.
6. Check the drain pan under the fridge — empty if full.
7. Restart the fridge.

If the clog returns within weeks, the defrost drain heater (a small element that prevents freezing) may have failed. That's a service call.

## Fix #2: Ice-maker supply line

The water line that feeds the ice maker comes in at the back of the fridge — usually a 1/4-inch line that connects to a saddle valve or proper shutoff valve.

Common failure points:
- **Compression fitting** at the back: loose. Tighten with a small wrench.
- **Fill tube** inside the freezer: cracked from age or ice damage. $15 part, 30-min DIY.
- **Plastic water line** itself: cracked. Replace the section. $20 for 25 ft of new line.
- **Saddle valve** (old install style): leaks at the pierce point. Replace with proper 1/4-turn valve. $25 part + plumber labor.

**Pro tip:** if your ice maker connection uses a "saddle valve" (pierces the cold water pipe to tap into it), retrofit to a proper compression-tee. Saddle valves are unreliable and many municipal codes prohibit new installs.

## Fix #3: Drain pan

The drain pan sits under the fridge at the bottom (often accessible by removing the kickplate at the front-bottom of the unit). Two issues:

- **Overflowing pan** — the defrost drain has been working, but the pan can't evaporate as fast as water comes in. Empty manually. If it refills quickly without obvious reason, the compressor or condenser fan may not be running (which heats the pan for evaporation).
- **Cracked pan** — pull and inspect. $20 for replacement, 10-min swap.

## Fix #4: Water filter

The water filter (for fridges with ice/water dispensers) sits inside the fridge — usually in the upper-right corner, the kickplate, or the door.

Leak causes:
- **Not seated fully** — push the filter in firmly until it clicks. About 30% of "leaking filter" calls are this.
- **Wrong filter** — third-party filters with bad gaskets, or wrong model number. Use OEM or known-good aftermarket only.
- **Damaged O-ring** — pull the filter, inspect the O-rings. Swap if cut or hardened.

## Fix #5: Compressor / refrigerant leak

If water is coming from the back near the compressor (not from the freezer drain or supply line), this is a refrigerant-system issue — refrigerant lubricant mixed with condensate, leaking from a failing seal or component.

This is appliance-repair work, not DIY. Cost: $200–$600 typical, depending on which component. For a fridge over 12 years old, replacement may be cheaper than a major refrigerant repair.

## When to call a pro

- You've cleared the defrost drain, verified the water filter, and tightened all visible connections, but the leak continues.
- The leak is at the compressor area, with oil/refrigerant present.
- The fridge isn't cooling well in addition to leaking.
- The flooring under the fridge is warped or moldy — see [drywall damage after a leak](/advice/drywall-damage-after-a-leak) for what to do about the secondary damage.

Appliance repair typical visit: $100–$200 trip + diagnostic, plus repair cost.

## Preventing future leaks

- **Annual defrost-drain check.** Pour a cup of warm water through the drain once a year to keep it clear.
- **Replace the water filter on schedule** (6 months typical). Old filters with degraded O-rings leak.
- **Inspect the ice-maker supply line annually.** Look for kinks, leaks, frosted-over connections.
- **Move the fridge out and clean under it** every year. Builds up dust on the compressor and can hide a slow leak you'd otherwise see.
