If an outlet is smoking: **kill the circuit breaker for that outlet immediately**, then unplug whatever was plugged in (if you can do it safely without contact with the outlet), and **call an electrician — not a handyman — for the repair**. If you see open flame, evacuate and call 911. **Never use water on an electrical fire.** A smoking outlet is one of the most common precursors to residential electrical fires; treat it like the emergency it is, not a "I'll deal with it later" problem.

## Do this immediately

**1. Kill the breaker.**
Go to the electrical panel. Find the breaker for that room or that outlet (panels are usually labeled, even if poorly). Flip it OFF. If you can't identify the correct breaker quickly, **kill the main breaker** at the top of the panel — this kills power to the entire house, which is the right move under uncertainty.

**2. Evacuate if there's flame.**
A small wisp of smoke is one thing; visible flame is another. If flame is visible: **get everyone out, close the door, call 911**. Do not try to extinguish anything bigger than a small visible flame yourself.

**3. Don't use water.**
Water conducts electricity. Even if the breaker is off, residual current and the live wires in the wall make water dangerous. If you need to extinguish a small flame: **Class C or ABC fire extinguisher**. Baking soda works as a last resort for very small flames.

**4. Unplug what was plugged in.**
Only after the breaker is off. Use a dry hand, dry shoes, no contact with the outlet itself. Some plugs come out easily; some have melted to the outlet — don't force.

**5. Ventilate.**
Open windows. Burning electrical insulation produces toxic smoke (PVC, fire-retardants); get it out of the house.

**6. Call an electrician.**
This is not a DIY repair. The outlet itself needs to be inspected — and replaced if damaged. The wiring inside the box needs to be inspected. The breaker should be tested. The circuit may need a load assessment. Expect $150-$400 for the inspection and outlet replacement; more if the wiring inside the wall is damaged. ## What this usually means

The most common cause is a **loose wire connection inside the outlet box**. Wire connections that are slightly loose generate heat under load. Over months or years, that heat degrades the insulation, melts the plastic, and eventually ignites. The smoke is the early warning.

Other causes:

- **Backstabbed outlets failing.** Older outlets (1980s-1990s) often use push-in "backstab" connections that loosen over time. Modern code prefers screw-terminal connections.
- **Overloaded circuit.** Too many high-draw devices on one circuit (space heater + microwave + hair dryer) can overheat the weakest connection point. The outlet at that connection is the first to fail.
- **Water damage.** Moisture in the outlet box (from a leak above, a tub overflow, a vapor source) corrodes the connections and creates arcing.
- **Failing appliance.** Sometimes the smoke originates not from the outlet itself but from a failing device. Pull the plug; if the outlet face is unharmed, the appliance is the source.

## Common mistakes

- **Trying to replace the outlet yourself before an electrician inspects the wiring.** The visible damage at the outlet is the symptom, not the disease. A burnt outlet with intact wiring is one repair; a burnt outlet with damaged wire inside the wall is a much bigger repair.
- **Turning the breaker back on to "test" before inspection.** If the failure was an arcing connection, the next time it draws load, it can ignite. Don't restore power until an electrician has cleared it.
- **Ignoring the warning.** A single smoking event without follow-up is gambling. Most electrical fires have warning signs (smoke, burning smell, intermittent issues) hours-to-weeks before ignition.

## Red flags after the incident

- **An electrician suggests replacing just the outlet without inspecting the wiring or testing the breaker.** Should at minimum: confirm wiring inside the box is intact, test the breaker for proper trip, evaluate the circuit's load.
- **Anyone telling you to "just keep the breaker off and use other outlets."** The breaker is off for a reason; using the same circuit at a different outlet doesn't fix the underlying connection failure if it's in the wire upstream.
- **A quote dramatically below market.** $50 for an "outlet replacement after a smoke incident" suggests they're not actually inspecting anything.

## What I'd do

Kill the breaker first, evacuate if there's flame, call an electrician for a same-day or next-day visit. Don't use the circuit until cleared. If multiple outlets on the same circuit have shown signs of failure (warm to touch, sparking when plugging things in, occasional smoke), the whole circuit may need new wiring — that's $400-$1,500 of work depending on accessibility, but cheaper than a fire.

After any electrical smoke incident, check that the home's smoke alarms are working (test button) and less than 10 years old. See [smoke detector replacement cost](/costs/smoke-detector-replacement) and [Should I replace my own smoke detector?](/diy-or-hire/smoke-detector).
