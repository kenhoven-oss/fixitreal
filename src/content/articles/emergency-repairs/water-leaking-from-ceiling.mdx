If water is actively leaking from your ceiling: **shut off the water main**, move valuables away, place a bucket, and **poke a small hole at the lowest point of any drywall bulge** to drain water in a controlled way. A bulging ceiling will collapse — a controlled drain is dramatically less destructive than letting it fall. Then document everything (photos, videos, moisture readings) and call your insurer within 24-48 hours. Most "sudden and accidental" water damage is covered by standard homeowners insurance.

## The first 10 minutes

**Minute 1: Shut off the water main.** If you don't know where it is, look for it where the main supply enters the house — typically near the front foundation, the basement wall facing the street, or in a utility closet. Turn the valve clockwise (lever-handle: 90 degrees perpendicular to the pipe). If it's a gate valve and won't turn, don't force it — gate valves seize. Move to the meter shut-off outside.

**Minute 2: Kill power to the affected area** if water is reaching outlets, light fixtures, or the panel itself. Trip the breaker for the room. If water is near the panel itself, kill the main breaker.

**Minute 3–4: Move valuables.** Electronics, papers, photos, anything that can be moved fast. Cover larger furniture with plastic sheeting or trash bags. Anything wood will warp; anything fabric will mold within 24-48 hours.

**Minute 5: Place containers and drain the bulge.** If the ceiling drywall is bulging, that's pooled water above. Position a bucket. Use an awl, small drill bit, or stiff coat hanger to poke a small hole at the lowest point of the bulge. Water drains in a controlled stream into the bucket. The drywall stays intact for the repair contractor.

**Minute 6-7: Find the source.** Go to the floor above. Common causes (in rough order of frequency):

- Overflowing fixture (toilet, sink, tub left running)
- Burst supply line under a sink, behind a toilet, or to an appliance
- Failed shutoff valve
- Tub or shower pan leak (slow; usually shows up after a shower)
- Roof leak after heavy rain
- Water heater failure
- Frozen and burst pipe (winter)

If the source is obvious and contained, shut off that fixture's local valve and you can usually restore the main.

**Minute 8: Document.** Take 20+ photos before any cleanup. The bulge, the drip pattern, the floor above, any visible source. Video walking through the scene. Note the time. Insurance adjusters use this evidence; your contractor uses it for scope.

**Minute 9: Call insurance.** Most policies have notification windows (often 72 hours). Call the claim line, get a claim number, ask if they want an adjuster on-site before any work starts. Some require it; some don't.

**Minute 10: Call a plumber.** If the source isn't obvious or shut-off didn't stop the flow. For active leaks, ask for emergency service — expect 1.5–2× standard rates (typical emergency-trip $200–$400, plus hourly). ## Common mistakes

- **Letting the bulge collapse on its own.** Almost always worse than draining it. Don't wait.
- **Removing the ceiling drywall yourself.** Some insurance policies require an adjuster or contractor to assess before tear-out. Hold off on demolition until you talk to the insurer.
- **Drying things with a hair dryer.** Underpowered and creates fire risk near wet drywall. Open windows, run fans on high, set up dehumidifiers.
- **Skipping the moisture reading.** A contractor with a moisture meter (see our [moisture meter guide](/tools/best-moisture-meters-for-homeowners)) can tell you what's wet vs. just stained. Hidden wetness becomes mold.
- **Throwing away wet drywall before photos.** Documentation is your claim leverage.

## Red flags

- **A contractor wants to start tear-out before insurance has been called.** Almost always a sign of a "storm-chaser" operation skirting the adjuster process.
- **A water-damage company asks you to assign benefits ("AOB") of your insurance claim.** AOB scams are common in water-damage work — sign nothing without reading carefully and ideally without your insurance agent in the loop.
- **A pro who can't produce a license number or proof of insurance.** Water remediation requires specific certifications (IICRC). Verify on the IICRC website before hiring.

## What I'd do

For most ceilings: drain the bulge through a small hole, set up fans and a dehumidifier (rentable for $30-$50/day at any hardware store), document everything, call insurance, and call a plumber. Don't tear out drywall yourself unless the insurer specifically tells you to. The repair scope depends on whether the water hit electrical and how much absorbed into framing — the moisture meter reading tells your contractor that.
