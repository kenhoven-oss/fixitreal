When a pipe bursts: **shut off the water main, kill power if water is near electrical, open faucets to drain remaining water in the lines, place buckets and start moving valuables, then document everything before any cleanup.** A typical household water supply pushes 250+ gallons per hour through even a small crack — the first 10 minutes contains an order of magnitude more damage than the next hour will.

## The first 10 minutes

**Minute 1: Find and close the main shut-off.**

- Inside main: usually basement or crawl space, on the wall facing the street, near the front of the house. Turn clockwise.
- Outside main / meter: typically at the curb in a concrete vault. Needs a water key (or sometimes a long screwdriver) to operate. Pry off the lid; reach the valve with the key.

If the shut-off is seized, skip to the outside meter. If neither works, call the utility&apos;s 24-hour emergency line.

**Minute 2: Kill power if water is near electrical.**

If water has reached outlets, light fixtures, the water heater, or the panel: trip the affected breakers or kill the main breaker. **Don&apos;t stand in water while doing this.** If you can&apos;t safely reach the panel, call the fire department — they have tools to safely disconnect power.

**Minute 3-4: Drain the supply lines.**

Open the highest faucet on the top floor first (hot and cold). Then a faucet on the lowest floor. Open the laundry-tub or hose-bib in the basement if you have one. This relieves pressure in the pipes and stops the residual water from continuing to dump out of the break.

Flush every toilet once after closing the main — the tank water drains into the bowl, lowering the system pressure further.

**Minute 5: Find the break.**

Most household burst-pipe locations:

- **Behind washing machine** (rubber hoses, very common, especially after 5-7 years)
- **Under sinks** (supply lines to faucets — braided stainless is more reliable than the old rubber ones)
- **Behind toilets** (supply line to the tank)
- **In the basement / crawl space** (exposed pipes in cold areas)
- **In an exterior wall** (frozen pipe — winter)
- **At the water heater** (failed connection, failed tank)

Visible breaks: dripping or spraying. Hidden breaks: water on floors with no obvious source above; the sound of running water with no fixtures open.

**Minute 6-7: Contain the water.**

Buckets, towels, mops. Run wet-dry vacs if you have them. Pull rugs away. Block water from rooms that aren&apos;t yet wet — towels or rolled blankets across thresholds work.

**Minute 8-9: Document.**

Photos and video before any cleanup. Note the time. Capture the break, the water spread, every wet wall, the affected area. This is your insurance evidence.

**Minute 10: Make calls.**

1. **Plumber** for emergency service. Most charge 1.5-2× rates after hours. Typical emergency callout $200-$500 trip + hourly. See our [plumber service call cost guide](/costs/plumber-service-call).
2. **Insurance company** within 24-48 hours. Get a claim number. Ask if an adjuster needs to be on-site before water-mitigation work starts.
3. **Water-mitigation company** if there&apos;s significant water (rugs soaked, drywall wet, water in finished spaces). They have industrial dehumidifiers, fans, and moisture meters. Hourly rate $100-$200; daily equipment rental $30-$100.

## Common mistakes

- **Don&apos;t flip breakers while standing in water.** Use a flashlight from a dry area; reach the panel via a dry path or kill the main from outside.
- **Don&apos;t skip the documentation step.** &ldquo;I&apos;ll get to the photos later&rdquo; means losing claim leverage. Photos take 2 minutes.
- **Don&apos;t pull up flooring or remove drywall before insurance is contacted.** Some policies require an adjuster to assess before any tear-out.
- **Don&apos;t throw away the broken pipe section.** It&apos;s evidence — sometimes the insurer wants to inspect it.
- **Don&apos;t sign an "Assignment of Benefits" form** from a water-mitigation company without reading carefully. AOB scams are a recurring problem in water-damage work.

## Red flags

- **A water-mitigation company shows up unsolicited.** Ask how they knew about the leak. Some monitor police/fire scanner traffic or pay neighborhood tipsters. Legitimate companies are referred by your insurance, your plumber, or your search.
- **Demands for upfront payment beyond a normal deposit.** Standard deposit is 10-33% of the project. More than that on an emergency job is a leverage play.
- **Refusal to itemize the invoice.** Equipment rental, hours, materials, disposal should each have line items.

## What I'd do

Practice locating the main shut-off and the meter shut-off in daylight, before any emergency. Test the valve twice a year to ensure it still turns smoothly. Replace any rubber washing-machine hoses with braided stainless steel ($25-$40, 30-minute swap). Install an inexpensive leak-detection valve at the water heater ($150-$300 installed; some shut off the water automatically when they sense a leak).

For a burst-pipe event: shut off the main, kill the affected breakers, drain the system, document, call. The first 10 minutes is mostly about stopping flow and capturing evidence — the actual repair starts with the contractors you call after.
