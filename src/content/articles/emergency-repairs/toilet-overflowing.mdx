If your toilet is about to overflow: **shut off the supply valve at the base of the toilet** (small valve on the wall behind it, turn clockwise), then **lift the tank lid and push the flapper down with your hand** to immediately stop water flowing into the bowl. Those two moves contain the overflow before it reaches the floor. Then deal with the underlying clog with a plunger or toilet auger.

## The two moves that stop the overflow

**Move 1: Close the supply valve.**
On the wall behind or beside the toilet, near the floor, there&apos;s a small chrome or plastic valve where the water supply line enters the wall. Turn it **clockwise** until it stops. This kills water flowing into the tank.

**Move 2: Lift the tank lid, push the flapper closed.**
Inside the tank, the flapper (rubber disk at the bottom) is what releases water into the bowl when you flush. If it&apos;s open and water in the tank is draining into the already-overflowing bowl, push it down with your hand. The bowl stops getting fuller. (Wash hands after — tank water is clean, but you&apos;ll feel better.)

These two moves take 30 seconds and prevent the bulk of the floor damage.

## Then deal with the clog

With the bowl stopped from rising:

1. **Wait 10-15 minutes.** Some of the water in the bowl will drain on its own as the clog softens. If the bowl level drops significantly, the clog is partial.
2. **Plunge with a flange plunger.** The flange (the rubber sleeve that extends past the bell) seals against the toilet drain. Plunge with steady, firm strokes — 10-15 plunges usually clears a typical clog.
3. **If plunging fails: toilet auger.** A toilet auger (~$30) is a 3-foot flexible cable with a rubber sleeve to protect the porcelain. Feed it into the drain, crank the handle, retrieve and flush. See [our drain snake buying guide](/tools/best-drain-snakes-for-homeowners).
4. **If the auger fails: plumber.** $130-$280 typical for a snake-out service call. See [plumber service call cost guide](/costs/plumber-service-call).

## When it&apos;s a bigger problem than the toilet

If closing the supply valve and pushing the flapper closed **doesn&apos;t stop the water rising in the bowl**, the problem isn&apos;t the toilet — it&apos;s the drain line backing up from further downstream.

Signs of a main-line clog:

- Water rises in the toilet bowl with no flushing going on
- Bubbles in the toilet when running the kitchen sink or shower
- Sewer smell in the bathroom (see [sewer smell in bathroom: what to check](/advice/sewer-smell-in-bathroom) — TBD)
- Water backing up at the lowest drain in the house (basement floor drain, basement shower) when other fixtures are used

**Stop using all plumbing in the house** until a plumber inspects. A backed-up main floods multiple fixtures fast and creates a contamination event that&apos;s harder to remediate than a single toilet overflow.

## Common mistakes

- **Continuing to flush the clogged toilet.** Each flush adds another 1.6 gallons of water with nowhere to go.
- **Using chemical drain cleaner in the toilet.** Liquid chemical drain cleaners (Drano, Liquid-Plumr) damage toilet seals over time and don&apos;t work well on toilet clogs (which are usually in the trap, not in the drain line below).
- **Plunging too hard right away.** Splashes contaminated water onto the floor and walls. Start gentle, build force as you confirm the seal is good.
- **Skipping the clean-up disinfection.** Bowl water is technically wastewater. Bleach-water or commercial disinfectant on any surface that got splashed.

## Red flags

- **Plumber tries to upsell you to a "full sewer line inspection" before doing the basic clearing first.** A typical clog 90% of the time clears with an auger or basic snake — $130-$280. Sewer-line camera inspections ($250-$500) are appropriate when there&apos;s recurring clogging or main-line evidence, not the first time you call about a toilet.
- **Quote includes "haul-away" or "disposal" fees over $50** for a basic toilet snake-out. There&apos;s nothing to haul.
- **Refusal to itemize the invoice.** Trip fee, labor, materials should each be listed.

## What I'd do

Buy a flange plunger ($15-$25) and a toilet auger ($25-$40) — keep both in the bathroom closet. The plunger handles 80% of clogs in under 10 minutes; the auger handles another 15%. Calling a plumber for the remaining 5% is fine, but doing the easy fixes yourself saves $150-$250 on the routine ones.

For homes with recurring toilet clogs (more than 2-3 per year on the same toilet), the cause is usually low-flow toilets that aren&apos;t getting enough gallons-per-flush to clear waste fully, or the wax ring / flange is leaking and the drain doesn&apos;t pull water out efficiently. Both are repairable; see our [toilet leaking at the base](/advice/toilet-leaking-at-the-base) and [Should I fix a leaking toilet myself?](/diy-or-hire/toilet) guides.
