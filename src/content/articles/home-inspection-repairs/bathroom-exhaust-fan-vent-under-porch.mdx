A bathroom exhaust fan venting into the cavity above a covered porch is a moisture problem waiting to become a mold problem. The porch roof traps the warm, humid exhaust against the porch ceiling, which over time rots the ceiling, peels the paint, and grows mold in the framing. The fix is straightforward — re-route the duct so it terminates to free outside air through an exterior wall cap or roof cap. Cost is typically $300–$1,200 depending on the route.

## Why it's a problem

A bathroom exhaust fan moves about 50–110 cubic feet per minute of warm, moisture-laden air every time you shower. The whole point of the fan is to get that moisture **outside the building envelope** so it can't condense on framing, drywall, or insulation.

When the duct terminates inside a porch ceiling cavity or under a porch soffit:

- The exhaust hits the underside of the porch roof and can't escape upward.
- It drifts back along the soffit, condensing wherever it cools.
- Some of it migrates back into the house through gaps in the soffit-to-wall transition.
- Some of it stays in the porch-ceiling cavity, where it grows mold on the framing and drywall over months and years.

The first visible sign is usually **stained or peeling paint on the porch ceiling**, sometimes a faint mildew smell on the porch in summer. By the time it shows, there's usually framing damage and active mold inside the cavity.

## What the code actually says

IRC M1505 (the model code most U.S. states adopt) requires mechanical exhaust to terminate to the outside. "Outside" specifically means free air — the duct can't dump into any enclosed building cavity, including attics, soffits, or porch ceilings, even if those cavities have some external venting.

Some jurisdictions allow termination into a properly **ventilated** soffit using a dedicated soffit vent cap, but a porch roof is rarely a true ventilated soffit — it's an enclosed roof cavity. Check your local amendments before assuming this is allowed.

## The fix

Three options, in order of preference:

**1. Re-route through the exterior wall.**
Cleanest fix. Run the duct from the fan housing to the nearest exterior wall and terminate through an exterior wall cap with a damper. Cost: $300–$700, including a handyman or HVAC tech, parts, and patching drywall.

- Pros: No roof penetration. Easy to inspect and maintain. Wall caps are cheap to replace if they get damaged.
- Cons: Sometimes requires running duct through a joist bay; longer duct runs reduce fan efficiency.

**2. Re-route up through the main roof.**
Run the duct up through the roof and terminate at a proper roof vent cap with a damper, on the main roof (not the porch roof). Cost: $500–$1,200, including a roofer for the penetration.

- Pros: Short, straight duct runs work better than long horizontal ones. The vent is well above the porch ceiling, so no recirculation.
- Cons: Roof penetration introduces leak risk if poorly flashed. Need a roofer who'll warranty the flashing.

**3. Re-route through the gable wall.**
If the bathroom is near a gable end, this is sometimes the easiest run. Same cost range as option 1.

## What not to do

- **Don't extend the duct further into the soffit.** A longer duct dumping into the same trapped cavity is the same problem with more parts.
- **Don't terminate into the attic.** Some installers do this in finished basements or single-story homes — it's a code violation and a guaranteed moisture problem.
- **Don't use a flex-duct run with sags.** Sags collect condensate, which drips back into the fan housing and stains the ceiling. Use insulated rigid or semi-rigid duct, sloped slightly downward toward the exterior so any condensate drains out.

## When it's safe to DIY

A re-route through an exterior wall is well within DIY range for someone comfortable cutting drywall and an exterior wall. Skill ceiling: you need to cut a clean hole in siding (vinyl, wood, fiber-cement, or stucco), seal the penetration properly to keep water out, and finish the wall cap so it sheds water and seals against pests.

For roof penetrations, hire a roofer for the cut and flashing. Roof leaks from amateur penetrations are an order of magnitude more expensive to fix than just paying the roofer up front.

## When to hire a pro

- Roof penetrations.
- Anywhere you'd be cutting a siding type you're not comfortable with (stucco, fiber-cement).
- Long, twisty duct runs that need new framing or joist drilling.
- Bath fans where you also need to upgrade the fan (the old fan is undersized, noisy, or doesn't have a damper).

## On an inspection report

If you're a seller and this is on your repair request: it's a legitimate ask. Re-routing the vent is cheap relative to what the moisture damage costs if it's left. Easier to spend $500 now than $5,000 on porch-ceiling repairs after the next owner sells.

If you're a buyer: this is one of the items where you almost certainly want the work done by your contractor, not the seller's. A bad re-route bakes the same problem into the wall. Ask for a credit and hire your own HVAC tech after closing.
