Sellers have to fix a much shorter list of inspection items than buyers typically ask for. The genuinely must-fix list is: **lender-required safety and habitability items** flagged on the appraisal, **anything written into the signed purchase agreement**, and in some states **smoke detectors and CO alarms**. Everything else — including most of what shows up on a typical inspection report — is purely negotiable. Sellers can refuse, counter, or offer a credit instead.

## The genuinely required list

These items the seller fixes, or the deal doesn't close:

**Lender minimum property requirements (FHA/VA loans, sometimes conventional).**
The appraiser inspects for these alongside their valuation work. If they fail, the loan can't fund until they're cured.

- **FHA** (HUD Handbook 4000.1): no exposed wiring, working heat, hot water, functioning roof (no active leaks), no major structural damage, no peeling paint on pre-1978 homes (lead concern), missing handrails on stairs with 4+ risers, missing smoke alarms.
- **VA** (similar but slightly stricter): everything FHA requires, plus a clean termite report in much of the South, working sewer or septic, safe drinking water.
- **Conventional** (Fannie/Freddie): looser, but appraisers still flag obvious safety issues.

**Items written into the purchase agreement.**
If the contract says "seller to replace water heater before closing," that's contractually binding regardless of whether the inspection turned up a problem.

**State-mandated safety equipment.**
Many states require working smoke alarms in every required location at the point of sale. Some also require CO detectors, water-heater earthquake straps (CA), or fire-extinguisher placement. Check your state.

**Known material defects under the seller disclosure form.**
Items the seller knew about and didn't disclose can become legal liability. The fix isn't literally required — disclosure is — but most sellers fix rather than disclose and have the price drop instead.

## What buyers typically ask for that isn't required

A typical inspection report has 30–80 line items. The repair request usually picks 5–15. Most of those aren't legally required to fix. Common asks the seller can refuse:

- **Cosmetic items** — paint, carpet, drywall scuffs, tile cracks, scuffed flooring.
- **Worn-but-working items** — old water heaters, old HVAC, old appliances. "Past useful life" isn't broken.
- **Minor electrical findings** — outlets without GFCI in older homes (grandfathered), aluminum branch wiring (mitigation, not replacement, is usually fine), older but functional panels.
- **Plumbing nits** — slow drains, minor faucet drips, sluggish toilet flush.
- **Insulation upgrades** — under-insulated attics aren't a defect, they're a different building era.
- **Tree trimming, gutter cleaning, dryer-vent cleaning** — routine maintenance, not seller obligations.

Buyers asking for these are often overshooting. Sellers refusing them politely is reasonable.

## The borderline list

These come up often and can go either way. Most successful seller responses agree to fix some, offer credit on others, refuse the rest.

- **GFCI outlets in kitchens/bathrooms** — usually code now, but grandfathered in older homes. Fair ask; usually a cheap fix; sellers often agree.
- **Missing handrails on shorter staircases** — required new construction, grandfathered older. Reasonable to install; usually $100–$300.
- **Older roof** — if there's no active leak, it's a price-and-credit conversation, not a forced repair.
- **End-of-life water heater** — if it works, the seller can refuse to replace. A credit is fair if it's clearly past 12+ years.
- **Settled or cracked concrete (walks, patios, driveways)** — almost never a forced repair unless it's a trip hazard at an entry.

## The must-not-refuse list

Items a seller is technically not legally required to fix but should fix anyway, because refusing them ends the deal or invites legal trouble:

- **Active water leaks** — even small ones. Refusing to fix an active leak signals the seller is hiding bigger issues.
- **Gas leaks** — small ones included. Safety item; fix it.
- **Mold of any visible quantity** — fix and disclose. Don't fight this one.
- **Exposed live wiring or knob-and-tube in contact with insulation** — fire hazard. Fix.
- **Anything the seller disclosed pre-inspection** — refusing to fix something you previously admitted was broken makes the disclosure look like a stall.

## What I'd do

If you're selling: get a copy of the lender-required list **before** the inspection. Knowing what the appraiser will flag in advance means no surprises. Address the lender items proactively — they're going to come up anyway, and pre-fixing puts you in control of the contractor pick.

For the buyer's repair request: sort line items into must-fix (lender required, active hazards), should-fix (active leaks, known defects), and won't-fix (cosmetic, end-of-life-but-working, maintenance). Counter with credits on the should-fix items rather than managing repairs yourself — see [repair credit vs. fix before closing](/home-inspection-repairs/repair-credit-vs-fix-before-closing).

If you're buying: lead with safety items, follow with active-leak items, drop cosmetics from the ask. A focused list that targets real problems gets more yeses than a kitchen-sink list that includes carpet wear.
