For most inspection-triggered repairs, a closing credit beats fixing things pre-closing — for both sides. The buyer gets to pick the contractor, control quality, and skip the seller's rushed bargain-fix. The seller closes on time without project-managing repairs during a move. The exception: items the lender requires before funding the loan. Those have to be completed pre-closing, regardless of who pays.

## Why a credit usually wins for the buyer

When the seller hires the repair work, the buyer gets:

- **The cheapest contractor** the seller can find — not the best one.
- **The narrowest fix** that technically addresses the line item — not the actual underlying problem.
- **No warranty** or recourse if it fails six months in. The seller is long gone.
- **No control** over scheduling, materials, or workmanship.

When the buyer takes a credit and hires their own contractor after closing:

- **The contractor reports to them.** Quality matches their standards, not the seller's budget.
- **They can scope creep.** Found a related issue mid-repair? Easy to expand the job.
- **They pick when.** Sometimes after they're moved in, not in the chaotic final week before closing.
- **The contractor warranty** is in their name, not the previous owner's.

## Why a credit usually wins for the seller

- **No coordinating contractors during a move.** That alone is worth a few hundred dollars.
- **No back-and-forth on workmanship.** Once the credit's issued, the seller is done.
- **No risk the work isn't ready by walkthrough.** Failed repairs at walkthrough delay closing.
- **The cash leaves the closing table once, cleanly,** instead of through a series of contractor checks.

The seller's net is almost the same either way — sometimes a bit lower with a credit, because buyers negotiate slightly bigger credits than the seller's contractor would have charged. But the time, stress, and risk savings usually outweigh the small dollar gap.

## When the seller should fix instead

A few cases push toward pre-closing repairs:

- **FHA, VA, or USDA loans** with appraisal-flagged safety or habitability items. The lender requires the work done before funding — no credit substitution allowed.
- **Active leaks** that'll do progressive damage between now and closing. Letting a roof leak run another three weeks while paperwork moves is unwise even with a credit lined up.
- **Items affecting the appraisal value** — sometimes the appraisal is conditioned on repairs being complete. Talk to the lender before assuming a credit works.
- **Buyers who specifically refuse cash credits.** Some buyers prefer the seller to handle it. Rare, but it happens.

## How big should the credit be?

A fair credit is usually **the buyer's contractor estimate plus a small contingency** — not the seller's discount-contractor estimate. The buyer is taking on the project-management risk; they should be compensated for it.

Practical math: get one or two contractor quotes for the repair from the buyer side, add 10–20% for unknowns, that's the credit. If the seller can't get comfortable with that number, they can do the work themselves at their preferred price.

## Concession limits to know

Lenders cap how much credit a seller can give:

| Loan type | Seller concession limit |
| --- | --- |
| Conventional, < 10% down | 3% |
| Conventional, 10–25% down | 6% |
| Conventional, 25%+ down | 9% |
| FHA | 6% |
| VA | No cap on concessions, but limits on what they can cover |
| USDA | 6% |

The credit also has to cover actual closing costs and prepaids — buyers can't pocket leftover credit at closing. If your repair credit is bigger than the buyer's closing-cost capacity, the excess has to come off the price instead.

## When a price reduction beats a credit

If the buyer is already at their cash limit for closing — putting down their full down payment, no room for closing-cost credit — a price reduction is better. It lowers their loan amount, monthly payment, and total interest, and avoids the concession-limit headache.

For a buyer with cash to spare, a credit is more useful because it leaves them with liquid cash post-closing.

## What I'd do

If I'm the seller and the buyer asks for repairs: counter with a credit before agreeing to any work I have to manage. Frame it as "we'll issue a $X closing credit toward buyer's repairs in lieu of pre-closing work." That puts the buyer in control of quality and timing, gets me out of the contractor-management business, and almost always saves both sides money and headaches.

If I'm the buyer: ask for a credit on everything except lender-required items. Even when the seller volunteers to fix something, the seller's contractor is rarely the one you'd hire.
