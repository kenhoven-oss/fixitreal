Yes — in almost every U.S. real-estate contract, a seller can refuse buyer repair requests after the inspection. The inspection report itself doesn&apos;t obligate the seller to fix anything. The buyer&apos;s repair request is a negotiating ask, and the seller can accept, counter, or decline it. The only items a seller can&apos;t refuse are lender-required ones (FHA/VA safety conditions, structural issues that block the appraisal) and anything specifically written into the signed purchase agreement.

## What sellers can refuse — and usually should

The inspection report flags every minor defect the inspector sees in three hours. Most don&apos;t belong on a repair request. Seller-safe refusals include:

- **Cosmetic issues** — scuffed paint, worn carpet, minor drywall dings, hairline tile cracks.
- **Routine maintenance items** — clean the gutters, change the furnace filter, caulk the tub.
- **End-of-life equipment that still works** — a 15-year-old water heater the inspector flagged as "near end of useful life" but is still functioning.
- **Code items grandfathered in** — older electrical panels (Federal Pacific, Zinsco, Pushmatic) that work but wouldn&apos;t pass new construction code. (Different from active fire hazards — see below.)
- **Items the buyer knew about** — anything visible during the showing or disclosed pre-offer.

Buyers asking for these are usually overshooting. Refusing them is reasonable.

## What sellers shouldn&apos;t refuse

- **Active safety hazards** — exposed wiring, gas leaks, missing handrails on tall stairs, lead-paint chipping on a pre-1978 home with kids in the buyer family.
- **Active leaks** — anything wet now, regardless of severity.
- **Lender-required items** — FHA/VA safety conditions, basic habitability (working heat, hot water, no major roof leaks).
- **Inspection items the seller already knew about** — failing to repair plus failing to disclose can become legal liability after closing.

Refusing these doesn&apos;t just risk the deal — it can come back as a lawsuit if the buyer discovers the seller knew and stayed quiet.

## The standard response options

When you receive a repair request, you have four moves:

1. **Accept** — agree to all repairs as written, get them done before closing.
2. **Counter with completed repairs** — agree to a subset.
3. **Counter with a credit** — offer cash at closing in lieu of repairs. This is almost always cleaner than rushing fixes in a moving window. See our companion guide on [repair credit vs. fixing before closing](/home-inspection-repairs/repair-credit-vs-fix-before-closing).
4. **Refuse entirely** — tell the buyer the price already reflects the property condition.

Most experienced sellers do a mix: accept the safety items, counter with a credit on the maintenance items, and refuse the cosmetics outright.

## How buyers typically respond to a refusal

Inside the inspection contingency window, the buyer can:

- **Accept the refusal** — keep the deal, close at the original price.
- **Walk** — exercise the inspection contingency, get earnest money back.
- **Counter the refusal** — come back with a smaller list or a smaller credit ask.

The walk-away rate after a flat refusal is real but not catastrophic — in normal markets, most buyers don&apos;t walk over a few hundred dollars of cosmetic items. But they will walk over safety items, active leaks, and anything that signals the seller is hiding bigger problems.

## What I&apos;d do

If you&apos;re selling and the repair request hits your inbox: read it twice. Sort each line item into three piles — must-fix (lender required, active safety), should-fix (active leaks, items you knew about), and won&apos;t-fix (cosmetic, routine maintenance, end-of-useful-life-but-working).

Then write a single response that does three things:

1. **Agree to the must-fix items**, with a specific contractor or completion plan.
2. **Offer a single line-item credit** ("$1,200 toward buyer&apos;s repairs at closing") on the should-fix items rather than coordinating contractors yourself.
3. **Refuse the won&apos;t-fix items** with a one-sentence reason for each ("Carpet wear noted in MLS photos and reflected in list price").

This shows the buyer you read the report seriously, fixed what mattered, gave on the borderline, and held the line on the rest. It&apos;s much harder for a buyer to walk on that response than on a flat refusal.

If you&apos;re the buyer getting refused: don&apos;t escalate the same list. Cut it in half, target safety items first, and ask for a credit instead of work. Sellers refuse to write checks at the closing table less often than they refuse to manage repairs.
