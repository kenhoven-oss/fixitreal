Yes, in most U.S. states sellers must have working smoke detectors installed at closing — it&apos;s a state fire-code requirement on residential property transfers, not just a buyer preference. FHA, VA, and most conventional lenders also require working alarms before they&apos;ll fund the loan. The total seller cost is usually under $200 for a typical home, so this is the wrong item to fight over.

## What the law actually requires

Most U.S. states require smoke detectors in:

- **Every bedroom** (rooms used for sleeping)
- **The hallway outside every sleeping area**
- **At least one detector per level**, including the basement
- **Within 10 feet of the kitchen** in newer codes, with interconnection where wiring exists

Eight states (California, New York, Massachusetts, Maryland, Oregon, Iowa, Vermont, and a growing list) now require **10-year sealed lithium-battery** units, not the user-replaceable 9-volt type. Some require **combination smoke + carbon monoxide detectors** in homes with gas appliances, fuel-burning fireplaces, or attached garages.

Your state&apos;s exact code is the only document that matters. The listing agent or local fire marshal can confirm — most state fire-marshal websites publish a one-page seller checklist.

## What lenders require

- **FHA loans** explicitly require working smoke alarms in every code-required location. The HUD 4000.1 appraisal handbook treats missing or non-functional alarms as a "Minimum Property Requirement" failure — it must be cured before the loan funds.
- **VA loans** follow similar Minimum Property Requirement rules.
- **Conventional loans** are looser but almost always defer to the appraiser&apos;s safety call.

If the appraiser flags missing alarms, the seller fixes it or the loan doesn&apos;t fund.

## What the buyer can ask for

Even when the law and lender don&apos;t force the issue, the buyer&apos;s repair request almost certainly will. This is one of the cheapest, lowest-risk items on a typical inspection report — pushing back on it makes the seller look unreasonable on a $50 fix.

The standard ask: **install code-compliant alarms in every required location before closing, with a receipt or completed local certification form provided at the walkthrough.**

## What it costs the seller

For a typical 3-bedroom, single-story home (4 alarm locations):

| Option | Parts | Labor | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| DIY, battery 10-year sealed | $80–$120 | — | **$80–$120** |
| DIY, smoke + CO combo (battery) | $140–$240 | — | **$140–$240** |
| Electrician swap, hardwired | $40–$80 | $300–$500 | **$340–$580** |

DIY install on battery units is a 30-minute job: pull old units, install mounting plate, click new unit into place, press test button. The only catch is hardwired homes — if the existing units are wired and interconnected, replacing them with battery units is technically a code downgrade in many jurisdictions, and a battery upgrade in a hardwired system breaks the interconnection. In that case, pay an electrician to swap matching hardwired units.

For DIY-vs-pro framing on the related work, see our [Should I replace my own smoke detector?](/diy-or-hire/smoke-detector) guide.

## When it&apos;s safe to DIY

- Battery-only units in a home that&apos;s always been battery-only.
- Swapping an existing battery unit for a newer one.
- Adding a new battery unit in a previously uncovered location.

## When to hire an electrician

- Hardwired, interconnected systems being replaced like-for-like.
- Homes where you&apos;re adding new hardwired locations (e.g., adding a basement alarm in a finished basement).
- Anything where you&apos;re unsure whether the wiring is hot. **Smoke alarm wiring shares a circuit with lighting in many older homes — turn off the breaker before touching it, and verify with a non-contact voltage tester.** For tester guidance, see our [voltage tester buying guide](/tools/best-voltage-testers-for-homeowners).

## What I&apos;d do

If you&apos;re a seller getting hit with this on the repair request: just install them. $100–$200 of parts, an hour of labor, and the alarm certification box gets checked on the closing checklist. Buyers who refuse to budge on smoke alarms are usually right to — it&apos;s a life-safety item, the fix is trivial, and it&apos;s the wrong fight.

If you&apos;re a buyer: ask for it in writing as part of the repair request, and verify it at the final walkthrough. Press the test button on every unit. If you find a missing or non-functional alarm at walkthrough, that&apos;s grounds for delaying closing until it&apos;s cured.
