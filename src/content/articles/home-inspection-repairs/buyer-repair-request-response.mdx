A buyer's repair request after a home inspection is a negotiating document, not a contract amendment. Yet most sellers either say yes to too much or panic-refuse the whole thing — both moves cost money or kill deals. The better answer is a structured response that takes each line item in turn.

<KeyTakeaway>
Sort the list into four buckets: <strong>must do</strong> (lender or safety), <strong>will do</strong> (cheap goodwill), <strong>credit instead</strong> (anything the buyer will redo their way), and <strong>won't do</strong> (cosmetic, grandfathered, or re-inspector pet items). Respond in writing within your contractual response window. Don't say yes to a single line out loud until the entire counter is on paper.
</KeyTakeaway>

## Read the request, then stop

The first move is to read the entire request and do nothing else for a few hours. Don't email back. Don't text your agent. Don't talk to your spouse about which items make you angry. The goal is to separate emotional reactions from the actual triage.

Inspection repair requests almost always look bigger than they are. Inspectors write up everything they see — that's their job — and buyer's agents almost always pass the entire list through with minor edits. What looks like 30 items is usually:

- 3–6 lender-required or safety items
- 10–15 cosmetic or "investigate further" items
- 5–10 things the buyer is going to redo themselves anyway
- 2–4 things the inspector flagged that are simply wrong or out of scope

You're going to triage each one. The list is not a single yes/no decision.

## The four-bucket triage

### Must do

Items the lender, the appraisal, or basic safety require done before closing. These are the items you can't refuse without killing the deal:

- Anything the appraiser called out as a condition of value (FHA/VA loans especially)
- Active safety hazards: exposed live wiring, gas leaks, missing handrails on raised decks, structural issues
- Items that touch insurability — for example, knob-and-tube wiring in many carriers' books
- Anything explicitly written into the signed purchase agreement

A licensed professional should do this work. For electrical, plumbing, structural, gas, or roof items, get a licensed contractor's invoice — it's the paper trail you'll want at closing.

### Will do (cheap goodwill)

Items that are inexpensive, uncontroversial, and signal good faith. Doing these voluntarily is often what makes the buyer accept "won't do" on bigger items:

- Replacing 1–3 missing smoke or CO detectors
- Re-caulking a tub or two
- Tightening a wobbly toilet
- Replacing a torn window screen
- Cleaning a clogged dryer vent

These should each take under an hour and cost under $50–$100 in materials. If a fix costs you more than that, it probably belongs in the next bucket.

### Credit instead

For anything in the middle — not safety, not cheap, and almost certainly something the buyer will redo to their own taste anyway — offer a credit at closing instead of doing the work:

- "Re-glaze the master bathtub" → buyer's call on color and product
- "Replace the cracked vanity" → buyer is going to remodel the bathroom anyway
- "Replace the deck boards" → buyer prefers composite, you'd install pine
- "Service the HVAC" → buyer's service tech of choice does it cleaner

A credit at closing is almost always cheaper than doing the work yourself, doesn't require coordinating contractors during a stressful month, and removes the risk that the buyer comes back at the final walk-through complaining about your workmanship.

### Won't do

Items you can refuse without bad faith:

- Re-inspector pet items (e.g., GFCI receptacles in pre-1970s kitchens where they weren't required)
- Cosmetic items: paint touch-ups, minor dings, "yellowed" outlets
- "Recommend further evaluation" items where the original inspector saw nothing wrong
- Items that conflict with the as-is clause in your purchase agreement
- Anything you've already disclosed on the seller's disclosure

A flat refusal here, in writing, with one sentence of reasoning per item, is usually accepted.

## Get the response in writing

Verbal "yeses" are how deals fall apart at the closing table. Respond in writing — either through your agent on the contract form, or directly to the buyer's agent — with each line item addressed and the bucket called out.

A clean response looks like this:

> Item 1 (HVAC service): **Credit $150 at closing.**
> Item 2 (Smoke detectors, 3 missing): **Will replace before closing.**
> Item 3 (GFCI in upstairs bathroom): **Will replace; receipts at walk-through.**
> Item 4 (Driveway crack): **No action — disclosed on seller's disclosure.**
> Item 5 (Re-grade soil at south foundation): **No action — outside scope of inspection contingency.**

The buyer's agent will read down the list, weigh it against the buyer's appetite for walking, and either accept, counter, or escalate. About 80% of the time, a structured response like this gets accepted with one or two minor counters.

## When to push back vs when to concede

A few patterns I've seen consistently:

**Push back when:**
- The market is hot for your price point and you have backup offers.
- The buyer financed at the top of their qualification — they almost certainly can't afford to walk and re-shop.
- The list has clear "wishlist" items (paint, finishes, upgrades dressed up as repairs).

**Concede when:**
- The market is slow and you have no backup offers.
- The buyer is paying cash or has a strong down payment — they have options.
- The items called out would show up in any subsequent inspection.

The single biggest seller mistake I see: refusing everything to "make a point" and then accepting the same buyer's same list two weeks later when no one else has made an offer. If you were going to concede in two weeks anyway, concede now and save the goodwill.

<SafetyWarning>
For safety-flagged items — gas, structural, electrical, roof, major plumbing — use a licensed professional and get a paid invoice. Do not patch these yourself before closing. Insurance carriers and future buyers will both ask for documentation.
</SafetyWarning>

## What the contract actually requires

Your written response is governed by the purchase agreement, not by what feels fair. Three contractual mechanics worth confirming with your agent before you respond:

1. **Response window.** Usually 2–7 calendar days from the buyer's repair request. Missing it can be deemed acceptance or rejection depending on your form.
2. **As-is clause.** If the contract has one, the buyer's repair request is purely optional — you can decline everything and they either accept or walk.
3. **Repair-vs-credit limits.** Some VA and FHA loans cap closing-credit amounts and exclude certain item types. Check before offering a credit on lender-required items.

Real-estate contract rules vary by state and by individual transaction. Run the response by your agent or attorney before sending it.
