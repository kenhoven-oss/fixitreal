A surprising number of pre-listing improvements lose money at resale. Sellers spend $30,000 on a kitchen remodel and recover $20,000 of it at sale price. The math says: don&apos;t spend it. The improvements that consistently return — paint, cleaning, hardware, curb appeal — cost $500–$2,500 and usually recover 100%+. The ones that consistently lose money are mostly the ones contractors are most eager to sell.

## The biggest money-losing pre-listing improvements

### 1. Full kitchen remodel

**Typical cost:** $25,000–$60,000+
**Typical resale recovery:** 50–70%

Why it loses: buyers want to remodel kitchens to their own taste. Paying $40,000 for white shaker cabinets and quartz counters when the buyer wanted dark cabinets and butcher block is a $40,000 expense they&apos;d undo. Even a perfectly tasteful remodel rarely recovers its cost because the next buyer either likes it (and pays roughly market price) or doesn&apos;t (and discounts for the &ldquo;wrong style&rdquo; remodel).

**What to do instead:** if your kitchen is dated but functional, leave it. Paint the cabinets if they&apos;re visibly worn. Replace hardware for $100. Clean the appliances. The buyer&apos;s price impression at &ldquo;dated kitchen&rdquo; is much closer to &ldquo;tasteful new kitchen&rdquo; than the cost gap would suggest.

### 2. Full master bathroom remodel

**Typical cost:** $15,000–$40,000
**Typical resale recovery:** 55–70%

Same logic as the kitchen. Replace visibly broken items, deep clean, fresh paint, new caulk and grout. Skip the gut remodel.

### 3. Hardwood floor refinishing on borderline floors

**Typical cost:** $3,500–$8,000
**Typical recovery:** ~70% (when the floors needed it; less when they didn&apos;t)

If your hardwood floors are scuffed but intact: leave them. Buyers accept lived-in hardwood. If they&apos;re worn through to the subfloor in heavy-traffic areas: it&apos;s a different problem, and refinishing may be necessary just to sell at all.

The middle ground — &ldquo;could use a refinish&rdquo; — is where money gets wasted. Buyers don&apos;t see the difference between &ldquo;decent hardwood&rdquo; and &ldquo;refinished hardwood&rdquo; in the price impression, even though the seller spent $5,000 on the upgrade.

### 4. New roof when the existing has useful life left

**Typical cost:** $8,000–$25,000+
**Typical recovery:** 60–70%

A 10-year-old asphalt-shingle roof has 10+ years of useful life. Buyers know this. Replacing it doesn&apos;t add equivalent dollars to the sale price; offering a credit (or just disclosing the age) almost always nets out better.

**The exception:** an actively leaking roof, or one visibly failing (multiple missing shingles, sagging deck, sections of moss). Those are sale-blockers; the replacement is mandatory.

### 5. New HVAC system on functional equipment

**Typical cost:** $5,000–$12,000
**Typical recovery:** 60–75%

Buyers expect a functional HVAC system; they don&apos;t pay a premium for &ldquo;recently installed.&rdquo; If your 15-year-old furnace still runs reliably, leave it. If it&apos;s actively broken or refuses to start, replace it before listing (you can&apos;t sell a house without working heat).

### 6. Window replacement

**Typical cost:** $8,000–$25,000 for a typical home
**Typical recovery:** 65–70%

Modern energy-efficient windows are a nice feature but rarely recover their full cost at resale. Don&apos;t do it for the sale. The exception: visibly failed windows (broken seals showing fog between panes, sashes that won&apos;t close, rotted frames) — those need repair but not necessarily full replacement.

### 7. Whole-house repaint of the exterior (when the existing is just dated, not failing)

**Typical cost:** $4,000–$15,000
**Typical recovery:** 70–85%

Exterior paint that&apos;s peeling, faded, or visibly failing IS worth doing. Exterior paint that&apos;s just &ldquo;not the current trend&rdquo; is not. The buyer&apos;s impression at &ldquo;solid exterior, slightly dated&rdquo; vs. &ldquo;freshly painted&rdquo; doesn&apos;t cover the cost gap.

### 8. Custom landscaping

**Typical cost:** $2,500–$10,000+
**Typical recovery:** 60–80%

Specialty landscaping signals maintenance the buyer didn&apos;t sign up for. Keep it simple: mow, trim, mulch, weed. Skip the high-end designed beds and water features.

### 9. New hot water heater on a working unit

**Typical cost:** $1,200–$3,000
**Typical recovery:** ~50%

Functional water heater: leave it. Functional but old water heater: leave it; offer a credit if a buyer asks. Failed water heater: replace before listing. Don&apos;t replace pre-emptively.

### 10. Smart-home systems

**Typical cost:** $1,500–$10,000
**Typical recovery:** rarely measurable

Whole-home audio, integrated lighting, smart locks, security systems wired into proprietary hubs — these have niche appeal. The next buyer might love them, might consider them clutter, might not even know they&apos;re there. Don&apos;t install pre-listing.

The exceptions (small smart upgrades that DO help): a $200 smart thermostat, a $250 video doorbell, motion-activated exterior lights. Cheap, broad appeal, easy for the next owner.

## What to do with the money instead

Channel it into the high-ROI work documented elsewhere — see [cheap repairs that help sell a house](/home-inspection-repairs/cheap-repairs-that-help-sell-a-house).

In short:
- Fresh interior paint in neutral colors ($400–$2,000): ~100–150% ROI
- Deep clean + carpet clean ($300–$600): ~200%+ ROI
- Replace dated hardware in kitchen + bathrooms ($80–$300): ~80–120% ROI
- Curb appeal — mulch, mow, power-wash ($100–$400): ~100–150% ROI
- Fix visibly broken items (wobbly railings, sticking doors, stained ceiling tiles): ~150%+ ROI
- Replace expired smoke and CO alarms ($100–$300): peace of mind, lender requirement

Total for the high-ROI list: $1,000–$3,500. Total for the money-pit list: $50,000–$120,000+.

## When the &ldquo;don&apos;t bother&rdquo; list becomes &ldquo;must do&rdquo;

Several items on the money-pit list flip into mandatory territory when the home actively needs them:

- **Active roof leak** — replace (or repair) before listing
- **No working heat or AC during the listing season** — replace
- **Water heater leaking actively** — replace
- **Major water damage** — remediate the cause first, then patch cosmetic
- **Anything that violates current code on a refinance buyer&apos;s lender review** — fix or be ready to credit

The line: if the buyer&apos;s lender won&apos;t fund without it, you fix it. If the buyer might prefer it but the lender doesn&apos;t care, you don&apos;t.

## What I&apos;d do

For a typical 3-bedroom home with $5,000 of pre-listing budget:
1. Paint the most-visible interior rooms ($1,500–$2,000 hired)
2. Deep clean + carpet clean ($400–$500)
3. Replace dated kitchen hardware + bathroom faucet handles ($150–$300)
4. Fix every visibly broken item ($300–$1,500)
5. Power-wash + mulch the front yard ($150–$300)
6. Replace any smoke/CO alarms over 8 years old ($100–$200)
7. Leave $500–$1,000 for unforeseen items

That&apos;s the entire budget — and it covers everything that consistently recovers at resale. Spending more is spending into territory where ROI evaporates fast.
