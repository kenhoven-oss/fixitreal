A surprising number of pre-listing improvements lose money at resale. Sellers spend $30,000 on a kitchen remodel and recover $20,000 of it at sale price. The math says: don't spend it. The improvements that consistently return — paint, cleaning, hardware, curb appeal — cost $500–$2,500 and usually recover 100%+. The ones that consistently lose money are mostly the ones contractors are most eager to sell.

## The biggest money-losing pre-listing improvements

### 1. Full kitchen remodel

**Typical cost:** $25,000–$60,000+
**Typical resale recovery:** 50–70%

Why it loses: buyers want to remodel kitchens to their own taste. Paying $40,000 for white shaker cabinets and quartz counters when the buyer wanted dark cabinets and butcher block is a $40,000 expense they'd undo. Even a perfectly tasteful remodel rarely recovers its cost because the next buyer either likes it (and pays roughly market price) or doesn't (and discounts for the "wrong style" remodel).

**What to do instead:** if your kitchen is dated but functional, leave it. Paint the cabinets if they're visibly worn. Replace hardware for $100. Clean the appliances. The buyer's price impression at "dated kitchen" is much closer to "tasteful new kitchen" than the cost gap would suggest.

### 2. Full master bathroom remodel

**Typical cost:** $15,000–$40,000
**Typical resale recovery:** 55–70%

Same logic as the kitchen. Replace visibly broken items, deep clean, fresh paint, new caulk and grout. Skip the gut remodel.

### 3. Hardwood floor refinishing on borderline floors

**Typical cost:** $3,500–$8,000
**Typical recovery:** ~70% (when the floors needed it; less when they didn't)

If your hardwood floors are scuffed but intact: leave them. Buyers accept lived-in hardwood. If they're worn through to the subfloor in heavy-traffic areas: it's a different problem, and refinishing may be necessary just to sell at all.

The middle ground — "could use a refinish" — is where money gets wasted. Buyers don't see the difference between "decent hardwood" and "refinished hardwood" in the price impression, even though the seller spent $5,000 on the upgrade.

### 4. New roof when the existing has useful life left

**Typical cost:** $8,000–$25,000+
**Typical recovery:** 60–70%

A 10-year-old asphalt-shingle roof has 10+ years of useful life. Buyers know this. Replacing it doesn't add equivalent dollars to the sale price; offering a credit (or just disclosing the age) almost always nets out better.

**The exception:** an actively leaking roof, or one visibly failing (multiple missing shingles, sagging deck, sections of moss). Those are sale-blockers; the replacement is mandatory.

### 5. New HVAC system on functional equipment

**Typical cost:** $5,000–$12,000
**Typical recovery:** 60–75%

Buyers expect a functional HVAC system; they don't pay a premium for "recently installed." If your 15-year-old furnace still runs reliably, leave it. If it's actively broken or refuses to start, replace it before listing (you can't sell a house without working heat).

### 6. Window replacement

**Typical cost:** $8,000–$25,000 for a typical home
**Typical recovery:** 65–70%

Modern energy-efficient windows are a nice feature but rarely recover their full cost at resale. Don't do it for the sale. The exception: visibly failed windows (broken seals showing fog between panes, sashes that won't close, rotted frames) — those need repair but not necessarily full replacement.

### 7. Whole-house repaint of the exterior (when the existing is just dated, not failing)

**Typical cost:** $4,000–$15,000
**Typical recovery:** 70–85%

Exterior paint that's peeling, faded, or visibly failing IS worth doing. Exterior paint that's just "not the current trend" is not. The buyer's impression at "solid exterior, slightly dated" vs. "freshly painted" doesn't cover the cost gap.

### 8. Custom landscaping

**Typical cost:** $2,500–$10,000+
**Typical recovery:** 60–80%

Specialty landscaping signals maintenance the buyer didn't sign up for. Keep it simple: mow, trim, mulch, weed. Skip the high-end designed beds and water features.

### 9. New hot water heater on a working unit

**Typical cost:** $1,200–$3,000
**Typical recovery:** ~50%

Functional water heater: leave it. Functional but old water heater: leave it; offer a credit if a buyer asks. Failed water heater: replace before listing. Don't replace pre-emptively.

### 10. Smart-home systems

**Typical cost:** $1,500–$10,000
**Typical recovery:** rarely measurable

Whole-home audio, integrated lighting, smart locks, security systems wired into proprietary hubs — these have niche appeal. The next buyer might love them, might consider them clutter, might not even know they're there. Don't install pre-listing.

The exceptions (small smart upgrades that DO help): a $200 smart thermostat, a $250 video doorbell, motion-activated exterior lights. Cheap, broad appeal, easy for the next owner.

## What to do with the money instead

Channel it into the high-ROI work documented elsewhere — see [cheap repairs that help sell a house](/home-inspection-repairs/cheap-repairs-that-help-sell-a-house).

In short:
- Fresh interior paint in neutral colors ($400–$2,000): ~100–150% ROI
- Deep clean + carpet clean ($300–$600): ~200%+ ROI
- Replace dated hardware in kitchen + bathrooms ($80–$300): ~80–120% ROI
- Curb appeal — mulch, mow, power-wash ($100–$400): ~100–150% ROI
- Fix visibly broken items (wobbly railings, sticking doors, stained ceiling tiles): ~150%+ ROI
- Replace expired smoke and CO alarms ($100–$300): peace of mind, lender requirement

Total for the high-ROI list: $1,000–$3,500. Total for the money-pit list: $50,000–$120,000+.

## When the "don't bother" list becomes "must do"

Several items on the money-pit list flip into mandatory territory when the home actively needs them:

- **Active roof leak** — replace (or repair) before listing
- **No working heat or AC during the listing season** — replace
- **Water heater leaking actively** — replace
- **Major water damage** — remediate the cause first, then patch cosmetic
- **Anything that violates current code on a refinance buyer's lender review** — fix or be ready to credit

The line: if the buyer's lender won't fund without it, you fix it. If the buyer might prefer it but the lender doesn't care, you don't.

## What I'd do

For a typical 3-bedroom home with $5,000 of pre-listing budget:
1. Paint the most-visible interior rooms ($1,500–$2,000 hired)
2. Deep clean + carpet clean ($400–$500)
3. Replace dated kitchen hardware + bathroom faucet handles ($150–$300)
4. Fix every visibly broken item ($300–$1,500)
5. Power-wash + mulch the front yard ($150–$300)
6. Replace any smoke/CO alarms over 8 years old ($100–$200)
7. Leave $500–$1,000 for unforeseen items

That's the entire budget — and it covers everything that consistently recovers at resale. Spending more is spending into territory where ROI evaporates fast.
