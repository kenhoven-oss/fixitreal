Before listing, fix the items buyers will see during showings (paint, hardware, lighting, curb appeal) and the items lenders will require at closing (safety equipment, active leaks, working HVAC and water heater). Skip big renovations — they rarely recover cost. The order below is sequenced for a typical 30–45 day pre-listing window and assumes a budget of $1,000–$5,000 for cosmetic + small mechanical work.

## The pre-listing fix list (in order)

### Phase 1: Inside the house — what buyers see

**1. Fresh paint in neutral colors.**
Every wall a buyer sees. Cost $400–$2,000 depending on DIY vs hired and home size. ROI is reliably positive. Skip accent walls and bold colors.

**2. Deep clean + carpet clean.**
Professional service, $300–$600 total. Done the week before listing. The smell and feel of clean is what buyers notice first.

**3. Replace dated cabinet pulls and hardware.**
Kitchen + bathrooms. $80–$300 in materials. 4–8 hours of work. Dramatic visual update for the cost.

**4. Replace dated light fixtures.**
Especially &ldquo;boob lights,&rdquo; brass-finish chandeliers, and vanity bars. Mid-range replacements $40–$80 each. Budget $200–$600 to update the most-visible 4–8 fixtures.

**5. Re-caulk bathrooms.**
White silicone caulk on the tub/shower seams, kitchen counter-to-backsplash, around toilets. $20 in materials. A noticeably newer bathroom.

**6. Repair every visibly broken item.**
Wobbly handrails, sticking doors, broken cabinet hinges, cracked outlet covers, stained ceiling tiles, torn screens. Each fix is cheap; the cumulative signal is &ldquo;this house is maintained.&rdquo;

**7. Replace burnt-out bulbs.**
Free if you have spares. Walk every room with a bag of fresh bulbs the night before showings start.

### Phase 2: Outside — curb appeal

**8. Fresh mulch + tidy landscaping.**
$50–$150 for mulch + a Saturday afternoon of edging and trimming.

**9. Power wash siding and driveway.**
$0–$400 depending on whether you own / rent / hire. Massive curb-appeal payoff.

**10. Repaint or clean the front door.**
$20–$100. The photo that ends up on the MLS.

**11. New doormat + house numbers.**
$40–$110. The smallest possible touch that signals updated taste.

**12. Trim overgrown trees and shrubs from house exterior.**
Free if you do it yourself. Important for inspection: branches touching the roof or siding is a flag.

### Phase 3: Safety equipment buyers and lenders will check

**13. Replace smoke alarms more than 8 years old.**
$15–$30 each, sealed 10-year lithium. Lender-required on FHA/VA, almost always asked in repair requests. See our [smoke detector replacement cost guide](/costs/smoke-detector-replacement).

**14. Replace carbon monoxide alarms more than 7 years old.**
Required in any home with gas, oil, propane, attached garage, or fireplace. See our [best carbon-monoxide detectors guide](/tools/best-carbon-monoxide-detectors).

**15. Verify GFCI outlets in bathrooms, kitchens, garage, outdoor.**
Code in newer homes; grandfathered in older. Easy buyer ask if missing. See [Should I replace a GFCI outlet myself?](/diy-or-hire/gfci-outlet).

**16. Verify handrails on staircases with 4+ steps.**
Lender-required on FHA. Easy add if missing — $30-$80 in parts, DIY install.

### Phase 4: Mechanical items buyers will inspect

**17. Service the HVAC (one tune-up, ~$120).**
A tune-up receipt dated within 6 months of listing reassures buyers and inspectors. Doesn&apos;t guarantee a clean inspection but signals maintained.

**18. Flush the water heater.**
Free if DIY (see our [water heater leaking diagnosis](/advice/water-heater-leaking) for the procedure). Extends life and may avoid an &ldquo;end of useful life&rdquo; inspection flag on units 8+ years old.

**19. Replace washing machine hoses with braided stainless steel.**
$25–$40 / 30 minutes / massive risk reduction. Burst hose is the #1 home insurance claim category.

**20. Check the bathroom exhaust fans actually vent outside.**
A fan venting into the attic or under a porch is an inspection flag and a mold-development risk. See [bathroom exhaust fan venting under porch roof](/home-inspection-repairs/bathroom-exhaust-fan-vent-under-porch) for the cost-to-fix.

### Phase 5: Documentation buyers will ask for

**21. Compile recent receipts and warranties.**
Roof age, HVAC service history, water heater age, appliance warranties, recent major repairs. A folder of documentation removes uncertainty from the buyer&apos;s pricing.

**22. Locate and label utility shut-offs.**
Water main, gas, electrical panel breakers. Map them with masking tape and a marker so the buyer can find them at the walk-through.

**23. Verify the seller-disclosure form is complete and accurate.**
Most states have a standard form. List anything you&apos;ve known about over the last 5+ years — even items you fixed. Non-disclosure is a much bigger legal exposure than disclosure.

## What to skip

These commonly-suggested pre-listing improvements rarely earn back their cost. Skip them, or only do them if you genuinely want to:

- **Full kitchen remodel.** $25K–$60K. Recovers 50–70%. Buyers want their own choices.
- **Bathroom gut remodel.** Same.
- **New hardwood floors.** Refinish > replace. Replace only if existing are unsalvageable.
- **Replacing windows.** Average payback under 70%. Only if windows are visibly failing.
- **Adding square footage.** Permitting takes months; the sale is faster.
- **Smart-home systems** (whole-home audio, lighting). Niche appeal.
- **High-end appliances.** Mid-range new if the existing are visibly old; otherwise leave.

## When pre-listing inspection IS worth it

Pre-listing inspection ($300–$600) is worth it when:

- The home is 25+ years old.
- You suspect specific issues (slow drip, soft spot, electrical quirk).
- You&apos;re in a slow market where inspection-killed deals are common.
- You have time before listing — usually 6+ weeks.
- You&apos;d rather control the timeline of repairs than face surprises.

When it&apos;s NOT worth it:

- Hot market with limited inspection contingencies.
- You&apos;re selling as-is and have disclosed accordingly.
- You can&apos;t afford to act on whatever the inspection finds.

## What I&apos;d do

Sequence over 4–6 weeks before listing:

**Week 1–2:** Order the carpet/deep clean for week 5. Buy paint and start painting. Replace cabinet hardware. Schedule HVAC tune-up.

**Week 3:** Continue paint. Re-caulk bathrooms. Replace dated light fixtures. Verify smoke/CO alarms.

**Week 4:** Fix everything visibly broken. Curb appeal: mulch, mow, power wash, paint front door. Update house numbers and doormat.

**Week 5:** Deep clean + carpet clean. Final detail walk-through with a critical eye.

**Week 6:** Compile documentation, label utility shut-offs, final lawn cut.

The first 4 weeks are where 80% of the perceived-value comes from. The last 2 weeks are detail and presentation. Big budget? Add to the mechanical-items list (HVAC, water heater). Small budget? Stop after Phase 1+2.
