The repairs that move a sale price most are usually the cheap, visible ones — fresh neutral paint, clean grout, updated cabinet hardware, working light fixtures, refreshed front-yard curb appeal. Total budget for the highest-ROI pre-sale work is typically $500–$2,500, well under what most sellers spend on the wrong things. Big renovations (new kitchen, master bath remodel, hardwood floors) almost never recover their full cost. The work below is ordered by ROI, not by what your contractor will suggest.

## The ROI-ordered list

### 1. Fresh interior paint in neutral colors — $400–$2,000

**ROI: typically 100–150%.** Walls form the first impression buyers carry. A fresh neutral repaint resets that impression in every room. The cheap way: do the most-visible rooms yourself (entry, living, kitchen) and hire out the bedrooms if you&apos;re short on time.

Stay neutral: agreeable beige, light gray, off-white. Skip accent walls and bold colors. The point is to make every room look move-in ready, not to express your taste.

### 2. Deep clean (professional) — $150–$500

**ROI: typically 200%+.** A professional cleaning service ($150–$300) plus carpet shampooing ($100–$200) and window washing ($75–$200) is the single highest-ROI use of $500 in pre-sale prep. The smell of clean is selling itself.

Includes: kitchen degreasing, oven, refrigerator, baseboards, blinds, light fixtures, baseboards, vents. The places buyers actually look.

### 3. Replace dated hardware — $80–$300

**ROI: typically 80–120%.** Cabinet pulls ($2–$5 each at the budget end), bathroom and kitchen faucet handles, light-switch plates, doorknobs — the small visible metalwork that signals &ldquo;updated&rdquo; or &ldquo;dated.&rdquo; A 30-cabinet kitchen costs $60–$150 to re-pull and looks dramatically newer.

Skip the cabinet doors themselves unless they&apos;re damaged — full cabinet replacement rarely returns its cost on resale.

### 4. Curb appeal — $50–$400

**ROI: typically 100–150%.** Buyers form their first opinion in the driveway. Cheap moves:

- Fresh black mulch in beds: $40-$100
- Mow + edge: free if you do it
- Power wash siding and driveway: $0 if you own/rent the equipment, $150-$400 hired
- New doormat: $20-$50
- New house numbers: $20-$60
- Front door paint touch-up or full repaint: $20-$100
- Trim overgrown shrubs

A $200 budget transforms most front yards.

### 5. Replace burnt-out and dated light fixtures — $50–$300

**ROI: typically 80–120%.** Every burnt-out bulb in the house, replaced. Add new daylight (5000K) LED bulbs in the kitchen — they brighten the room dramatically without rewiring. Replace any fixtures that scream 1990s — boob lights, brass-finish chandeliers, glass-globe vanity lights. Mid-range replacements from Home Depot are $40–$80 each.

### 6. Caulk + grout refresh — $20–$80 DIY

**ROI: typically 200%+.** Re-caulk the bathtub and shower surrounds, the kitchen counter-to-backsplash seam, around toilets. White silicone caulk + bleach pen for grout discoloration = a noticeably newer bathroom for under $30 in materials.

### 7. Touch up the front door — $20–$150

**ROI: typically 150–250%.** A freshly painted (or even just freshly cleaned) front door is the photo that ends up on the MLS listing. $20 of paint or $40 of door cleaner + new hardware can dramatically increase the curb-appeal photo.

### 8. Replace dated bathroom mirrors and lighting — $80–$300

**ROI: typically 80–120%.** A frameless mirror and a fresh vanity light fixture transforms a small bathroom. Both available for under $150 combined at any home improvement store.

### 9. Repair anything broken in plain sight — $50–$500

**ROI: typically 150%+.** Wobbly stair railings. Sticking doors. Broken cabinet hinges. Stained ceiling tiles. Cracked outlet covers. These signal &ldquo;the house has been neglected&rdquo; — a much bigger turn-off than the repair&apos;s actual significance. Fix everything visibly broken before the first showing.

### 10. Smoke alarm + CO detector refresh — $100–$300

**ROI: peace-of-mind, not direct return.** Replace any unit older than 8 years with 10-year sealed lithium models. Lender-required at closing on FHA/VA, often inspection-flagged on conventional. Get ahead of it. See our [smoke detector replacement cost guide](/costs/smoke-detector-replacement) and [Do sellers have to replace smoke detectors before closing?](/home-inspection-repairs/sellers-replace-smoke-detectors-before-closing).

## What NOT to spend money on

These commonly-recommended pre-sale improvements rarely recover their cost:

- **Full kitchen remodel.** Average $25,000–$60,000. Typical recovery: 50–70%. Buyers want to choose their own.
- **Master bathroom remodel.** Similar ROI. Buyers want their own taste.
- **Hardwood floor refinish on bad floors.** Often cheaper to offer a flooring credit than to refinish.
- **New roof when the existing one has 5+ years left.** Buyers don&apos;t pay for roofs they didn&apos;t pick; offer a credit if it&apos;s near end-of-life.
- **High-end appliances.** Buyers will replace to their preferences. Mid-range new appliances if the existing ones are visibly old; otherwise leave them.
- **Custom landscaping.** Goes the wrong direction — buyers see maintenance they didn&apos;t sign up for. Stick to clean and tidy.
- **Permitted additions or remodels just to add square footage.** Permitting alone takes 2–3 months in many jurisdictions; the sale is faster.

## Sequence: when to do each

The 30-day pre-listing schedule that works:

**Weeks 1–2:**
- Schedule deep clean and carpet cleaning for week 3 or 4
- Buy paint and start interior repaint of most-visible rooms
- Replace hardware throughout kitchen and bathrooms
- Replace all burnt-out bulbs

**Week 3:**
- Finish painting
- Caulk and grout refresh
- Curb appeal: mulch, mow, power wash
- Repair visibly broken items

**Week 4:**
- Deep clean + carpet clean (use the cleaning service the week before listing)
- Final touches: doormat, house numbers, front door touch-up
- Smoke / CO alarms updated
- Walk through with a critical eye — what would a buyer see?

## What I&apos;d do

For a typical $400K home with a $500–$2,000 pre-sale budget: paint the entry, living room, and kitchen ($200–$600 DIY), deep clean ($200–$400), replace cabinet hardware in the kitchen ($60–$120), refresh curb appeal ($100–$200), update one bathroom mirror and light ($100–$200). Total: $660–$1,520. Recovers in nearly every case.

Skip everything else until those are done. The instinct to &ldquo;do something big&rdquo; is the most common pre-sale money-loser. Big renovations rarely recover cost; small visible work almost always does.
