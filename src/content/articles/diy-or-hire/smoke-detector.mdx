Yes — replacing a smoke detector is a beginner-level DIY job. Battery-only swaps are 15 minutes per unit with no tools beyond a screwdriver. Hardwired replacements take longer, need the breaker off and a voltage tester to verify, and only get tricky if you&apos;re also changing the wiring. The total parts cost is $15–$60 per alarm depending on type. The verdict for nearly every homeowner: DIY.

## When DIY is the right call

- **Like-for-like battery replacement.** Pull old, install new. No skills required.
- **Like-for-like hardwired replacement** using the same brand and connector style as the existing alarm. Most major brands (Kidde, First Alert, BRK) sell replacement units that snap into the existing wiring harness.
- **Adding a new battery-only unit** in a previously uncovered location (an extra hallway, a finished basement).

## When to hire an electrician

- **Switching between hardwired brands** that use different wiring harnesses. The plug doesn&apos;t match; you&apos;d be cutting and splicing inside a junction box. Pay an electrician $100–$200 to do it right, or buy the same brand replacement and stay DIY.
- **Adding a new hardwired location.** Drilling through framing, running new cable, and tying into an existing alarm circuit needs a code-aware install — and a permit in many jurisdictions.
- **Old aluminum branch wiring.** The connector style and torque specs are different, and amateur splices on aluminum cause fires. Pro work.
- **Anything that requires opening a panel.** Smoke alarms tie into the lighting circuit; you should never need to touch the panel for a swap. If the work involves the panel, hire it out.

## The DIY job, start to finish

**For battery-only units:**

1. Twist the old unit counter-clockwise to release it from its mounting plate.
2. Pull the battery from the old unit; recycle (or trash) per local rules.
3. Press the test button on the new unit to confirm it&apos;s alive before mounting.
4. Activate the new unit (10-year sealed lithium units have a pull tab; older 9V units need a battery installed).
5. Align with the mounting plate and twist clockwise to lock.
6. Press and hold the test button for ~5 seconds — alarm should sound. Done.

**For hardwired units (same brand replacement):**

1. **Turn off the breaker that feeds the smoke-alarm circuit.** Confirm with a non-contact voltage tester at the alarm itself. Smoke alarms in older homes sometimes share a circuit with hallway lighting; verify both are dead. (See our [voltage tester buying guide](/tools/best-voltage-testers-for-homeowners).)
2. Twist the old unit off the mounting plate.
3. Disconnect the wiring harness (small plug, sometimes with a release tab).
4. Unscrew the mounting plate from the ceiling box only if changing brands; keep the existing plate if it&apos;s the same brand.
5. Plug the harness into the new unit (will only fit one way).
6. Insert the battery backup if not already pre-installed.
7. Tuck the wiring back into the ceiling box and twist the new unit onto the mounting plate.
8. **Turn the breaker back on.** Press and hold the test button — alarm should sound, and on interconnected systems, all the other alarms should sound too within a few seconds.

## Safety notes

- **The 10-year clock starts at manufacture, not install.** A unit that&apos;s been sitting on a hardware-store shelf for 3 years is already 30% through its life. Check the manufacture date on the back before you buy.
- **Combination smoke + CO alarms are smart for any home with gas, oil, propane, an attached garage, or a fireplace.** They&apos;re not much more expensive than smoke-only.
- **Don&apos;t use the silence button as your maintenance habit.** A chirping alarm is telling you something. Silence it once to figure out which alarm is chirping, then replace the battery (or the whole unit if it&apos;s 8+ years old).

## What I&apos;d do

If you&apos;ve got an inspection coming, a buyer asked for it in a repair request, or your existing alarms are 8+ years old: do all of them at once. Same trip to the hardware store, same hour of work, and you reset the 10-year clock across the house.
