For most small holes — picture hangers, door knobs through the wall, minor dings — drywall patching is a confident DIY. A $12 patch kit, an evening of intermittent work (patches need drying time between coats), and you've saved $80–$200 in handyman minimum-charge fees. For larger holes, textured surfaces, ceiling work, or water-damaged areas, hiring is usually the right call. The cost premium is real but so is the skill gap.

## When DIY is the right call

- **Small holes** (under 4 inches across). Patch kits make this nearly foolproof.
- **Flat-finish walls.** Smooth walls forgive amateur work; texture walls don't.
- **Multiple small repairs** in one weekend. Batching makes DIY worth the setup time.
- **Cosmetic touch-up before listing or after move-out.** No structural concerns, just visual.

## When to hire instead

- **Textured walls** (knock-down, orange-peel, skip-trowel). Texture matching is a specialty skill.
- **Ceiling patches.** Working overhead is harder; falling mud is worse than falling sweat.
- **Holes 12+ inches across.** Requires backing material, taping, multiple coats over days.
- **Water-damaged drywall.** The damage is the symptom; the cause needs diagnosis (active leak? past event? hidden mold?) before patching.
- **Pre-1980 popcorn ceilings.** Asbestos risk — test first, abate by licensed contractor if positive.
- **Holes near electrical boxes or plumbing.** The repair needs to work around or relocate the fixture safely.

## The small-patch DIY job

For a hole under 4 inches:

1. **Clean the edges.** Knock loose drywall fragments off with a putty knife; vacuum dust.
2. **Apply the patch.** Pre-mesh-backed patch kits stick directly over the hole — peel and press.
3. **Skim with joint compound.** Use the 6-inch knife in the kit. Feather the edges outward 2–3 inches past the patch. Let dry 8–24 hours.
4. **Light sand.** 220-grit sandpaper. Knock down high spots only; don't over-sand.
5. **Second coat if needed.** For visible patches, a second thin coat with more feathering improves the finish.
6. **Prime + paint.** A flat or eggshell paint over a primed patch usually disappears.

Total: 20 minutes of active work, spread across 2 days of drying.

## The medium-patch DIY job

For a hole 4–12 inches:

1. **Cut a clean square** around the hole with a drywall saw.
2. **Add backing.** A small piece of scrap drywall or wood, glued or screwed inside the hole to provide a backing surface for the new patch.
3. **Cut a drywall patch** to fit the cleaned-up hole. Set it in place.
4. **Tape the seams** with mesh or paper tape. Cover with joint compound.
5. **First, second, third coats.** Each thinner than the last, each feathering 2 inches further out. Dry between coats (4–24 hours each).
6. **Sand, prime, paint.**

Total: 90–180 minutes of active work over 3–5 days. Where DIY gets challenging is matching the surrounding texture — flat finishes are forgiving, anything else requires practice or willingness to live with a visible patch.

## Common DIY mistakes

- **Skipping the backing material** on holes 4+ inches. Patches without backing crack.
- **Building up too much mud at once.** Thin coats with drying time produce flatter, smoother finishes. One thick coat shrinks and cracks.
- **Over-sanding.** Aggressive sanding takes off the texture you're trying to match. Light, steady passes.
- **Skipping primer before paint.** Bare joint compound absorbs paint differently than surrounding wall — primer evens it out.
- **Mixing acrylic and oil-based products.** Modern joint compound + latex paint + latex primer is the standard combo. Don't innovate.

## Red flags from a contractor quote

- **Bundled "wall patch + texture + paint + cleanup" at $400+ for one small patch.** Get a second quote.
- **"Mold testing" added** to a water-damage drywall job without visible mold.
- **Pressure to replace entire walls** rather than patch localized damage, with no visible reason.
- **No itemization** of patch / coat / sand / paint on the invoice.

## Safety note

For pre-1980 homes especially: textured ceiling coatings (popcorn, blown-on stucco) may contain asbestos. Don't sand, scrape, or disturb without an asbestos test first ($30–$100 for a sample kit at any hardware store or environmental lab). If positive, licensed abatement is required by law in most jurisdictions — never DIY.

For drywall behind water damage: confirm with a moisture meter that the framing behind is dry before patching over. Sealing wet wood inside a wall guarantees mold within months.

## What I'd do

For small holes (picture hangers, doorknobs, scrapes): always DIY. Buy a single $15 patch kit, watch a 5-minute YouTube video, and finish a list of 4–6 small patches in one weekend.

For one medium patch in a high-visibility area (a living-room wall, a foyer): hire it. The texture and finish quality matter more than the $200 saved.

For multiple medium patches OR any ceiling work OR anything water-damaged: hire it.
