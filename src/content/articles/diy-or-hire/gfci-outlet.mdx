GFCI outlet replacement is borderline DIY. The mechanical work is simple — two or four wires onto labeled terminals — but you&apos;re working with live circuits (after turning off the breaker), and a miswire can leave downstream outlets unprotected without you knowing. For a comfortable DIYer with a voltage tester and a careful approach, this is a 20-minute job and a clear DIY. For anyone uncertain about wiring or how to verify the circuit is dead, hire an electrician for $80–$200.

## When DIY is the right call

- **Like-for-like GFCI replacement** where the original install is correct (Line wires on Line terminals, Load wires on Load terminals).
- **A standard outlet you&apos;re upgrading to GFCI** in a kitchen, bathroom, garage, basement, outdoor, or laundry location — current NEC requires GFCI there.
- **You own a non-contact voltage tester** (or a contact multimeter), know how to use it, and verify the circuit is dead before touching wires.
- **The wiring in the box is in good shape** — no aluminum, no scorch marks, no melted insulation.

## When to hire an electrician

- **Aluminum branch wiring.** GFCI outlets work on aluminum only with special "CO/ALR" rated devices and specific torque on the connections. Amateur work on aluminum is a fire risk.
- **Backstabbed outlets in a stack.** If the existing outlet uses backstab connections (wires pushed into spring clips on the back, common in 1980s–1990s homes), it&apos;s prone to loosening over time. Replacing with screw terminals is the right move, but if multiple wires are coming into the box and you can&apos;t identify Line vs. Load, it&apos;s an electrician&apos;s job.
- **The box has more than two cables coming in.** Multi-cable boxes are common in kitchens where the GFCI feeds several downstream outlets. The Line/Load logic gets harder; mistakes leave downstream protection broken.
- **Any sign of trouble in the box** — scorched wire, melted insulation, evidence of overheating, three-prong outlet on a two-wire system (no ground).
- **Older outlets that don&apos;t accept GFCI without box adaptation.** Some pre-1965 homes have shallow boxes that don&apos;t fit modern GFCIs without surgery.

## The DIY job

What you&apos;ll need:

- Replacement GFCI outlet (15A for general circuits, 20A for kitchens and bathrooms — match the existing).
- Non-contact voltage tester. (See our [voltage tester buying guide](/tools/best-voltage-testers-for-homeowners).)
- #2 Phillips screwdriver.
- Wire strippers.
- Painters tape and a pen for labeling.

Steps:

1. **Identify the breaker.** Go to the panel and find the breaker feeding this outlet. Turn it off.
2. **Confirm the outlet is dead.** Plug a lamp in or use the voltage tester at both slots. No power.
3. **Remove the wall plate and outlet.** Pull the outlet out of the box. Don&apos;t disconnect anything yet.
4. **Identify Line vs. Load.** If there are two cables in the box: one is Line (incoming, from breaker), one is Load (outgoing, to downstream outlets). With the breaker on, the Line is the one that&apos;s hot. With the breaker off, you can&apos;t tell electrically — so this is the moment to label them with painters tape *before* disconnecting anything. If you&apos;re replacing an existing GFCI, the wires going to "LINE" on the old unit are Line; same for Load.
5. **Disconnect the old outlet.** Loosen the terminal screws, pull each wire off, note the colors.
6. **Connect the new GFCI.**
   - Line: black wire on Line brass terminal, white wire on Line silver terminal.
   - Load: black wire on Load brass terminal, white wire on Load silver terminal. *(If there&apos;s no Load cable, leave the Load terminals empty and don&apos;t remove the warning sticker over them.)*
   - Ground: bare or green wire on the green grounding screw.
7. **Tuck wires into the box** without bending them sharply. Push the outlet into the box. Screw it in. Replace the wall plate.
8. **Restore power.** Press the TEST button — outlet should click off (red LED). Press RESET — power restored. Plug a lamp in to verify, and confirm any downstream outlets work and are protected.

## Safety notes

- **Verify the breaker is off, twice.** Touch the voltage tester to a known-hot outlet first to confirm it works. Then test the outlet you&apos;re working on. False negatives from a dead-battery tester are the #1 cause of "I thought it was off" shocks.
- **Don&apos;t skip the Load wiring if there are downstream outlets.** A GFCI miswired with both cables on Line terminals works as an outlet but provides no protection downstream. Half-protected kitchens are how electrical fires start.
- **Press TEST monthly after install.** This is the only way to verify GFCI protection still works. About 1 in 10 older GFCIs eventually fails in a "stays on but no protection" state.

## What I&apos;d do

For a single GFCI swap in a clean box with two cables clearly labeled by the previous installer, I&apos;d DIY without hesitation. For anything where I can&apos;t immediately identify Line and Load, where the box is crowded with multiple cables, or where I notice anything off about the existing wiring, I&apos;d call an electrician. The $100–$200 is cheap insurance against half-protecting a kitchen.
