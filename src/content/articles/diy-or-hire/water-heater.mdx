Water heater replacement is the one job on this site where our verdict and our gut instinct don't quite match. The plumbing is simple. The connections are two water lines, one gas or electric hookup, and a drain. If you've replaced a faucet or a toilet, you've already done harder work than the actual pipe-joining here.

And yet, for most homeowners, this is a **hire-a-pro** job. Here's why, and here's how to tell if you're in the small minority of cases where DIY might make sense.

<PullQuote>
  Water heater replacement isn't a plumbing job — it's a permit, an inspection, and a dozen code items that trip up careful DIYers who got the pipe work right.
</PullQuote>

## Why this job is different

Faucets leak visibly. Toilets leak onto the floor where you can see them. A water heater that's been installed wrong can leak slowly behind the wall for months, drip combustion gases into the living space, or rupture catastrophically if the pressure-relief path is blocked.

The mechanical install is maybe 60% of the work. The other 40% is a pile of small code-mandated peripherals that each serve a real safety purpose. An inspector walking in after a DIY install looks straight at those peripherals, not at the plumbing joints.

## The code items that catch careful DIYers

**Expansion tank on closed systems.** If your municipal water supply has a check valve or pressure-reducing valve (most do), the plumbing system is "closed." When water heats and expands, it has nowhere to go. A small pressure vessel ($75–$150) absorbs the expansion. Without it, the T&P valve weeps, the tank works harder, and the warranty may be void. Code in most jurisdictions requires one.

**T&P valve discharge piping.** The temperature-and-pressure valve is the failsafe: if pressure spikes, it releases water before the tank ruptures. Code requires its discharge tube to run to an approved termination — typically 6 inches above the floor or into an indirect waste receptor — without reductions in diameter, without threads at the end, and without a shutoff. Homeowners frequently reduce the line to fit an existing drain, or cap it to stop a weeping valve. Both are inspection failures. Both can be fatal.

**Drip pan with drain or alarm.** If the unit sits where a leak would cause damage — a closet over a living space, an attic, a finished basement — most jurisdictions require a drip pan plumbed to a drain or fitted with a leak alarm. Not every install, but most residential ones.

**Seismic strapping.** California, Washington, Oregon, and parts of Nevada: two straps at the upper third and lower third of the tank. Non-negotiable.

**Gas-line sediment trap** (gas units). A short vertical pipe section upstream of the gas control valve that catches debris. Missed on DIY installs constantly; inspectors catch it every time.

**Proper combustion venting** (gas units). Metal flue of the right diameter, pitched per code, with proper clearances to combustible materials. Venting a new high-efficiency gas unit into an old B-vent designed for the old unit is the most common — and dangerous — DIY mistake. Carbon monoxide in the living space is not a "maybe" risk.

**Dielectric unions.** Where copper meets galvanized steel, a dielectric union prevents galvanic corrosion. Homeowners skip this because the old unit didn't have one (it had rotted the pipes instead).

## The permit-and-insurance angle

In every US jurisdiction I'm aware of, replacing a water heater requires a plumbing permit. Homeowners often skip it — the permit fee is $150–$500, the inspector coordination is annoying, and the local building department isn't going to knock on the door if the unit is hidden in a closet.

Here's the catch: if the tank fails and causes water damage, your homeowner's insurance adjuster is going to ask whether the install was permitted and inspected. An unpermitted install commonly voids the water-damage claim. A $2,000 tank replacement that saves $500 in labor can cost $20,000 in uncovered drywall, flooring, and contents damage six years later.

The permit is the insurance. Pay for the permit.

## When DIY might actually make sense

A small minority of cases:

- You've done it before, under a permit, with a passed inspection.
- You have a licensed plumber willing to review your work before the inspector arrives.
- It's a simple like-for-like electric swap in a detached utility building (no combustion, no vent, no structure above it).
- You've already invested in an expansion tank, drip pan, and straps from a prior DIY cycle.

In all these cases, pull the permit. Budget a full weekend. Budget for the inspection to fail the first time — it usually does.

## What a fair pro quote looks like

For a standard 50-gallon replacement: **$1,350–$2,400 total**, including the unit, labor, permit, haul-away, expansion tank, and any code-required upgrades. See our full [water heater replacement cost breakdown](/costs/water-heater-replacement) for the itemized math.

Before you approve any quote over $2,800, verify:

- **Permit is being pulled.** Ask for the permit number before work starts. The plumber shouldn't roll trucks without it.
- **Expansion tank and drip pan are itemized** — not buried in "miscellaneous."
- **Haul-away of the old unit is included.** Should be. If it's a $75 line item, push back.
- **T&P valve discharge routing is itemized** if the old unit's wasn't code-compliant.
- **Inspection is scheduled.** Hold final payment until the permit passes inspection.

Anything over **$4,000** on a standard like-for-like swap without a specific reason (moved location, service upgrade, gas-line work, tankless conversion) warrants a second quote.

## Tankless is a different animal

Tankless ("on-demand") water heaters are not a like-for-like swap. They typically require:

- A larger gas line (3/4" vs 1/2") for residential BTU ratings.
- Dedicated PVC or stainless venting, often running to the exterior.
- New electrical, even for gas units (the ignition and controls need 120V).
- A condensate drain for high-efficiency models.
- Sometimes a water softener if the supply is hard — tankless heat exchangers scale and fail faster than tanks.

Tankless installs run **$2,800–$5,500** and are specialty pro work. The fuel savings are real but payback is typically 5–10 years, which matters if you don't plan to stay that long.

## The one-line version

Water heaters are the job where competent DIY saves $800 and costs $12,000 if it goes wrong. Hire the plumber. Keep the permit in the file. When the tank fails in year nine, your insurance company will be glad you did.
