Drain clogs are the single most overcharged routine plumbing call. A $20 hand snake from any hardware store handles about 90% of residential clogs in under 30 minutes. The same 30 minutes at a plumber's rate costs $150 to $400 once you include the service call fee and the two-hour minimum.

For anything short of a main-line backup, this is a clear DIY.

<PullQuote>
  The best first tool for 90% of drain clogs is a $20 hand snake. The second best is a plunger matched to the fixture. The third best — actually, there isn't a third best. Chemicals don't make the list.
</PullQuote>

## The tool that does most of the work

A 25-foot hand-crank drum snake is the single most useful homeowner plumbing tool I own. It costs $15–$30, fits in any under-sink cabinet, and handles:

- Bathroom sink clogs (hair at the pop-up stopper, gunk in the trap).
- Tub and shower clogs (hair and soap buildup at the drain strainer and in the trap).
- Kitchen sink clogs (grease and food debris in the trap and first few feet of line).
- Utility sink and washing-machine standpipe clogs.

The technique is the same across fixtures: remove the stopper or strainer, feed the cable in slowly while cranking the drum, push through any resistance, and pull back. The clog usually comes with the cable. Rinse, repeat if needed.

Complementary tools, in order of how often you'll use them:

- **Plunger** — a flat-cup sink plunger and a flanged toilet plunger. Separate plungers. Don't use the toilet one on sinks.
- **Bucket** — for catching the water in the P-trap when you unscrew it.
- **Rubber gloves** — the contents of a clog are more biologically interesting than you want.
- **Flashlight** — under-sink visibility is always bad.

Total investment for a full drain-clog kit: **$30–$50.** One plumber call-out: $150–$400. Savings pays for the tools on the first use.

## Per-fixture approach

**Bathroom sink.** Remove the pop-up stopper (twist off or unscrew the retaining nut under the sink). Most bathroom sink clogs are hair wrapped around the stopper itself — pulling the stopper often solves the problem with nothing else needed. If the drain still runs slow, put a bucket under the P-trap, unscrew the slip nuts, empty and clean the trap. That's 95% of bathroom sink clogs.

**Kitchen sink.** Hot water and dish soap first — pour a kettle of hot (not boiling, which can crack older porcelain) water plus a squeeze of dish soap, wait 5 minutes, plunge. This handles grease softening. If that fails, P-trap off and cleared. If that still fails, snake into the drain line past the trap. If *that* fails, you may have a deeper line issue; stop here and call.

**Tub or shower.** Remove the drain cover or stopper (push-and-turn, twist off, or lift and unscrew depending on type). Feed a drain snake in slowly, crank past resistance, pull back. The clog is almost always a hair ball within 18 inches of the opening.

**Toilet.** Flanged plunger first, aggressive downward pressure for 20–30 cycles. If that fails, a closet auger ($15–$25) — different shape from a drain snake, designed to navigate the toilet's curved trap without scratching the porcelain. Don't use a regular drain snake on a toilet; it marks up the bowl.

## When the pattern says "main line"

Stop DIY and call a plumber when you see any of these:

- **Multiple drains slow at once.** Kitchen AND bathroom both draining poorly, or a single floor's worth of fixtures all affected.
- **Flushing the toilet causes the tub to bubble or gurgle.** Classic sign of a blockage downstream of the branch drains.
- **Sewage backing up into the shower, tub, or floor drain.** Stop using water and call.
- **A manual snake can't clear it after 15 minutes** of genuine effort. At that point you're past the trap and into the branch drain or main — territory that often needs a power auger or hydro-jet.

A main-line clearing runs **$300–$600** with a power auger. A camera inspection to see what's causing a recurring clog: **$200–$400**. Both are fair prices when the situation warrants them.

## Why chemical drain cleaners are a trap

Drano, Liquid-Plumr, and similar products:

- **Are corrosive to older pipes.** A pre-1970 galvanized drain or an aging PVC joint can fail after repeated exposure.
- **Don't work on hair or grease** — the two most common real clogs. They're formulated for soap-scum thinning, which is rarely the actual problem.
- **Are dangerous in the follow-up.** If the drain cleaner didn't work and you unscrew the P-trap, that caustic liquid is now a hazard to your skin and eyes.
- **Complicate the plumber's job** if you do end up calling one. The first thing they need to know is what's been poured down the drain.

Mechanical clearing (plunger, snake) is cheaper over time, works better on real clogs, and doesn't create follow-up hazards. Skip the chemicals.

## DIY cost math

A single drain clog cleared by a plumber: **$150–$400** (trip charge + two-hour minimum + sometimes a "drain-clearing premium"). The same clog cleared with a $20 snake: **$20 once**, then **$0 per future clog**.

Budget the $20 and never think about a plumber call-out for a simple clog again. When the pattern genuinely calls for a pro — main-line backup, recurring clogs, sewage — the snake won't solve it and a plumber is worth the money.

## When a plumber is actually the right call

- Recurring clogs at the same fixture within a year (points to a line issue, not a trap issue).
- Any sign of a main-line backup (see list above).
- A clog you can't reach because the cleanout is buried or inaccessible.
- An older home where you suspect a cast-iron or galvanized line is collapsing from corrosion.

For those scenarios, see our broader breakdown of [when to pay for a plumber](/costs/plumber-hourly), and our checklist for [avoiding an overpriced quote](/advice/signs-of-overpriced-quote) when the job exceeds a standard snaking.

## The one-line version

Own the $20 snake. Skip the chemicals. Call the plumber when multiple fixtures fail at once — and only then.
