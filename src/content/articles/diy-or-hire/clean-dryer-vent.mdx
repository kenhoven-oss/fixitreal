For most homes with a wall-vent dryer setup, **cleaning the vent yourself is a 45-minute job that costs $25–$50 in tools and pays for itself the first time you avoid the $100–$200 service call**. Annual DIY cleaning maintains airflow, cuts drying time, reduces fire risk, and tells you when something more serious (damaged duct, blocked roof vent) needs a professional. The job is well within DIY range for any homeowner comfortable disconnecting the dryer and using a shop vac.

## When DIY is the right call

- **Wall-vent terminating outside within 20 feet.** Most ranch homes, most basement laundry rooms.
- **You have a shop vac** (or willing to buy / borrow one — see our [shop vac buying guide](/tools/best-shop-vacs-for-water-cleanup); the same vac works for dryer-vent work).
- **You&apos;re comfortable** pulling the dryer away from the wall, disconnecting the duct clamp, and reconnecting it.

## When to hire instead

- **Vent run exceeds 25 feet.** DIY brush kits lose effectiveness past that length.
- **Roof-terminating vent without easy ladder access.**
- **Vent runs through finished walls / ceilings** where you can&apos;t see what you&apos;re cleaning.
- **Shared vent with another unit** (some condos / townhouses).
- **You suspect duct damage** — crushed sections, gaps, animal nests visible. The pro can diagnose and replace.
- **The dryer has actively shown overheating signs** (smell of burning, plastic warping, repeated thermal-fuse blow). Fix it now; risk-tolerance is zero.

## The DIY job, start to finish

**What you&apos;ll need:**

- Dryer vent brush kit with extendable rods (sold as &ldquo;dryer vent cleaning kit&rdquo;) — $20-$40
- Shop vac with a 1.25" or 2.5" hose
- Phillips screwdriver
- Painters tape
- A drop cloth or newspaper (lint everywhere)
- Optional: cordless drill (most brush kits attach to a standard drill for rotational cleaning)

**Steps:**

**1. Unplug the dryer.** Gas dryers: also shut off the gas valve behind the dryer.

**2. Pull the dryer away from the wall.** Most dryers slide out on their own feet; you may need to walk it 1–2 feet to access the back. Caution: tight kink in the venting hose can crush the duct.

**3. Disconnect the duct from the dryer.** Loosen the clamp (usually a small Phillips screw or a hose clamp). Pull the foil/aluminum duct off the dryer&apos;s exhaust port.

**4. Disconnect the duct from the wall.** Same clamp setup. The duct should now be free at both ends.

**5. Inspect the duct.** Crushed or kinked sections, holes, separations at joints, animal nests, foil duct that&apos;s collapsed inward. If you see any of those, replace the duct ($15-$25 at any hardware store). Use **rigid or semi-rigid aluminum**, not the white plastic/vinyl &ldquo;slinky&rdquo; type (it&apos;s a code violation in most jurisdictions and a fire hazard).

**6. Brush-clean the wall vent.** Insert the dryer vent brush into the wall opening. Twist and push by hand (or with a drill on the rotational kits), adding extension rods as you go. Push until you reach the outside vent cap. Pull back, rotating, catching lint on the brush bristles.

**7. Vacuum the wall vent.** Use the shop vac with the smallest available hose attachment to suck out any loose lint the brush dislodged. Vacuum from both the inside opening and outside vent cap.

**8. Clean the outside vent cap.** Check the flap on the exterior — it should open freely under air pressure. Many caps have a screen behind them that&apos;s clogged with lint or even nests; remove and clean.

**9. Brush-clean the duct itself.** Even if the duct is intact, lint builds up inside it. Run the brush through it the same way.

**10. Vacuum behind the dryer and inside the lint trap housing.** Lint accumulates on the floor behind the dryer over time. Open the lint trap, vacuum inside the housing. Some dryers have a removable back panel for accessing the blower assembly — vacuum that too if you can.

**11. Reconnect the duct** on both ends. Test by running the dryer empty for 1-2 minutes; feel the outside vent cap for strong, warm airflow. Weak airflow means something&apos;s still blocked.

**12. Slide the dryer back.** Don&apos;t crush the new ductwork — leave 3-6 inches of slack at the back.

Total time: 30–60 minutes for a first attempt; 20–30 minutes once you&apos;ve done it before.

## Common mistakes

- **Using plastic / vinyl &ldquo;slinky&rdquo; duct.** Code violation in most jurisdictions; flammable; collapses easily, restricting airflow. Use rigid or semi-rigid aluminum.
- **Leaving the duct kinked or compressed against the wall** when sliding the dryer back. Reduces airflow even when clean; can cause overheating.
- **Skipping the outside vent cap.** A bird&apos;s nest behind the cap is a common cause of &ldquo;why doesn&apos;t the dryer dry anymore?&rdquo;
- **Cleaning the lint trap but not the housing.** The trap catches most lint, but fine lint slips through into the housing and accumulates. Open and vacuum that area annually.
- **Running the dryer with disconnected venting.** Don&apos;t test the dryer with the duct disconnected and lying on the floor — you&apos;ll get warm humid air everywhere, and a gas dryer creates a CO risk.

## Red flags during cleaning

- **Significant lint volume** (a grocery-bag-full) suggests you&apos;ve been overdue for years. Plan to do it twice yearly going forward.
- **Burnt smell** on the lint. Means the dryer has been operating hot — call a technician to inspect the heating element and thermal fuses.
- **Damaged duct sections.** Crushed, cracked, separated, missing tape at joints — replace.
- **Animal nests, dead pests, or significant debris.** Common with long runs and unscreened outdoor caps. Add a screen to the outdoor cap (or replace the cap with a properly-screened model).
- **Vent terminating in the attic or crawl space.** Code violation; needs re-routing to terminate outside. See our [bathroom exhaust fan vent under porch roof guide](/home-inspection-repairs/bathroom-exhaust-fan-vent-under-porch) — same principle applies for dryer vents.

## What I&apos;d do

DIY annually, ideally in spring when you&apos;re doing other home maintenance. Buy a quality brush kit ($30-$50) the first time; reuse for 5+ years. Combine with a 5-minute vacuum behind the washer at the same time — same effort, both jobs done.

If the vent run is long, complex, roof-terminating, or accesses the attic / crawl space — hire a professional every 2-3 years and DIY in between. Their rotary equipment handles long runs better than handheld brushes.

For an inspection report flagging a clogged or damaged vent: have it fixed before listing. It&apos;s a $100-$200 spend that removes a real fire-risk callout from the buyer&apos;s repair request.
