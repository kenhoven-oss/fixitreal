Re-routing a bathroom exhaust fan that&apos;s venting into an attic, soffit, or under a porch roof typically costs $300–$1,200 by route and scope. Through-wall re-routes are cheapest at $300–$700. Through-roof re-routes run $500–$1,200 because they need a roofer. DIY parts cost is $40–$120 for either, with most of the savings on the through-wall option since the through-roof penetration shouldn&apos;t be DIY for most homeowners.

## What you&apos;re paying for

A bath-fan vent re-route has four cost drivers:

1. **The duct itself.** Insulated rigid or semi-rigid duct ($1.50–$3.50 per foot), elbows ($4–$8 each), connectors and clamps ($10–$25 total).
2. **The exterior cap.** Wall cap with damper ($20–$40), roof cap with flashing ($40–$90).
3. **Labor.** Through-wall is typically 2–4 hours of handyman or HVAC tech time ($150–$500). Through-roof adds 1–2 hours of roofer time ($150–$400).
4. **Patching.** Drywall patch, paint touch-up, exterior siding/shingle repair as applicable ($50–$200).

## What it should NOT cost

- **Don&apos;t pay $1,500+ for a simple through-wall re-route.** That&apos;s contractor padding. Get a second quote.
- **Don&apos;t pay to "extend the existing duct further into the soffit."** That&apos;s the same problem with more parts billed.
- **Don&apos;t pay for "mold testing" on an unrelated vent issue unless there&apos;s visible damage.** A vent terminating where it shouldn&apos;t is a fix-it issue; testing is for cases with visible mold.

## Cost variations to know

**Distance from bathroom to exit point.** Long horizontal duct runs (>10 feet) reduce fan effectiveness and add parts cost. Budget +$50–$150 for runs over 10 feet.

**Wall material.** Vinyl siding is the cheapest to cut and patch. Fiber-cement and stucco add $80–$200 to labor because the contractor needs special blades and patching skill. Brick or stone exterior walls can add $200–$500 because of the masonry work involved.

**Multi-story homes.** Routing the duct through a finished second-floor ceiling adds drywall work that doesn&apos;t exist on a single-story home. Budget +$150–$400 for the extra drywall patch and paint.

**Roof age.** Cutting into a roof less than 5 years old is straightforward. Cutting into a 15+ year old roof sometimes pushes the contractor to recommend partial re-shingling around the penetration — fair, since you don&apos;t want to be replacing the roof in 3 years and discovering the bath-fan flashing was the weak point.

## Service-call minimums to budget for

- **Handyman or HVAC tech:** $80–$150 minimum trip.
- **Roofer:** $200–$500 minimum trip (some roofers won&apos;t come out for less than $400–$500).

If you&apos;re combining roof-cap install with any other roof work, bundle the trip — the minimum-trip fee gets amortized.

## When DIY is reasonable

- **Through-wall re-routes on vinyl siding.** Straightforward; the wall cap is forgiving on installation.
- **Adjusting duct routing in an unfinished basement or attic.** No drywall patching needed.
- **Replacing a wall cap that&apos;s failed.** Pull old, caulk, install new.

## When to hire

- **Roof penetrations.** Pay the roofer. A bad flashing job becomes a roof leak in 2–5 years and is much more expensive to remediate than just doing it right the first time.
- **Fiber-cement, brick, or stone exterior walls.** Special tools and skill; most homeowners damage the wall more than they save in labor.
- **Bathrooms above finished ceilings.** Drywall patches on finished ceilings are visible if done poorly.

## What I&apos;d do

For a single-story home with vinyl or wood siding and a bath fan that&apos;s easily accessible from an unfinished attic: DIY a through-wall re-route for $50–$100 in parts and a half day of work. The skill ceiling is moderate; the tools are common.

For anything roof-related, or for second-story bathrooms: hire it out. Get two quotes from HVAC contractors (not just one handyman). Ask each contractor to itemize the duct work and the patching separately so you can see the labor split. Quotes within 20% of each other are normal; an outlier is the one to question.
