An electrician service call in 2026 typically costs $80–$200 just for the trip — covering the first 30–60 minutes — then $75–$150/hour for any work beyond that. For a single small fix (one outlet, one breaker, one fixture), expect $150–$300 total. Bundling multiple small items into one visit is the biggest lever you have on cost, because the trip fee gets amortized across the work.

## What you&apos;re paying for

A service call has three layers:

1. **Trip / dispatch fee.** Flat charge to send a truck and a licensed electrician to your address. $80–$200 typical. Some companies call this a "service call minimum" or "diagnostic fee."
2. **Labor.** After the trip-covered window expires, billed hourly at $75–$150 ($150–$250+ in expensive metros like NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle). Some companies use a flat-rate book instead of hourly — they look up your job in a pricing book and quote a fixed price.
3. **Materials.** Marked up 20–50% over retail at most companies. A $15 outlet from the truck stock can show up on the invoice at $25–$40.

## Typical service-call costs by metro

Pricing varies more than most homeowners realize:

| Region | Trip / dispatch fee | Hourly rate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rural / small metro | $60–$110 | $60–$95 |
| Mid-size metro | $90–$150 | $80–$120 |
| Major metro | $130–$200 | $100–$150 |
| High-cost coastal (NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle, DC) | $180–$300 | $150–$250 |

Use the local range as a sanity check on your quote, not a target — actual trade rates vary by company, season, and demand.

## Common job totals

| Job | Time | Typical total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Replace one outlet | 20–40 min | **$120–$220** |
| Replace one GFCI outlet | 20–40 min | **$140–$240** |
| Troubleshoot tripping breaker (no replacement) | 30–60 min | **$150–$300** |
| Replace a breaker | 30–60 min | **$170–$320** |
| Hardwire one smoke detector | 20–40 min | **$130–$220** |
| Install one new light fixture (existing wiring) | 45–90 min | **$200–$380** |
| Diagnose dead circuit (single trip) | 60–120 min | **$250–$450** |
| Install one new outlet (new wiring) | 90–180 min | **$300–$550** |

These assume one visit and no panel work. Anything involving the main panel, sub-panels, or service-entrance wiring tends to start at $500 minimum and climbs from there.

## How to keep the cost down

**Bundle.** If you have a tripping breaker AND a flickering fixture AND a GFCI you want added, schedule them all in one visit. You pay one trip fee, and the labor stays at the hourly rate. Three separate visits = three separate trip fees, which can be $400+ in extra cost.

**DIY the diagnostics.** When you call, describe the issue precisely:

- Which breaker is tripping?
- When? On startup of a specific appliance, or randomly?
- Which outlets / fixtures are dead?
- What have you already tested (other GFCI outlets, the reset button, plugging into different outlets)?

A clear description shaves 15–30 minutes off the diagnostic time — real money at $100–$150/hour.

**Ask about flat-rate vs. hourly.** Both have trade-offs. Flat-rate gives certainty but typically prices higher to absorb the risk. Hourly gives you the upside if the job is quick but exposure if it&apos;s slow. For a clearly-defined small job, flat-rate is usually fine. For diagnostics, hourly is usually cheaper.

**Time the call.** Mon–Thu, 8 AM – 4 PM, gets the standard rate. Friday afternoon, weekend, and holiday calls run 1.5–2×.

## Red flags on the invoice

- **"Diagnostic fee" added on top of the trip fee** for a job where you already knew what was wrong. If you called for an outlet replacement, you shouldn&apos;t be billed for diagnostics.
- **Marked-up materials at 3–5× retail.** A $5 wire nut shouldn&apos;t appear on the invoice at $25. 1.5–2× retail is normal; 3×+ is padding.
- **Vague labor line items.** "Electrical work — $X" isn&apos;t an invoice. Real invoices list the work done.
- **Refusing to itemize.** A licensed electrician should be able to break trip / labor / materials cleanly. If they won&apos;t, that&apos;s a sign of a flat-rate book where the prices are inflated.

## When you don&apos;t need to call

A surprising number of "electrician problems" are DIY. Tripping breakers are sometimes just overloaded circuits. Dead outlets often trace back to a tripped GFCI elsewhere in the house. Flickering lights are sometimes just a loose bulb. Before paying $150–$300 for a service call:

- See [breaker keeps tripping: DIY checks](/advice/breaker-keeps-tripping)
- See [GFCI outlet keeps tripping or won&apos;t reset](/advice/gfci-outlet-keeps-tripping)
- Reset every GFCI in the house — kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoor, basement.

## What I&apos;d do

For a single small electrical issue, expect $150–$300 all-in, and don&apos;t accept less than three things to make the trip worthwhile if you can help it. For diagnostics on a recurring problem, get the electrician to commit to either a flat-rate diagnostic + repair price or an hourly cap before they start.

Bundle aggressively. The biggest savings aren&apos;t in negotiating the trip fee down — they&apos;re in adding more line items to the same visit.
