A handyman in 2026 typically charges $60–$125 per hour in most US metros, with major-metro premium rates reaching $150–$200. For small odd jobs that don't require a licensed contractor, this is the right pricing tier — cheaper than licensed trades, but with a real minimum charge and real overhead. Knowing what you should pay, what counts as fair scope, and when a job exceeds handyman territory keeps you from overpaying or hiring the wrong person.

## Typical hourly rates by region

| Region | Hourly rate |
|---|---|
| Rural / small metro | $40–$75 |
| Mid-size metro | $60–$110 |
| Major metro | $85–$150 |
| High-cost coastal | $125–$200 |

Use the local range as a sanity check. Within any single metro, individual handymen vary 30–50% in rates. The highest-priced isn't always the best; the cheapest is often the riskiest.

## What you're paying for

A handyman's hourly rate covers three things:

1. **Labor.** The actual on-site work time, plus prep and cleanup. Higher-skilled handymen — those who can also do basic electrical, plumbing, or finish carpentry — charge at the upper end of the range.
2. **Trip overhead.** Driving to your job, unloading tools, dealing with parking. This is why there's a minimum charge (usually 1–2 hours, $80–$200).
3. **Tool investment + insurance.** A working handyman has $10,000–$30,000 of tools, vehicle expenses, and a liability policy. That's baked into the rate.

These assume the homeowner provides the parts. Markup on parts varies wildly; some handymen mark them up 30–50%, others charge cost.

## When a handyman is the right hire

- Small, defined odd jobs the homeowner doesn't want to do.
- Multi-task days where bundling several items beats hiring specialists.
- Work that doesn't require a permit or licensed trade.
- Cosmetic repair, light carpentry, fixture swaps, drywall patching.

## When to hire a licensed pro instead

- **Anything requiring a permit** in your jurisdiction (electrical inside the panel, gas lines, structural changes, plumbing relocations).
- **Anything that could fail catastrophically** (water heater install, roof work, structural).
- **Work with insurance implications** (smoke alarms in a state with mandatory licensing, electrical that touches the main panel).
- **Larger projects** ($2,000+). Licensed contractors carry better insurance, pull permits, and handle multi-trade coordination.

Some states (California, Florida) require any work above $500 to be done by a licensed contractor, period. Handymen working over that threshold there are unlicensed by definition. Check your state.

## Red flags

- **No insurance** or refusal to provide proof. Uninsured injury on your property is your problem.
- **No written estimate** for jobs over $200. Verbal-only quoting is where price surprise happens.
- **Cash-only**. Means no paper trail and possibly no tax compliance. Insist on check or card.
- **Refusing to itemize** materials vs. labor on the invoice. Means part markup is hidden.
- **Vague hourly tracking.** "About 3 hours" isn't a record. Reputable handymen track and report time accurately.

See [signs of an overpriced quote](/advice/signs-of-overpriced-quote) for the deeper version.

## How to keep the cost down

**Bundle.** One trip fee absorbs multiple small jobs. The biggest single lever on cost.

**Supply your own parts.** Buy the faucet, the disposal, the fan, the door hardware yourself. Avoid the 30–50% parts markup.

**Be ready when they arrive.** Furniture moved, area cleared, parts on site, decision-questions already answered. Handyman time is metered.

**Schedule weekdays.** After-hours and weekend rates run 1.25–1.5×.

**Three quotes** for anything over $400. Within a metro, three handyman quotes for the same job often span 40%+.

## What I'd do

For a list of 4–5 small items: bundle them into one half-day visit and budget $300–$500 total. Provide the parts. Get a written estimate first. For one bigger item (>$500), get three handyman quotes plus at minimum one licensed-contractor quote — the handyman might be the right hire, but you want to verify by comparison, not assumption.

For anything that touches code-regulated work, hire the licensed pro. The cost difference is real, but so is the cost of a botched permit or failed inspection at resale.
