A plumber service call in 2026 typically costs $75–$200 just for the trip, covering the first 30–60 minutes of on-site time. After that, expect $85–$175/hour for residential work, with emergency and after-hours work at 1.5–2×. For a single small fix (one leaky faucet, one stuck disposal), expect $150–$350 total. Bundling multiple small items into one visit is the most reliable way to reduce per-job cost, because the trip fee gets amortized.

## What you&apos;re paying for

A plumber service call has three components:

1. **Trip / dispatch fee.** $75–$200. Covers truck cost, dispatch overhead, and the first 30–60 minutes of on-site time.
2. **Labor.** $85–$175/hour after the trip-covered window. Many shops use flat-rate pricing books — they look up your job in the book and quote a fixed price regardless of how long it takes.
3. **Materials.** Marked up 30–60% over retail at most companies. A $10 supply line shows up at $20–$30; a $30 garbage disposal flange shows up at $60–$90.

## Typical service-call costs by metro

| Region | Trip / dispatch fee | Hourly rate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rural / small metro | $60–$110 | $65–$100 |
| Mid-size metro | $90–$150 | $85–$140 |
| Major metro | $130–$200 | $110–$175 |
| High-cost coastal (NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle, DC) | $180–$300 | $150–$250+ |

Use the local range as a sanity check on your quote, not a target.

## Common job totals

| Job | Time | Typical total |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Unclog a single drain (kitchen, bath sink) | 20–40 min | **$130–$280** |
| Snake a main line clog | 60–120 min | **$250–$500** |
| Replace one faucet (cartridge or full unit) | 60–90 min | **$200–$400** |
| Replace a toilet (you supply the toilet) | 60–90 min | **$280–$500** |
| Unjam a garbage disposal | 15–30 min | **$120–$220** |
| Replace a garbage disposal (you supply) | 45–90 min | **$200–$400** |
| Fix a leaking shutoff valve | 30–60 min | **$160–$320** |
| Replace water heater (40-50 gal gas) | 2–3 hours | **$900–$1,800** |
| Diagnose a slow leak | 30–60 min | **$150–$300** |

These assume one visit, no permit pulls, and no wall-opening repairs.

## How to keep the cost down

**Bundle small jobs.** Schedule the leaky faucet, the slow drain, and the running toilet in one visit. One trip fee, three jobs at hourly rates = real savings versus three trips at $150 each.

**DIY the diagnostics.** When you call, describe precisely:

- Which fixture is affected?
- When does it leak / clog / drip? Constantly, or only when used?
- How fast is the drain / leak?
- What have you tried (plunger, snake, hex wrench, reset button)?

A clear problem description saves 15–30 minutes of plumber time.

**Try the obvious DIY first.** Most "plumber problems" are DIY-able with a $5 wrench and a 10-minute YouTube video:

- A humming garbage disposal is a jam → [10-minute DIY fix](/advice/garbage-disposal-humming-not-spinning).
- A leaking toilet at the base is usually a wax-ring failure → [DIY-vs-pro guide](/diy-or-hire/toilet).
- A clogged drain is usually solvable with a plunger or hand snake → [drain snake buying guide](/tools/best-drain-snakes-for-homeowners).

**Time the call.** Weekdays 8 AM–4 PM get standard rates. Friday afternoon, weekend, holiday, and overnight calls run 1.5–2×.

**Confirm pricing before the work starts.** Many shops have a written estimate process — they diagnose, quote, then start work after you approve. Always get the number in writing before the plumber starts cutting pipe.

## Red flags on the invoice

- **Flat-rate "premium" pricing** that&apos;s 40–80% above the hourly equivalent. Some shops use flat-rate books from the late 2010s that haven&apos;t been re-calibrated since labor rates went up, but most that have premium pricing baked in are profit-padding.
- **Materials marked 3–5× retail.** A $5 PEX coupling shouldn&apos;t appear at $25. Some markup is fair; 5× is padding.
- **Pressure to upgrade.** "Your water heater is 8 years old, you should replace it now" with no leak, no failure, no efficiency concern — this is a sales pitch dressed as advice.
- **Refusal to itemize.** A licensed plumber can break trip / labor / materials cleanly. Refusal signals padding.

For deeper coverage, see [signs of an overpriced quote](/advice/signs-of-overpriced-quote).

## When you don&apos;t need to call

A meaningful percentage of plumber service calls would have been solved by:

- A plunger (cup-shaped for sinks, flange-shaped for toilets). See our [plunger buying guide](/tools/best-plungers-for-homeowners).
- A $25 hand snake. See [drain snake buying guide](/tools/best-drain-snakes-for-homeowners).
- The hex wrench under the disposal. See [garbage disposal humming fix](/advice/garbage-disposal-humming-not-spinning).
- The reset button on a tankless water heater (when GFCI-protected).

Try the obvious first. A $20 tool that saves a $250 service call pays for itself on the first use.

## What I&apos;d do

For one small fix, expect $150–$350 all-in. For two or three small fixes bundled, the per-job cost drops by 30–40% relative to separate calls. For emergencies (active leak, sewage backup, no water), call right away — the 1.5–2× premium is still cheaper than the water damage from waiting.

For anything diagnostic-heavy ("there&apos;s a slow drip somewhere"), get the plumber to commit to either a capped hourly diagnostic ($100–$200 max for diagnosis) or a fixed diagnostic-plus-repair price before they start.
