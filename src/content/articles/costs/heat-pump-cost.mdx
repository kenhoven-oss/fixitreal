Heat pumps have become the default HVAC choice for new installs in most US climates. Federal tax credits, lower lifecycle cost, and modern cold-climate performance have changed the math. Here's what a fair quote looks like in 2026.

<QuickAnswer>
A standard 2.5–3 ton **air-source heat pump** runs **$5,500–$12,000 installed** in 2026 — outdoor unit, indoor air handler, labor, permit, and refrigerant. **Federal tax credits return 30% up to $2,000** through 2032, often stacking with utility rebates of $500–$2,500. Net cost after credits typically lands at **$4,000–$8,500** for most homes. **Cold-climate heat pumps** (rated below 0°F) cost $1,000–$2,500 more. **Dual-fuel systems** (heat pump + gas furnace backup) add $2,500–$4,500 over heat-pump-only. Most quotes above **$14,000 on a basic 3-ton install** warrant a second quote.
</QuickAnswer>

## What you're actually paying for

### Unit

- **Standard air-source heat pump (SEER 16 / HSPF 9):** $2,800–$4,800 wholesale (outdoor + indoor combined).
- **Cold-climate heat pump (rated to -15°F):** $4,200–$6,500 wholesale. Required for northern climates if you want heat-pump-only.
- **Variable-speed inverter unit (SEER 18+ / HSPF 10+):** $4,500–$7,500 wholesale. Quieter, more efficient.
- **Geothermal heat pump:** $15,000–$35,000 installed. Different category — uses ground loops, not air-source. Worth a separate quote.

Contractors mark up 1.3×–1.7× over wholesale.

### Labor

- **Standard install (existing ducts, basic location):** 1–2 day job. $1,800–$3,500 labor.
- **Conversion from furnace + AC to heat pump:** adds 4–8 hours for electrical work (typically 240V circuit) + venting closure. $600–$1,500 additional.
- **Dual-fuel system install (heat pump + gas furnace):** $3,000–$5,500 total labor.

### Code-required items

- **Permit:** $100–$300.
- **Outdoor pad + lineset:** $300–$700.
- **Electrical work:** new disconnect, sometimes a new 240V circuit. $300–$900 depending on panel availability.
- **Condensate drain + safety switch:** $80–$200.

### Tax credits and rebates (the math)

- **Federal Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit:** 30% of qualified install up to **$2,000** through 2032. Requires SEER2 16+ and meeting CEE tier 2 efficiency.
- **Inflation Reduction Act Home Energy Rebate:** up to **$8,000** for income-qualified households. Administered by states; check your state energy office for status.
- **Utility rebates:** $500–$2,500 in many markets. Some require pre-approval.
- **State tax credits:** vary widely. NY, MA, CO, OR, WA offer additional credits.

A $9,000 install in NY with federal + utility rebates often nets to **$5,500–$6,500 out of pocket**.

## Cold-climate heat pumps — what changed

Through about 2015, heat pumps lost most of their efficiency below 32°F and stopped producing useful heat around 25°F. That's no longer true. Modern cold-climate heat pumps (CCHPs):

- Maintain **100% rated heating capacity at 5°F**.
- Continue producing useful heat down to **-15°F to -20°F**.
- Use **vapor injection compressors** that work at extreme low temperatures.

For climates with sustained below-zero winters (MN, ND, WI interior, NY upstate, ME, AK):
- **CCHP alone**: works if you accept electric backup running on the coldest nights.
- **Dual-fuel (heat pump + gas furnace)**: heat pump runs above ~30°F; gas furnace runs below. Best of both. Adds $2,500–$4,500 install cost but eliminates winter discomfort risk.

For climates with mild winters (Pacific NW, mid-Atlantic, Southeast): standard heat pump (non-CCHP) is fine and cheaper.

## What inflates the bill

- **"Mandatory" surge protectors and other small add-ons** padding the quote 5–10%. Real value debatable; not required.
- **Refrigerant priced 2–3× wholesale.** Same problem as AC quotes — a $30/lb refrigerant billed at $120/lb adds $400–$800 to the bill on initial charge.
- **Labor billed at "installer + helper" rates** when only one person worked. Watch the install team size.
- **"Premium" lineset insulation** marked up 4×. Insulation is real but should add $100–$200, not $600.
- **Tax-credit "paperwork fee"** of $200–$400 for filing the credit forms. The forms take 15 minutes to fill out — the contractor doesn't file them, you do at tax time.

## A fair quote breakdown

A typical 3-ton SEER 16 / HSPF 9 air-source heat pump, like-for-like replacement for an existing furnace + AC:

- Unit (matched outdoor + indoor): $4,200
- Install labor: $2,400
- Permit: $175
- Electrical (existing 240V available): $300
- Pad + lineset + materials: $500
- Disposal: $100
- **Total: $7,675** (range: $7,000–$9,500 acceptable before credits)
- **After federal credit ($2,000):** $5,675
- **After utility rebate ($1,000 typical):** $4,675

Add $1,500–$2,500 for a cold-climate unit. Add $3,000+ for dual-fuel with gas furnace backup.

## When to keep the existing furnace + AC

- **Both furnace and AC are under 8 years old** — replacement cost won't recoup before they'd naturally die.
- **Natural gas is unusually cheap** in your market AND electricity unusually expensive (Wyoming, parts of LA, OK, KS) — operating cost may favor gas.
- **No 240V capacity in the electrical panel** AND panel upgrade adds $3,000–$5,000.

When the heat pump is the clear right answer:

- **AC is dying anyway** — adding heat pump function adds little to the AC replacement cost.
- **Federal + state + utility incentives bring the install below the furnace+AC alternative.**
- **You want to electrify** for climate / energy independence / future-proofing.
- **Switching from oil or propane heat** — heat pump dramatically beats both on operating cost.
