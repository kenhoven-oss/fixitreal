When a plumber tells you "we'll find the leak" and quotes a separate diagnostic fee, this is what that money pays for and what's a fair number for the job. Real ranges in 2026.

<QuickAnswer>
Professional water leak detection in the U.S. typically runs **$200–$800** for diagnostics alone, depending on where the leak is:

- **Visible / accessible:** $150–$300
- **Inside a wall or ceiling:** $250–$450
- **Slab leak (under the foundation):** $350–$650
- **Service-line leak (between meter and house):** $400–$900
- **Emergency / after-hours:** 1.5×–2× standard rates

Repair is billed separately. Many plumbers credit some of the diagnostic fee toward repair if you hire them for the fix — **ask before you sign.** A written report with location, method, and readings should be standard.

## What you're actually paying for

A plumber walking in with a flashlight is not the same service as a leak-detection specialist arriving with a truck full of equipment. The price gap reflects:

- **Acoustic listening gear.** Microphones amplify the high-frequency hiss of water escaping a pressurized line. A pro can localize a wall or slab leak inside a square foot with one of these in 20–40 minutes.
- **Thermal imaging.** Cold streaks show on a cold-line leak; warm spots show on a hot-line slab leak. Modern thermal cameras run $4,000–$15,000 — not what a generalist carries.
- **Pinless moisture meters.** Cheap to buy ($30 consumer; $400 pro grade) but tell the technician whether a suspect wall is actually wet before any cut is made.
- **Tracer gas equipment.** Pressurizes a depressurized line with helium-hydrogen blend; a surface sensor detects where the gas escapes. Used on hard-to-find service-line leaks. Specialty rental gear.
- **Time and skill.** A technician who has localized 500 slab leaks moves faster than a plumber doing his first this year. You're paying for the experience curve.

A homeowner could buy a moisture meter and a thermal phone attachment for $250 total. What's harder to buy is the experience that interprets the readings.

## Typical U.S. ranges by leak type

<PullQuote>The first question to ask a leak-detection company is not "how much" — it's "what tools do you bring." The answer tells you whether you're paying for diagnosis or for guessing.</PullQuote>

### Visible or accessible leak — $150–$300

The leak is in a fixture, supply line, water heater, appliance hose, or anything else you can see by opening a cabinet. The plumber confirms the source, gives you a repair estimate, and you decide. Most generalists handle this in under an hour. If the company charges a flat service-call fee that includes the first hour (see our [plumber service call cost guide](/costs/plumber-service-call)), the visit may cost no more than a standard service call.

### Wall or ceiling leak — $250–$450

Drywall is wet but the source isn't visible. The technician runs a moisture meter across the suspect area, maps the readings, and confirms with thermal imaging. If a small inspection cut is needed, expect a 4-inch hole rather than a 2-foot one. Includes the diagnostic report.

### Slab leak — $350–$650

A leak in the pressurized water lines under or through the concrete foundation. Acoustic and thermal tools localize it without breaking the slab. Time on site is 2–3 hours. The diagnostic itself is the cheap part — repair (rerouting, spot repair, or full repipe) is what gets expensive. Get the leak located before you let anyone bid the repair.

### Underground service-line leak — $400–$900

The leak is between the meter and the house, buried under lawn or hardscape. Acoustic correlators and ground microphones can pinpoint it within a few feet without trenching the whole yard. Tracer gas may be added on tough cases. The most expensive diagnostic category because of the gear and the time, but always cheaper than digging the wrong spot.

### Emergency / after-hours surcharge

1.5×–2× standard rates for evening, weekend, or overnight. A leak that's actively damaging the house may justify the premium; a confirmed leak you can isolate at the main can wait until morning. See our [pipe-burst first 10 minutes](/emergency-repairs/pipe-burst-first-10-minutes) playbook for what counts as a real emergency.

## What should be in the bill

A fair leak-detection invoice itemizes:

- **Service call / trip fee.** Usually $50–$150 flat. Sometimes credited to the diagnostic fee.
- **Diagnostic labor.** Hourly or flat-rate. Confirm which up front.
- **Equipment surcharge.** Some specialists bill thermal or tracer-gas use as a separate line item. Reasonable if disclosed.
- **Written report.** Should be no extra charge.
- **Repair estimate** (if the company also does repair). Should also be no extra charge.

What should not be on the bill:

- "Diagnostic fees" that repeat on every visit for the same issue.
- A separate "report fee" or "documentation fee."
- Fuel surcharges stacked on top of a service call fee.
- Anything vague ("inspection," "consultation") without itemized time or scope.

## When the diagnostic fee is worth it

- You've confirmed the meter test shows a leak but can't localize the source.
- The suspected location is inside a wall, ceiling, slab, or yard.
- You're about to authorize a $2,000+ repair and want the source confirmed before anyone cuts.
- You're filing an insurance claim and need a written diagnostic report for the adjuster.

The diagnostic fee is bad value when:

- The leak is obvious and the tech is just confirming. Pay a service call, not a "detection package."
- The company won't quote until they "see it." Most reputable specialists will give a range over the phone for typical leak categories.

## How to compare quotes

Three calls. Ask each:

1. What's the flat or hourly rate for diagnostic?
2. What tools do you bring — moisture meter, thermal, acoustic, tracer gas?
3. Is the fee credited toward repair if I hire you for the fix?
4. Is a written report included?

You'll usually see one company come in obviously cheaper because they don't bring acoustic gear, and one obviously more expensive because they do leak detection only. The right pick depends on what category your leak falls into. For a slab leak, pay the premium for the specialist. For a wall leak with obvious drywall damage, the generalist plumber with a moisture meter is fine.

## After the leak is found

The diagnostic ends with a report. The next decisions are:

- **Repair** through the same company (often the lowest-friction path) or a separate plumber with the report in hand.
- **Insurance claim.** Call within 24–48 hours; the Insurance Information Institute notes that timely notification is part of most "sudden and accidental" claim qualifications.
- **Drying and remediation** before any drywall closes. See [drywall damage after a leak](/advice/drywall-damage-after-a-leak) for the right order.
- **Sensor installation.** A $15 puck-style sensor at the now-known weak point catches the next leak at minute one. Our [best water leak detectors](/tools/best-water-leak-detectors) buying guide breaks down which type fits which home.

## What this isn't

This guide covers leak *detection* — finding where the leak is. The repair is separate, and the cost varies enormously by type (slab leak repair: $1,500–$4,500+; service-line repair: $1,500–$5,000+; wall pipe repair: $300–$1,200; fixture-side repairs: $150–$600). Get the diagnostic done first, then bid the repair.
