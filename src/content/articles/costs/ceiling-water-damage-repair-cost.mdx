A wet ceiling is rarely just a wet ceiling. The drywall stain is the visible 10% of the job; the framing, the insulation, the mold check, and the source repair are the rest. Here's what each component actually costs in 2026 and where the bills get inflated.

<QuickAnswer>
**Typical U.S. ceiling water damage repair costs in 2026:**

- **Small patch** (≤ 4 sq ft, smooth ceiling, dry framing): **$250–$500**
- **Medium patch** (5–16 sq ft, popcorn or texture match): **$400–$900**
- **Full ceiling replacement** (one room, smooth, dry framing): **$1,500–$3,500**
- **With wet insulation replacement:** add **$1.50–$3.50/sq ft**
- **With mold remediation** (visible growth): add **$500–$4,000+**
- **Source repair** (the actual plumbing or roof leak): **billed separately, $200–$2,500**

A typical claim — small leak from above, contained ceiling damage, no mold — clears at **$600–$1,500 all-in**. Skip the source repair or rush the drying step, and the same job becomes a $3,000–$8,000 project six months later.

## What you're actually paying for

Ceiling water damage is rarely one line item. A fair quote breaks down at least these components:

### Demolition and tear-out

Removing the damaged drywall, often the insulation above, and disposing of waste. $150–$500 depending on access and disposal fees. Plaster ceilings cost 2–3× more to remove than drywall because of weight and dust.

### Framing inspection and dry-out

Confirming the ceiling joists and any structural framing aren't wet. If they are, fans and dehumidifiers run for 3–7 days to bring moisture below ~17%. Equipment rental: $30–$50/day for a fan, $40–$60/day for a dehumidifier. Often included in a remediation quote; check whether you're paying for them per day or as part of a flat fee.

### Insulation replacement

If the insulation is wet, it has to go. R-30 fiberglass batts cost $1.50–$2.50/sq ft installed; cellulose loose-fill is similar. Spray foam isn't usually replaced because it doesn't absorb water.

### Drywall installation

½-inch drywall on a ceiling is $40–$70 per 4×8 sheet installed, including taping, mudding, and sanding. Tight access (closets, low rooms) bumps cost. Old homes with non-standard joist spacing may need furring or shims, adding labor.

### Texture matching

Smooth ceilings are the cheapest. Popcorn (acoustic), knockdown, orange-peel, and skip-trowel textures are progressively more expensive to match. Skilled texture-match adds $1–$4/sq ft. A small popcorn patch can cost more in texture match than in everything else.

### Primer and paint

Stain-blocking primer ($30–$60 a gallon) is non-negotiable for any water-damaged ceiling. Two coats of finish paint on top. For a typical small patch, materials are $50–$100; labor is $150–$300.

### Source repair

This is separate from ceiling work. A leaky toilet wax ring, a failed shower pan, a roof flashing, a frozen pipe — each has its own cost. Without fixing the source, the ceiling repair is a clock counting down. For the plumbing side, see [our plumber hourly cost guide](/costs/plumber-hourly) and the [pipe burst playbook](/emergency-repairs/pipe-burst-first-10-minutes) for emergency cases.

## When mold remediation enters the bill

A ceiling that stays wet more than 48 hours can grow mold. The EPA's guidance is that materials kept wet beyond that window are at risk; once visible mold appears or musty smell becomes pronounced, mold remediation is part of the project.

Mold remediation is licensed work in many U.S. states. Typical costs by scope:

- **Small (≤ 10 sq ft, surface mold only):** $500–$1,500.
- **Medium (10–100 sq ft, contained area):** $1,500–$3,500.
- **Large (>100 sq ft, multiple rooms, HVAC involvement):** $3,500–$10,000+.

Remediation includes plastic-barrier containment, HEPA-filtered negative-pressure ventilation, removal and bagging of contaminated material, antimicrobial treatment, and post-remediation air testing. It's not optional once mold is visible — homeowners insurance and any future buyer's inspection both look for proper remediation documentation.

The cheapest path is preventing mold by drying properly within 48 hours. Our [drywall damage after a leak](/advice/drywall-damage-after-a-leak) guide covers the right drying order.

## Cost by damage size

<PullQuote>The hourly rate of the contractor isn't what blows budgets. It's the surprise that "the framing is wet too" or "this is popcorn so we need an asbestos test."</PullQuote>

### Small patch — $250–$500

A stain or small section under 4 sq ft. Smooth ceiling, dry framing, source already fixed. One technician, 2–4 hours of work, half a day to return for paint. Most common scenario after a minor toilet leak from above.

### Medium patch — $400–$900

Damage up to 16 sq ft. May include partial joist exposure to confirm dry framing. Often involves texture match. Two visits — one for tear-out and patch, one for paint after the patch cures.

### Full ceiling replacement — $1,500–$3,500

The whole ceiling of one room. Drywall is removed, joists are inspected, insulation is replaced if wet, new drywall is hung, taped, mudded, textured, primed, and painted. Typical bedroom ceiling: 2–3 days of work, smooth finish. Add 30–50% for popcorn or other textured finishes.

### Plaster ceiling — premium pricing

Older homes with plaster (and often lath) ceilings cost 1.5–2.5× drywall replacement. The materials are heavier, the demolition messier, and the skilled-plaster labor pool is smaller. Many homeowners convert plaster to drywall during repair; that's cheaper than matching plaster but changes the historical look.

## What inflates the bill

Watch for these line items — they're sometimes legitimate, sometimes padding:

- **"Asbestos testing required"** — Legitimate on pre-1986 popcorn ceilings. Test cost: $150–$400. If positive, abatement is licensed work and adds $1,500–$5,000+.
- **"HVAC system cleaning"** — Sometimes required if mold has been blowing through ducts. Confirm with a moldy-duct visual or air test before paying $500–$2,000 for whole-system cleaning.
- **"Contents pack-out"** — Moving furniture to a remediation warehouse. Quoted at $1,000–$5,000+. Often unnecessary for contained ceiling damage; ask whether a tarp-and-stay approach works.
- **"Daily equipment fees"** that run for two weeks — Equipment should not be running for two weeks unless framing is severely saturated. Insist on a moisture-reading log before paying past day 5.
- **"Repair credits"** that don't appear — Some remediation companies quote a price that includes "credit toward repair" but never apply it to the final invoice. Get the credit structure in writing.

## The right order to spend the money

1. **Stop the source.** Whether it's a plumbing fix ($200–$2,500) or a roof repair, the leak repair comes first. Patching a wet ceiling above an unfixed leak guarantees a second invoice.
2. **Document everything.** Photos, video, moisture readings, dates. Pre-cleanup documentation is what your insurer's adjuster uses.
3. **Call insurance** before authorizing any non-emergency work. Most policies require notification within 24–48 hours.
4. **Dry to confirmed-dry readings.** Moisture meter on drywall at 12% or below, on framing at 17% or below. Don't close the ceiling on wet readings.
5. **Remediate if mold present.** Don't paint over visible mold. Don't let a contractor talk you out of remediation when it's clearly needed.
6. **Repair the drywall.** Patch or replace based on scope.
7. **Stain-block prime.** Always. Even on a small patch.
8. **Paint** to match the surrounding ceiling.

Skipping or rushing any step is how a $500 problem becomes a $5,000 one.

## DIY vs. hire

A small smooth-ceiling patch under 4 sq ft is reasonable DIY for someone comfortable with drywall. Tools: utility knife, joint compound, mesh tape or self-stick patch, sanding block, stain-blocking primer, ceiling paint. Material cost: $40–$80. Time: 4–6 hours over 2 days.

Anything beyond a small patch — popcorn texture, full ceiling, suspected mold, wet framing, plaster ceiling — is hired work. The skill curve on overhead drywall is steep, and the consequence of a bad patch is visible from every angle of the room.

## Insurance reality

Standard U.S. homeowner policies typically cover ceiling water damage from "sudden and accidental" interior plumbing failures. They typically *exclude*:

- Damage from a leak that ran for weeks before discovery.
- Damage from outside flooding (separate flood policy required).
- Damage from a homeowner's failure to maintain (e.g., obvious roof flashing failure ignored for years).
- The cost of the pipe or roof repair itself — only the resulting water damage.

Documented timely response is what separates a paid claim from a denied one. The Insurance Information Institute notes that timely notification and proper documentation are part of most "covered loss" qualifications.
