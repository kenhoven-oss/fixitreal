Smoke detector replacement is one of the cheapest home-safety repairs you can do. DIY swap of a battery 10-year unit runs $15–$30 per location. A hardwired swap by an electrician runs $105–$210 per location. A full-house refresh on a typical 3-bedroom home runs $80–$300 DIY or $500–$1,000 hired. These ranges reflect 2026 retail and labor pricing across most U.S. metros.

## What you&apos;re paying for

A smoke detector job has three cost components:

1. **The device.** Battery 10-year sealed alarms ($15–$30), 9V battery-replaceable alarms ($10–$20), hardwired alarms ($25–$60), smoke/CO combo units ($35–$60), and smart/connected units ($45–$100).
2. **Labor**, if hired. Electrician minimums apply ($80–$150 for the trip), plus per-device labor ($60–$120 swap, $200–$400 new location).
3. **Permits**, sometimes. Like-for-like swaps almost never need a permit. New hardwired locations sometimes do (low fee, $25–$75).

## Whole-house parts math

For typical home sizes, total parts cost for a complete refresh:

- **2-bedroom apartment / condo:** 3 alarm locations × $20–$30 = $60–$90 DIY
- **3-bedroom ranch:** 5 locations × $20–$30 = $100–$150 DIY
- **4-bedroom two-story:** 7 locations × $20–$30 = $140–$210 DIY
- **5+ bedroom or 3-story:** 9+ locations × $20–$30 = $180–$270 DIY

If switching to smoke/CO combo units (smart in any home with gas, an attached garage, or a fireplace), roughly double parts cost.

## Cost variations to know

**Sealed lithium price varies $15–$30** mostly by brand. The cheapest big-box-brand 10-year sealed unit is roughly equivalent in spec to the mid-tier name-brand units; the premium models add features like voice alerts, smart-home integration, or escape-light functionality.

**Code-required alarm types vary by state.** California, Massachusetts, Oregon, and others now require 10-year sealed lithium units in homes, so a $15 9V unit is technically non-compliant for a sale or new install in those states.

**FHA/VA appraisal-required swaps don&apos;t inflate the cost.** A lender requiring functional alarms before closing doesn&apos;t change the device price — but it does compress the timeline, which can mean paying $100 more for an electrician&apos;s rush trip.

## Service-call minimums

Most electricians charge a flat trip fee (often listed as a "service-call minimum") of $80–$150 in major metros. If you&apos;re only doing one or two smoke alarms, you&apos;re paying that whole minimum on top of the per-device cost. **Bundle the trip:** if you have any other small electrical work to do, line it up for the same visit. Even adding $20–$40 of additional labor work in the same visit is worth it because the trip fee gets amortized.

## When the price might be higher

- **Older homes with unusual ceiling boxes.** Pre-1960s homes sometimes have shallow or non-standard boxes that need adapting; budget extra $20–$60 per location.
- **Hardwired systems with non-standard brand pairings.** If the existing alarms are a discontinued model and you have to switch brands, the electrician may need to splice or re-pigtail the wiring, adding 20–40 minutes per location.
- **Cathedral or vaulted ceilings.** Anything requiring a ladder taller than a 6-foot step ladder adds time; some electricians charge a "high-reach" surcharge.

## What to ask before hiring

1. **"Is the price per alarm or per visit?"** Clarify if quoted lump sum vs. per device.
2. **"What brand and model are you installing?"** Get this in writing. Brand matching matters in hardwired interconnected systems.
3. **"Do you include the battery backup?"** Hardwired alarms still have a 9V or lithium backup. Verify it&apos;s included.
4. **"Do you pull a permit?"** New hardwired locations sometimes require one; like-for-like swaps usually don&apos;t.

## What I&apos;d do

For nearly every homeowner: DIY this. Battery 10-year sealed lithium units in every required location is code-compliant in most states, lasts a decade, and costs less than one electrician trip. Hardwired homes can stay hardwired by buying same-brand replacements and doing the swap with a voltage tester and 30 minutes per unit.

Hire an electrician for: new hardwired locations, aluminum-wired homes, anything requiring panel work, or if you&apos;d simply rather not work on a ladder. The $400–$700 labor on a whole-house job is real but not absurd given the safety stakes.
