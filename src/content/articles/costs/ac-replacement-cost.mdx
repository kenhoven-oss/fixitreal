A new central AC is one of the largest unexpected home expenses most homeowners face. The range is wide, the line items are opaque, and the same job can be quoted at $5,000 by one contractor and $11,000 by another. Here's what the money actually pays for and where to push back.

<QuickAnswer>
A standard 2.5–3 ton central AC replacement runs **$4,500–$9,000 installed** in 2026. The big variables: **SEER rating** (efficiency tier, +$800–$2,500 for high efficiency), **brand** (Goodman/Rheem at the low end, Carrier/Lennox at the high), **ductwork condition** (existing OK = baseline; major repair adds $1,000–$3,500), and **install complexity** (basic swap vs. relocated condenser + new line set). Any quote above **$11,000 on a basic swap** without ductwork or electrical-panel work warrants a second quote. **Permits are required** in most jurisdictions and should be on the invoice.
</QuickAnswer>

## What you're actually paying for

A central AC replacement quote should break down into at least five real line items. If it's a lump sum, ask for itemization.

### Unit (the equipment)

- **Outdoor condenser + indoor coil (matched system):** $2,200–$4,800 wholesale, depending on tonnage and SEER. Contractors mark up 1.3×–1.8× retail.
- **Goodman / Rheem (value tier):** lowest equipment cost, 10-year parts warranty standard.
- **Trane / Carrier / Lennox (premium tier):** $800–$2,000 higher equipment cost, often quieter and slightly more efficient at the same SEER rating.
- **Air handler** (if heat pump or electric backup): $600–$1,500 additional.

### Labor

- **Install labor:** 1–2 day job for an experienced 2-tech crew. $1,500–$3,500 typical. Coastal, urban, and union markets run 40–80% higher.
- **Electrical disconnect** (often required by code on a new condenser pad): $150–$400.

### Code-required items

- **Permit:** $75–$250 in most jurisdictions. Some contractors quote without — that's a red flag.
- **Pad** (concrete or composite for outdoor unit): $50–$200.
- **Line set** (refrigerant lines from condenser to indoor coil): $200–$600 if replaced; reused if in good condition.
- **Condensate drain + safety switch:** $50–$150.

### Materials that often get padded

- **Refrigerant charge** (R-410A or new R-454B): $30–$80/lb wholesale; some contractors bill $150+/lb.
- **Pad delivery surcharge:** real, but usually $50–$100 — not $300.
- **"Haul-away" fee:** $75–$150 fair; should not run $300+.

### Optional add-ons

- **Smart thermostat:** $150–$350 unit + 30 min install.
- **Whole-house surge protector:** $250–$500 install + part.
- **UV light / air purifier:** $300–$700. Real value is debatable; ask if it actually solves a problem in your home.

## Cost by tonnage

AC capacity is measured in tons (each ton = 12,000 BTU). Picking the right size matters more than picking the right brand. Rough sizing:

| Home size (sq ft) | Tonnage | SEER 16 install (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| 600–1,000 | 1.5 ton | $4,200–$5,800 |
| 1,000–1,500 | 2 ton | $4,800–$6,800 |
| 1,500–2,000 | 2.5 ton | $5,800–$8,200 |
| 2,000–2,500 | 3 ton | $6,500–$8,800 |
| 2,500–3,500 | 3.5 ton | $7,500–$9,500 |
| 3,500–5,000 | 4–5 ton | $8,800–$12,000+ |

These assume an existing-ductwork swap with no major surprises. Climate matters — homes in Phoenix, Houston, Atlanta need more capacity per square foot than homes in Seattle or Boston.

A contractor who quotes without doing a Manual J calculation is sizing by rule of thumb. That works for ballpark, but a 30-year-old house with single-pane windows and weak insulation needs different sizing than a 2015 build with the same square footage.

## SEER rating — what's worth paying for

<PullQuote>SEER 16 is the new sweet spot. Below it, you're leaving energy savings on the table; above it, you're paying for efficiency that won't pay back before the unit dies.</PullQuote>

- **SEER 14**: federal minimum in southern states (was the floor; now most regions are SEER2 14.3 = old SEER 15). Buy only if budget is the absolute constraint.
- **SEER 16**: the practical sweet spot. Typically pays back over the AC's life through utility savings; meets most state and federal rebate thresholds.
- **SEER 18–20**: meaningful efficiency gain in hot-summer climates (FL, TX, AZ); marginal in cool climates. Adds $1,500–$2,500 to install.
- **SEER 22–26**: variable-speed inverter units. Quietest, most efficient, $3,000–$6,000 premium. Right pick only for very hot climates with high electricity rates.

The federal Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit refunds 30% of qualified equipment up to $600 for central AC (or $2,000 for heat pumps) through 2032. Many state and utility rebates stack on top — some markets reach $1,500+ combined.

## Existing ductwork — when it's actually a problem

A contractor saying "your ducts are too small" or "you'll need new ductwork" can be a legitimate $2,000–$5,000 line item, or a $3,500 upsell that wasn't needed. The honest test:

- **Existing AC runs fine through current ducts** = ducts likely fine for the same-sized replacement.
- **Going up a tonnage size** = new ducts may genuinely be needed for the larger airflow.
- **Major rooms have been added since the original install** = duct rework legitimate.
- **Ductwork is visibly damaged, disconnected, or crushed** = repair real, get a second opinion on full replacement.

Get a static pressure measurement (a real HVAC contractor will do this with a manometer) before authorizing duct work. If the contractor can't show you the reading, the recommendation is opinion not measurement.

## What inflates the bill

Watch these line items — sometimes legitimate, sometimes padding:

- **"Premium" refrigerant pricing** — $50/lb refrigerant marked up to $150+/lb. A typical residential system holds 4–8 lbs; that's $400–$800 in markup alone.
- **"Crane fee"** for rooftop units — legitimate ($250–$600) if your unit is rooftop; not legitimate if it's slab-mounted.
- **"Disposal fee" multiplied** — haul-away is $75–$150 fair, not $300+.
- **Permit "fee" with a markup** — the permit is what the city charges. Some contractors add their own permit-handling fee on top. Reasonable up to $100; padding above that.
- **Bundled smart-home add-ons** — the smart thermostat is fine, but a $1,500 "smart-home AC upgrade" for a thermostat that retails at $200 is padding.

## What a fair quote looks like

A fair 2026 quote for a typical 3-ton SEER 16 central AC swap, with existing ductwork in good condition:

- Unit (matched outdoor + indoor coil): $3,200
- Install labor: $2,200
- Permit: $125
- Pad + line set + condensate + minor materials: $400
- Disposal: $100
- **Total: $6,025** (range: $5,500–$7,500 acceptable; $8,000+ warrants comparison)

## When repair beats replacement

For a unit under 10 years old:
- Capacitor failure ($150–$350): repair.
- Refrigerant leak in a coil or line ($400–$1,200): repair.
- Compressor failure ($1,400–$3,500): replace, even mid-life — the parts cost approaches replacement cost.

For a unit 12+ years old:
- Any failure over $1,000 in repair cost: replace. The next failure is statistically close.
- A failing condenser fan motor or contactor ($300–$500): repair, but plan for replacement within 2 years.

If the AC is on R-22 refrigerant (phased out, no longer manufactured), replacement is usually the right call on any major repair — R-22 reclamation costs run $100+/lb.
