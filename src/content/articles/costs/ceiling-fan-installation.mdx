Ceiling fan installation cost depends almost entirely on one question: is there already a fan-rated electrical box in the ceiling? Everything else is rounding noise.

<QuickAnswer>
A like-for-like fan replacement runs **$50–$130 DIY** or **$150–$300 hired** assuming a fan-rated box already exists. Installing a fan where there is currently NO ceiling fixture (or only a non-fan-rated light box) runs **$300–$650+ hired** because the box has to be installed and possibly new wiring run. Any quote over **$800** for a straightforward replacement is overpriced.

## The two big cost-driver scenarios

### Scenario A — Replacing an existing ceiling fan

Cheapest and most predictable. The fan-rated box is already there (assuming the existing fan was installed correctly). Electrician unfastens the old fan, disconnects the wires, hangs the new fan, reconnects, balances. **45–90 minutes, $150–$300 in most metros.** DIY-friendly if you're comfortable on a ladder and reading wire colors.

### Scenario B — Installing a fan where there is no fan-rated box

This is where homeowners get sticker-shock from quotes. Two sub-cases:

**B1: There's a light fixture box but it's not fan-rated.** A retrofit fan-rated box (Saf-T-Brace, Westinghouse expandable) can usually be installed *through* the existing hole without cutting drywall — the brace expands between joists and grips. Then the fan goes on. **1.5–3 hours, $300–$500 hired.** Borderline DIY for confident homeowners.

**B2: There's no electrical box at all.** Now we're cutting drywall, running new 14/3 or 14/2 cable from a switch, installing the fan-rated box, patching the drywall. **3–5+ hours, $500–$900+ hired**, often with a permit. Not DIY unless you have real electrical experience.

For most homeowners' "I want a fan in my bedroom that currently has no fan," Scenario B1 is the most likely path.

## DIY vs. hire

Fan replacement is one of the more DIY-friendly electrical jobs because you're working at the fixture, not in the panel. But it's not zero-risk:

- **Killing power matters.** A ceiling fan is connected to the lighting circuit, which is usually 15A and shared with several outlets in adjacent rooms. Throw the breaker AND verify with a non-contact voltage tester. Many homeowners get shocked because they killed the wrong breaker.
- **Weight matters.** Most ceiling fans are 25–40 lbs. Carrying one above your head on a ladder while finding wire nuts in your pocket is awkward. A helper is genuinely useful.
- **Wire-nuts and grounding.** If the wires in the box are unfamiliar (old houses sometimes have cloth-insulated knob-and-tube remnants, or the previous fan was wired with a bonded ground that the new fan doesn't expect), call an electrician.

For first-time DIY: budget 2 hours, $25 in basic tools (voltage tester, wire nuts, electrical tape if you don't already have them), and $80–$200 for the fan. If you've never wired anything overhead before, hire it out for $250–$300 — the safety margin is worth the money.

## What a fair quote should include

A defensible electrician quote for a ceiling fan installation looks like:

1. **The fan model** (or "homeowner-supplied")
2. **Whether a fan-rated box is required** — this is the biggest cost driver, get it specified up front
3. **Service-call fee or hourly rate** plus minimum billed time
4. **Permit responsibility** (electrician pulls, or customer pulls, or no permit required)
5. **Whether new wiring needs to be run** — and from where
6. **A workmanship warranty** — typically 1 year on labor

## Red flags

- **Quote over $800 for a like-for-like replacement.** Something is off in the scope.
- **No mention of whether the existing box is fan-rated.** This is the first question a working electrician asks. If it's not in the quote, the contractor either didn't look or is going to surprise you with an upcharge.
- **"Permit not required" claim without verification.** New wiring runs typically need a permit. A contractor avoiding permits is avoiding inspection — which is for *your* safety.
