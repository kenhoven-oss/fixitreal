When the AC quits in July or the furnace fails in January, the HVAC service call is unavoidable. Here's what fair pricing looks like and the line items most often padded.

<QuickAnswer>
A standard residential HVAC service call in 2026 runs **$100–$250 for the trip + diagnostic fee**, then **$100–$200/hour** after that. Most diagnostics resolve in 30–90 minutes. A typical complete visit (trip + diagnostic + simple repair) lands at **$250–$600 all-in**. **Emergency / after-hours rates** run **1.5×–2.5×** standard. **The diagnostic fee is often credited toward repair** if you hire the same company — always ask up front.
</QuickAnswer>

## What's in the service call fee

The HVAC service call is a bundled charge that includes:

- **Trip / dispatch fee.** Truck operating cost, driving time, and dispatch overhead. $75–$175 typical.
- **First diagnostic time.** First 30–60 minutes on-site. Sometimes included, sometimes billed separately.
- **Initial system inspection.** Refrigerant pressure check (for AC), combustion check (for furnace), basic visual inspection.

Some shops bundle everything into a flat "service call" rate ($150–$250). Others itemize trip + diagnostic ($75 trip + $100 diagnostic = $175). Either is fine; mixing them on the invoice is where padding happens.

## Hourly rate after the first hour

- **Standard weekday hourly:** $100–$180.
- **Premium markets** (NYC, SF, Boston, Seattle): $150–$220.
- **Lower-cost metros** (rural, smaller cities): $80–$130.
- **Specialty work** (commercial, refrigeration, geothermal): adds 20–40%.

## Emergency and after-hours

- **After 5pm weekday or Saturday:** 1.5× standard.
- **Sunday / holiday:** 2× standard.
- **Overnight (10pm–7am):** 2×–2.5× standard.

A $150/hr weekday rate becomes $300–$375/hr at 11pm on a Sunday. Plus the trip fee climbs proportionally. A typical 90-minute overnight emergency visit clears $500–$900 all-in.

## When emergency rates are worth it

- **AC out during a heat wave** with elderly resident, infant, or pets at home.
- **Furnace out below 10°F** with water-pipe freeze risk.
- **Smell of gas** anywhere in the system — gas company or fire department first, then HVAC.
- **Burning smell or smoke** from the unit — kill power at the breaker, then call.

When emergency rates are not worth it:
- AC out on a comfortable evening (60s–70s).
- Furnace out in October when weather is mild.
- Routine repair you've been putting off.

## What inflates the bill

- **Refrigerant priced 2–3× wholesale.** Wholesale R-410A is $25–$40/lb in 2026; some contractors bill $100–$200/lb. A 2-lb top-off should cost $100–$200, not $400.
- **"Diagnostic fee" that repeats on every visit** for the same issue. A real diagnostic should be done once.
- **"System inspection" added after the diagnostic** as a separate line. The diagnostic should include basic inspection.
- **Parts marked up 3×+.** A $40 capacitor billed at $180 is high; at $250 is padding.
- **"Truck stocking fee"** beyond the trip fee. Truck stocking is the trip fee's job.

## How to keep costs down

- **Schedule routine maintenance in the off-season.** April for AC, September for furnace. Same work, lower demand pricing.
- **Bundle issues into one visit.** A second small task during the same visit adds incremental time, not another trip fee.
- **Describe the symptom precisely on the booking call.** "AC not cooling, condenser unit not running" is faster to diagnose than "AC is broken." Saved diagnostic time = saved billable time.
- **Get a written quote before any repair over $400.** Verbal estimates drift; written ones don't.
- **Check for warranty coverage.** Unit under 5 years old? Parts likely still under warranty. The labor isn't, but you can save $100–$400 on the part.

## When to call a different trade

- **Smell of gas** → gas company or fire department first, not HVAC.
- **Electrical breaker tripping repeatedly** → may be an electrical panel issue, not the HVAC unit.
- **Visible water leak around the unit** → may be a plumber's drain issue, not refrigerant.
- **Ductwork problem only** (no HVAC unit problem) → some specialty duct contractors are cheaper than full-service HVAC for duct-only work.
