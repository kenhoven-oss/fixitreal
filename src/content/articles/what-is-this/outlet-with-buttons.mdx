An outlet with TEST and RESET buttons in the middle is a **GFCI outlet (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)**. It detects when electrical current is escaping to ground through a person or other unintended path and shuts off power within 1/40th of a second — fast enough to prevent a fatal shock. They&apos;re code-required by the National Electrical Code in bathrooms, kitchens, laundry, garages, basements, and outdoor outlets. The TEST button verifies it&apos;s working; RESET turns the outlet back on after a trip.

## What it does

A standard outlet provides electricity. A GFCI outlet does that PLUS monitors the current going out versus coming back. If those don&apos;t match — meaning current is leaking somewhere it shouldn&apos;t — the GFCI trips, cutting power.

Where current leaks: through your body if you&apos;re touching a faulty appliance and standing on a wet floor. Through corroded wiring in a damp environment. Through a partially-shorted appliance internal. All of these create the "ground fault" the GFCI is named for.

The trip happens fast — typically 25 milliseconds — and at a low current threshold (around 5 milliamps). That speed and sensitivity is the difference between &ldquo;tingled a little&rdquo; and a fatal shock.

## Where you&apos;ll see them

National Electrical Code requires GFCI protection in:

- **Bathrooms** — every outlet
- **Kitchens** — counter outlets and within 6 feet of a sink
- **Laundry rooms** — outlets near the sink or washing machine
- **Garages and unfinished basements** — all outlets
- **Outdoor outlets** — all
- **Crawl spaces, accessory dwellings, boathouses** — all
- **Wet bar / bar sinks** — within 6 feet

Older homes built before the requirement often have non-GFCI outlets in these locations. Upgrading to GFCI is a code improvement that&apos;s straightforward and cheap. See [Should I replace a GFCI outlet myself?](/diy-or-hire/gfci-outlet).

## The two buttons

**TEST** simulates a ground fault. Press it and the outlet should click off (power cuts to anything plugged in; usually a small LED changes). This is how you verify the outlet is functioning. Press monthly.

**RESET** restores power after a trip. After the GFCI trips (from a real fault or from pressing TEST), press RESET to put it back into normal operation.

If pressing TEST doesn&apos;t cut power, the GFCI is dead. Replace immediately — a non-functional GFCI provides no protection while still working as a regular outlet, the most dangerous failure mode.

## Why yours keeps tripping

Three causes:

**1. Actual fault on the circuit.**
Something on the protected circuit is leaking current. Old appliances are common culprits — refrigerators, dishwashers, freezers with degraded insulation. Outdoor lights with moisture in the fixture. Pool pumps. Even a slightly-shorted hair dryer or curling iron.

To find it: unplug everything on the GFCI&apos;s circuit, reset the GFCI, plug things back in one at a time, waiting 5-10 minutes between each.

**2. Wet outlet.**
GFCI outlets installed outdoors or in damp basements trip when water reaches the terminals. Bubble covers protect against this; a damaged cover or no cover at all is a frequent source of outdoor GFCI trips after rain.

**3. The GFCI itself is failing.**
GFCIs have a 10-25 year service life. As they age, they trip more sensitively — sometimes randomly. If yours is 15+ years old and trips frequently with no identifiable load cause, replace it. The new unit is $15-$25.

For deeper troubleshooting, see [GFCI outlet keeps tripping or won&apos;t reset](/advice/gfci-outlet-keeps-tripping).

## Safe to touch?

Yes — the buttons themselves are safe to press at any time. The outlet face is also safe to touch in normal operation. Don&apos;t insert anything into the slots beyond a plug.

If you can see internal components (cracked housing, exposed wiring), don&apos;t touch — kill the breaker for that circuit and call an electrician.

## Common mistakes

- **Skipping the monthly TEST.** A GFCI that&apos;s failed silently looks fine but provides no protection.
- **Removing a GFCI when "it keeps tripping."** A tripping GFCI is doing its job — finding the actual fault is the right move, not bypassing the protection.
- **Wiring Line and Load backwards** on replacement. A miswired GFCI works as an outlet but doesn&apos;t protect downstream outlets, leaving half a kitchen unprotected. See our [GFCI replacement guide](/diy-or-hire/gfci-outlet) for the correct procedure.
