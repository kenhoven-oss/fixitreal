Water in the cabinet under your bathroom sink almost always traces to one of three sources: a drain connection (P-trap area), a supply line, or a shutoff valve. The five-minute diagnostic below identifies which.

## What it usually means

The connections under a bathroom sink are mechanical: slip-nuts on the drain side, threaded compression on the supply side. Over time, washers compress, supply lines age out, and valves develop seeps. Most under-sink leaks are minor and DIY-friendly.

## Immediate safety concern

Low. The water is clean (from the supply or drain side; not waste backflow). The main risks are slow:

- **Cabinet damage**: MDF cabinet bottoms swell and warp from sitting water. A few days of pooling can warp the cabinet bottom permanently.
- **Mold**: a chronic small leak feeds mold growth in the cabinet over months.
- **Floor damage**: water that overflows the cabinet base seeps under the bathroom floor.

Pull everything out of the cabinet today and dry it. Diagnose the source within a week.

## The 5-minute diagnostic

1. **Pull everything out of the cabinet.** All cleaning supplies, baskets, toilet paper. Dry the cabinet floor and back wall with a towel.
2. **Lay paper towels under each connection point.** One under the P-trap, one under each supply-line connection at the bottom of the faucet, one under each shutoff valve.
3. **Don't use the sink for 1 hour.** Then check the paper towels. Wet supply-line towels = supply leak or valve seep. Wet P-trap towel only when the sink is running = drain leak. Dry everywhere = the leak might be in the wall, or you got lucky and it's already stopped (failed gasket that resealed temporarily — still worth fixing).
4. **Now run the sink for 30 seconds with the drain plug open.** Watch every drain joint while the water flows. A drip during drainage that stops after = drain connection (slip-nut or drain tailpiece).
5. **Now fill the sink to the overflow and let it drain.** Some leaks only show under full-bowl drainage. Watch the trap again.

## Likely causes (in order of probability)

1. **P-trap slip-nut leak** (most common). The plastic or chrome nut at one of the trap connections has loosened, or its washer has compressed past sealing. Hand-tighten or replace the washer.
2. **Supply line failure**. Braided stainless supply lines age out (5–7 years typical). They develop a slow drip at the connection or burst suddenly. $5–$15 to replace.
3. **Shutoff valve seep**. The angle stop where the supply line meets the wall develops a small drip at the packing nut or compression fitting. Fixable in place ($5 packing washer) or replaceable ($15–$30 valve + 30 minutes).
4. **Faucet base leak** (less common). Water sneaks down through the faucet body and drips at the underside of the sink instead of around the faucet. Confirm by drying the underside of the faucet and watching after sink use.
5. **In-wall leak** (rare but serious). The supply pipe inside the wall has failed. Call a plumber.

## What you can check safely

- All of the above. Under-sink work is one of the lowest-risk DIY repair categories.
- **Test the shutoff valves.** Turn each off, open the faucet, confirm zero flow. If a valve doesn't fully close, replace it.
- **Inspect the supply lines** for kinks, corrosion, or visible cracks. Braided stainless lines are inexpensive — replace anytime you're in there.

## When to stop and call a pro

- The leak appears to come from the back wall (not from any specific fitting)
- A shutoff valve is seized and won't turn at all
- The cabinet bottom is soft, rotted, or has visible mold over 10 sq ft
- You've replaced the washer and the leak persists

## Likely cost range

- **DIY**: $2–$20 in parts (washer, supply line, or shutoff valve)
- **Hired**: $150–$300 for a service call to diagnose and fix under-sink connections; $300–$600+ if in-wall work or shutoff valve replacement is required
