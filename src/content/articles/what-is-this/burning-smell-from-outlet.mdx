<SafetyWarning>

**A burning, plastic, or fishy smell from an electrical outlet is a fire-risk symptom. Take action now.**

Do these things in order, immediately:

1. **Unplug whatever is in the outlet** (don't touch the outlet itself).
2. **Go to your electrical panel and shut off the breaker for that outlet.** If you don't know which breaker, kill the main breaker until you can identify the right one.
3. **Do not use the outlet until an electrician has inspected it.**
4. **If you see smoke, sparks, or any visible damage at the outlet — call 911 first, then call your power company to cut service to the home.**

</SafetyWarning>

## What it usually means

The outlet, the wires connected to it, or the wires behind it in the wall are heating up enough to break down the plastic insulation. Common causes:

- **Loose wire connection** at the back of the outlet (screw terminal or push-in/backstab). Loose connection = resistance = heat.
- **Overloaded circuit**. The outlet is on a circuit pulling more current than the wire is rated for. Common with space heaters, hair dryers, microwaves on the wrong circuit.
- **Aluminum wiring (homes built ~1965–1975)**. Aluminum expands and contracts at the screw terminals more than copper, eventually loosening. The fishy smell is a classic aluminum-wiring warning sign.
- **Damaged outlet** from prior overload, water exposure, or simply age. Outlets are rated for ~20 years; older ones develop internal cracking.
- **Arc fault** — wires shorting briefly inside the wall. Almost always requires opening the wall to find.

## Immediate safety concern

**This is the most urgent symptom on the FixItReal what-is-this list.** Electrical fires can develop from a smelly outlet within hours to weeks depending on the underlying cause. NFPA data attribute roughly 31,000 US home fires per year to electrical sources. The smell is your warning.

After you've killed power to the outlet at the breaker:

- **Smell other outlets on the same circuit.** Same circuit smelling at multiple outlets means the issue is in the wire run between them, not at one specific outlet.
- **Check for warm spots on the wall** near outlets, switches, and any visible cable runs. Warmth = current flow + resistance = problem area.
- **Check the breaker panel**. Touch each breaker (not the buses!) with the back of your hand — a warm breaker is investigating. A breaker that's warm with no load drawn is failing internally.

## What you can check safely

- **All of the above (with the breaker OFF).**
- **Visual inspection of the outlet face**: scorch marks, melting, discoloration, cracked plastic. Any visible damage means the outlet is junk regardless of any other diagnosis.
- **What was plugged in when the smell started**: space heater, microwave, hair dryer, anything with a high amp draw? That's the load that exposed the underlying problem. Don't use it on that outlet again until the outlet and wiring are inspected and (if necessary) replaced.

## What NOT to do

- ❌ Don't remove the outlet cover or stick anything into the slots
- ❌ Don't reset the breaker repeatedly to "see if it holds"
- ❌ Don't simply replace the outlet without an electrician inspecting upstream
- ❌ Don't paint over scorch marks
- ❌ Don't cover the outlet with furniture and forget about it

## When (always) to call a pro

Now. The breaker stays off until an electrician has been in. This is one of the rare home-repair issues where the right answer for nearly every homeowner is "schedule an electrician within 24–48 hours." A single service call to diagnose and replace a damaged outlet (with brief upstream inspection) typically runs **$150–$350** in most US metros.

If you have aluminum wiring (homes built ~1965–1975, check by removing one outlet cover and looking at the wire color — silver/gray = aluminum, copper = copper), this is a known issue that requires either:

- **CO/ALR-rated devices** (outlets and switches rated for aluminum) installed at every connection
- **AlumiConn or COPALUM connectors** at every wire junction
- **Full rewire** with copper (most expensive but permanent fix)

## Likely cost range

- **Single outlet replacement (no upstream issues)**: $150–$350
- **Outlet + 2–3 feet of damaged wire**: $300–$600
- **Larger damaged wire run, possibly opening drywall**: $400–$1,200
- **Aluminum wiring full home remediation with AlumiConn at every device**: $1,500–$5,000+
- **Full home rewire (copper)**: $8,000–$25,000+

Don't cheap out on this category. The cost of fixing it correctly is far less than the cost of an electrical fire.
