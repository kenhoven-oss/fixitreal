A toilet leaking water around its base is almost always a failed wax ring — the donut-shaped seal between the bottom of the toilet and the closet flange (the metal ring screwed into the bathroom floor that connects to the drain pipe).

## What it usually means

The wax ring is failing or has already failed. When you flush, water that should be going down the drain is finding its way out the side, dripping onto the floor around the toilet&rsquo;s base. The leak is usually small but consistent — a tablespoon to a quarter-cup per flush.

## Immediate safety concern

None to you directly — the water is clean (it&rsquo;s the supply water flushing through the bowl, not waste backflow) and the leak is small. But the subfloor under the toilet is at risk if this continues:

- **Under 1 week**: no real damage. Mop and plan the repair.
- **1–4 weeks**: minor staining on subfloor, no structural issue.
- **1–3 months**: subfloor begins to soften. Rot starts.
- **3+ months**: subfloor needs replacement. $300–$800 repair on top of the toilet fix.

Confirm there&rsquo;s no soft spot in the floor *before* you replace the wax ring — if the floor feels spongy when you stand near the toilet, you have a bigger job.

## Likely causes (in order of probability)

1. **Failed wax ring** (most common — 70%+ of base-leak cases). The wax dries out, the toilet shifts microscopically over time, or the flange is slightly off-level. Lifespan: 10–20 years typically.
2. **Damaged or broken closet flange** (10–15%). The flange (plastic or metal ring at the floor) cracks or rusts through. The wax ring loses its sealing surface and water escapes.
3. **Loose toilet bolts** (5–10%). The two bolts holding the toilet to the floor have backed off or weren&rsquo;t tightened correctly during the last install. The toilet rocks slightly with use, breaking the wax seal.
4. **Cracked toilet** (rare — 1–3%). A hairline crack in the porcelain at the base. Look carefully with a flashlight — if you see a crack, the toilet is done.

## What you can check safely

- **Confirm the leak source.** Dry the floor completely. Flush and watch where the water appears. Leaks at the supply-line connection (back of the toilet) are NOT wax ring issues — they&rsquo;re cheaper and easier to fix.
- **Check if the toilet rocks.** Sit on the toilet and gently shift your weight side-to-side. If you feel any movement, the bolts are loose or the floor is uneven. A rocking toilet will break a fresh wax ring within months.
- **Press on the floor around the toilet.** Spongy, soft, or yielding floor = subfloor damage from a longer-term leak. Stop the DIY assessment and call a plumber or contractor.
- **Check the bolts.** With the porcelain caps removed (they pop off), are the toilet bolts visibly corroded or stripped? If yes, they get replaced when the wax ring does.

## When to stop and call a pro

- The floor around the toilet is soft, spongy, or visibly stained
- The toilet rocks significantly when you sit on it
- The closet flange appears broken, sunken below the floor surface, or rusted through
- You see the porcelain crack at the base of the toilet
- You can&rsquo;t safely lift the toilet (~50–80 lbs)

## Likely cost range

- **DIY**: $4–$15 (just the wax ring + maybe new closet bolts)
- **Hired**: $150–$400 if the closet flange is fine; $300–$800 if the flange needs replacing; $500–$1,500+ if subfloor repair is required.
