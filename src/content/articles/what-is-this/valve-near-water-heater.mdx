The valves clustered around a water heater are the **cold-water inlet shutoff** (top), the **temperature & pressure relief valve / T&P valve** (top or side, with a discharge pipe), the **drain valve** (bottom), and sometimes a separate **gas shutoff** (gas heaters only) and an **expansion tank** mounted nearby. Each has a specific role. Most homeowners only need to know how to use the cold-water shutoff (for emergencies) and the drain valve (for annual maintenance) — the T&P valve is automatic and should never be tested casually.

## The four valves and what each does

### 1. Cold-water inlet shutoff (top)

A standard plumbing valve on the cold water line entering the top of the tank. Lever-handle (90 degrees to close) or wheel-handle (rotate clockwise). Closing it:

- Stops water from entering the tank.
- Useful for stopping a tank-failure leak, performing maintenance, or replacing the heater.
- The hot-water side doesn't need to be closed for these purposes.

**Test it once a year** by closing it briefly. Valves that haven't been operated in years seize. Lever ball-valves are more reliable than gate valves; if yours is a gate valve and feels stiff, that's worth flagging to a plumber for replacement during your next service.

### 2. Temperature & pressure relief valve (T&P)

The most safety-critical valve in the system. A spring-loaded valve that opens automatically if:

- **Tank temperature exceeds ~210°F** (water is heated dangerously high; can cause a tank explosion)
- **Tank pressure exceeds 150 psi** (system pressure is dangerous)

Either condition could rupture the tank — the T&P valve is the safety release that prevents catastrophic tank failure.

You'll see a brass valve on the top or side of the tank with a copper or plastic **discharge pipe** running down to within 6 inches of the floor or to a drain. That pipe is required code — it directs any discharge safely away from people.

**Signs of T&P trouble:**

- **Small drip occasionally during heating cycle:** sometimes normal, but should be investigated.
- **Continuous drip or steady flow:** either the valve has failed, the tank is over-pressurizing, or there's no expansion tank when one is needed. **Call a plumber.**
- **Standing water around the valve discharge:** indicates ongoing release. Bad.
- **Pipe missing or pointed up:** code violation; the pipe should point down to within 6 inches of the floor or drain.

**Never plug or cap a T&P discharge pipe.** It's a safety device; blocking it can lead to tank explosion. This is one of the most dangerous "DIY fixes" in plumbing.

### 3. Drain valve (bottom)

A hose-bib style valve at the bottom of the tank. Used to drain water for:

- **Annual sediment flush** (recommended for tank life and efficiency)
- **Tank removal during replacement**

To use: shut off the cold-water inlet, attach a garden hose, run it to a floor drain or outside, open the drain valve. Open a hot-water faucet upstairs to break the vacuum and let water flow. Allow tank to drain (~30 minutes for a 50-gallon).

**Plastic drain valves are common** and known to fail. If yours is plastic and you operate it, replace it with a brass valve while empty — the plastic ones sometimes won't close fully after use, causing slow drip indefinitely.

### 4. Gas shutoff (gas water heaters only)

A separate valve on the gas line entering the heater. Quarter-turn handle that's parallel to the pipe when open, perpendicular when closed.

Close this when:

- Tank is leaking (after closing the water shutoff)
- Performing work on the gas line
- Pilot won't stay lit and the unit needs service

**Gas smell anywhere?** Don't flip switches, don't use phones, leave the house, then call the gas company or 911 from a safe distance.

## Expansion tank (sometimes mounted nearby)

A small (typically 2-gallon) blue or white tank mounted on the cold-water inlet above or near the heater. It absorbs the thermal expansion of heated water in homes where the plumbing system is "closed" (has a pressure regulator, backflow preventer, or check valve at the meter).

Without an expansion tank, heated water expands but has nowhere to go. Pressure rises, T&P valve discharges water, you get a constantly-dripping T&P. **An expansion tank fixes this** for $150-$300 installed.

Expansion tanks themselves have a 5-10 year life. Test annually: tap the side — it should sound mostly hollow on top, solid on bottom. If it sounds solid throughout, the internal bladder has failed and the tank needs replacement.

## Safe to touch?

Yes for the cold-water shutoff and the drain valve. Safe to look at but **don't test casually** the T&P valve — older T&P valves sometimes don't reseat properly after operation and start dripping permanently. Test the T&P valve only when you're planning to replace it anyway (annual inspection is recommended every year, replacement every 5 years per manufacturer guidance).

For gas valve: safe to close in an emergency; don't attempt repairs on gas lines yourself.

## Common mistakes

- **Plugging a leaking T&P valve.** Catastrophically dangerous. Always call a plumber.
- **Skipping the annual flush.** Sediment buildup is the #1 cause of premature water-heater failure. 20 minutes once a year extends tank life.
- **Operating a plastic drain valve and then leaving it in service.** Often won't fully close after first use. Replace with brass while the tank is empty.
- **Using the cold-water valve as the primary shut-off in an emergency.** It only stops water to the heater — the rest of the house keeps running. For a full home shutdown, use the main shutoff.
