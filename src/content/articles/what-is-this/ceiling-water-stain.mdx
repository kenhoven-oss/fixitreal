    answer: "Almost never. Water travels along framing, follows pipe runs, and drips down whatever low point it reaches before saturating drywall. A stain in your dining room can be from a leak in an upstairs bathroom 20 feet away. The visual location of the stain is where the water exited the framing, not where it entered the building."
  - question: "Should I paint over it?"
    answer: "Not until you've confirmed the leak is fixed AND the area is fully dry. Painting over a wet ceiling does three things: (1) the paint won't bond properly and will bubble or peel within weeks, (2) you trap moisture against the drywall and accelerate mold growth, and (3) you lose your visual indicator that the leak is back. Use a moisture meter (pin-style works fine on drywall) to confirm the area reads below 16% moisture content before painting. For staining that bleeds through paint even after drying, use a stain-blocking primer like Kilz Original or Zinsser BIN before the topcoat."
  - question: "How long does drying take after the leak is stopped?"
    answer: "4–14 days depending on humidity, ventilation, and how saturated the area got. A small stain from a one-time event (e.g., overflowed bathtub above): 3–5 days. Chronic slow leak that finally got fixed: 10–14 days, often with help from a fan or dehumidifier in the affected room. Don't trust appearance — wet drywall can look surface-dry while the back is still saturated. Use a moisture meter."
  - question: "Is mold a serious concern?"
    answer: "Yes, if the water sat there for more than 48 hours and especially if the stain is dark (gray, black) or has a musty smell. Surface mold on the underside of the drywall is one issue; mold growing on the back side (where you can't see it) in the framing or insulation is a bigger one. A brown or yellow stain on your ceiling means water has been somewhere it shouldn&rsquo;t. The stain itself is cosmetic; the source is what matters.

## What it usually means

Water reached the back side of your ceiling drywall and saturated it, leaving a tannin-and-mineral stain as evidence. The stain&rsquo;s color and shape tell you something:

- **Yellow / light brown ring with a darker center**: classic single-event drip leak. The center is where water dripped repeatedly; the ring is the dried boundary.
- **Large diffuse brown patch**: longer-term leak; water saturated a wider area.
- **Black / gray staining**: mold has likely started. The leak has been ongoing or the area didn&rsquo;t dry quickly.
- **Multiple separate stains in a line or pattern**: water following a framing member (joist or beam) and dripping at multiple low points.

## Immediate safety concern

- **Active dripping**: catch the water (bucket, towels), turn off any plumbing above if the leak is plumbing-related (shut off the supply to the upstairs bathroom or the whole house if you can&rsquo;t isolate). A bulging, sagging ceiling means water is pooled above the drywall — puncture the lowest point with a small hole to drain the water before the ceiling falls.
- **Electrical fixture nearby**: if there&rsquo;s a light, fan, or smoke detector in the saturated area, kill power to that circuit at the breaker. Water + 120V = real risk.
- **Old roof / attic leak**: less urgent in cold dry weather; very urgent in active rain.

## Likely causes (in order of probability for stains directly below an interior space)

1. **Upstairs bathroom**: shower pan leak, tub overflow, toilet wax ring failure, supply-line leak under the sink. The stain&rsquo;s location often lines up roughly with the bathroom above.
2. **Roof leak**: if the stain is on a top-floor ceiling, suspect the roof first. Look for stains directly under roof valleys, plumbing vents, chimneys, or flashing transitions.
3. **HVAC condensate**: AC units in attics have condensate pans that overflow when the drain line clogs. Annual stain pattern (appears in summer) is the giveaway.
4. **Ice dam (cold climates)**: meltwater backs up under shingles in winter, drips through into the ceiling.
5. **Plumbing in the wall above**: less common; a supply line or drain inside a wall fails and water tracks down a stud bay to the ceiling below.
6. **Window above with failed flashing**: water enters at the window head, tracks down inside the wall, exits at the top of the ceiling below.

## What you can check safely

- **Locate what&rsquo;s directly above the stain.** Bathroom? Roof? Attic with HVAC?
- **Run the upstairs bathroom for 5 minutes** with someone watching the stain. Flow that triggers new water = bathroom plumbing.
- **Check the attic** (if accessible). Look for staining on framing, insulation that&rsquo;s wet, light coming through where it shouldn&rsquo;t.
- **Check the area during a hard rain.** New drips = roof. No new drips during rain = plumbing or condensate.
- **Photograph everything** with timestamps. If this becomes an insurance claim, you want the record.

## When to stop and call a pro

- The ceiling is actively dripping and you can&rsquo;t isolate the source
- The ceiling is bulging or sagging
- You can see mold larger than ~10 sq ft
- The stain is on a top-floor ceiling and you suspect roof damage (call a roofer)
- The stain is recurring after you thought you fixed it (call a plumber or roofer for proper diagnosis)
- Electrical fixtures in the area have water visible at them

## Likely cost range

- **Patching the ceiling after dry**: $30–$100 DIY (spackle, sand, stain-blocking primer, paint); $250–$700 hired for the drywall patch alone (see our [drywall repair cost guide](/costs/drywall-repair)).
- **Fixing the source**:
  - Bathroom wax ring or supply line: $30 DIY, $150–$400 hired
  - Roof flashing repair: $300–$1,500
  - AC condensate line snaking: $100–$300
  - In-wall plumbing repair: $400–$1,500+
- **Mold remediation if needed**: $500–$2,500+ for professional remediation in a residential space
