<SafetyWarning>

A soft spot in the floor is a structural symptom. Don&rsquo;t cover it with plywood or rugs and ignore it — diagnose the cause, fix the source, and repair the damage properly. Stop using the spot for heavy loads while you investigate.

</SafetyWarning>

## What it usually means

The structure under that part of the floor is compromised. Specifically, one or more of:

- **The subfloor** (the sheet of plywood or OSB under the finished flooring) is damaged or detached from the framing
- **A floor joist** (the framing member running across your floor span) is damaged
- **A girder, beam, or column** supporting the floor system is failing
- **The foundation itself** has shifted enough to throw the floor system out of plane

Most cases are #1 or #2. #3 and #4 are less common but more serious.

## Immediate safety concern

Moderate but not usually acute. The likely failure mode is progressive — the spot gets worse over months, not catastrophic in a day. But the underlying scenarios vary:

- **Water-damaged subfloor**: usually safe to walk on but accelerates fast. Get it inspected within a few weeks.
- **Active termite or carpenter ant infestation**: the structural members are being actively eaten. Speed matters — months, not years.
- **Joist failure or large-area subfloor rot**: stop heavy use of the area (no full bathtubs upstairs, no piano, no group seating in that part of the room). Inspect this month.
- **Foundation settlement**: usually slow, but if you see new wall cracks alongside the soft floor, that&rsquo;s active and warrants prompt professional inspection.

**Stop using the area for heavy loads** until you understand the cause.

## Likely causes (in order of probability for a residential interior floor)

1. **Subfloor delamination or loose fasteners** (~30%). Plywood subfloor that has come loose from the joists below. Cheap to fix.
2. **Water damage from above** (~30%). Long-term leak from a shower pan, toilet, supply line, or appliance. The leak source needs to be fixed before any structural repair.
3. **Water damage from below** (~10%). Bathroom on a slab with plumbing leak underneath, or basement bathroom with floor seepage.
4. **Wood-destroying insect damage** (~10%). Termites or carpenter ants in the joists. Common in older Southeast and Southwest US homes.
5. **Undersized framing** (~5%). The floor was built to code at the time but the joists are too small for the span — common in older homes converted to heavier modern uses.
6. **Foundation settlement** (~5%). The supporting structure underneath has shifted. Look for accompanying signs: cracks in walls, doors and windows out of plane.
7. **Other** (~10%): poor original construction, retrofit damage, fire damage that wasn&rsquo;t fully repaired.

## What you can check safely

- **Identify the room above** (if applicable). Is there a bathroom, kitchen, or laundry above the soft spot? Plumbing leak from above is the most common cause.
- **Identify the room below** (if accessible). Basement, crawlspace, or first-floor ceiling — can you see joists, water staining, or visible damage from below?
- **Smell the area** for the musty / earthy odor of rot. Roll up the rug, lift the flooring if removable, and smell directly.
- **Press around the soft spot.** Note the size of the affected area. A 1-foot spot is different from a 4-foot spot. Mark the boundary with painter&rsquo;s tape so you can tell if it grows.
- **Check for water history.** Has the room above this area ever had a leak? Even a long-ago leak that you "fixed" can leave behind damage that finally lets go years later.
- **From below (if accessible): inspect joists.** Look for discoloration, sagging, soft wood, frass (sawdust-like material from carpenter ants), or active mud tubes (termites).

## What you should NOT do

- ❌ Don&rsquo;t put a piece of plywood over the soft spot to "stiffen" it. This hides the symptom and lets the damage progress.
- ❌ Don&rsquo;t lay heavy furniture or appliances on the area
- ❌ Don&rsquo;t hire a flooring contractor to install new flooring over the spot until the subfloor and framing have been inspected and repaired

## When to call a pro (and which pro)

Almost always. The right pro depends on what you find:

- **Loose subfloor only, no rot, no water history**: a handyman ($200–$500 to refasten properly).
- **Water damage from a plumbing leak above**: a plumber for the source fix, then a contractor for the floor system. Two separate scopes.
- **Visible joist damage**: a contractor specializing in structural repairs. Get 2–3 quotes — pricing varies widely.
- **Termites or carpenter ants**: a pest-control company FIRST (treatment), then a contractor for the framing repair after the colony is dead. Doing them out of order wastes the framing repair.
- **Foundation settlement**: a structural engineer ($400–$800 for a written report), then a foundation contractor with the engineer&rsquo;s scope. Don&rsquo;t hire a foundation contractor without an engineer involved.

## Likely cost range

- **Loose subfloor refastening**: $200–$500
- **Subfloor replacement in a single bathroom**: $800–$2,500 (after source-fix)
- **Joist sistering (reinforcing damaged joists with new wood alongside)**: $2,000–$5,000
- **Joist replacement**: $4,000–$10,000+
- **Termite treatment + framing repair**: $5,000–$15,000+
- **Foundation work**: $5,000–$25,000+ (and up — significant settlement can run six figures)

Don&rsquo;t guess. Pay for proper inspection.
