The curved pipe under your sink is called a **P-trap** (named for its shape). It holds a small reservoir of water at all times — that water blocks sewer gas from rising up through the drain into your kitchen or bathroom. Above the trap is the **tailpiece** (from the sink), and after the trap is the **drain arm** that connects to the wall. None of these connections are glued in standard residential work — they use slip-nut compression fittings that are hand-tightened, easy to take apart for cleaning or repair.

## The parts under there

Starting from the sink and working toward the wall:

**1. Sink strainer / drain basket.**
The chrome fitting in the sink itself. The bottom side has a threaded nipple sticking down through the sink and through plumber's putty, secured by a lock nut underneath.

**2. Tailpiece.**
A short piece of pipe that hangs down from the strainer. Often plastic (white PVC or black ABS) or chrome-plated brass on older installations.

**3. P-trap.**
The U or J-shaped curved piece. Sometimes called a J-bend or J-trap. The lowest section holds water — that's the trap seal that blocks sewer gas. P-traps are almost always plastic (PVC, ABS, polypropylene) or chrome-plated brass.

**4. Trap arm.**
Horizontal piece going from the trap into the wall. May have a slight downward slope (called a "fall") of 1/4 inch per foot.

**5. Drain stub-out in wall.**
The pipe coming out of the wall, where the trap arm connects via a slip-nut.

**6. Optional: dishwasher drain hose connection.**
On kitchen sinks with dishwashers, the dishwasher drain hose connects to either the disposal (top side, removable knock-out plug must be removed first), the tailpiece (via a branch tailpiece), or an air gap on the counter.

**7. Optional: garbage disposal flange.**
For sinks with disposals, the disposal mounts to the strainer flange and has its own discharge pipe that connects into the drain plumbing above the trap.

## Why the P-trap matters

Sewer gas — hydrogen sulfide, methane, carbon monoxide, ammonia — backs up through drain lines from the building drain. The trap seal is the simple, no-moving-parts barrier that keeps it out of your house.

If you smell sewer gas at a sink: the trap may have dried out (common on rarely-used sinks). **Pour a cup of water down each drain** to restore seals. If the smell persists, the problem is elsewhere — possibly a missing trap, a venting failure (see "vent on roof" if curious), or a leaking trap.

## How it's connected

Slip-nut compression fittings everywhere, with a beveled plastic washer that compresses between the nut and the pipe to make the seal. **Hand-tight is correct.** No glue, no PVC cement, no Teflon tape.

The exception: the connection at the drain stub-out in the wall is sometimes a glued (PVC cement) joint if it's the original 1970s+ build, or a slip-nut if the home has been remodeled. Mid-2000s and newer construction often uses no-hub bands or threaded adapters.

## Safe to touch?

Yes. Drain plumbing isn't under pressure (it's gravity flow), and there's no electrical or gas involvement. The only risk: when you open the trap (for cleaning), the water inside spills. Place a bucket. Wear gloves if you're cleaning out a clog — drain contents are bacterially active.

## Common things you can DIY

**Clean out the P-trap.** Place a bucket under the trap. Unscrew the two slip-nuts (one above, one below the curve). Lift the trap free. Empty contents. Scrub with a bottle brush. Reinstall — hand-tighten only. 10 minutes; clears most kitchen-sink slow drains caused by hair, food, and grease in the trap.

**Replace the entire P-trap.** $10-$25 for a complete kit at any hardware store. 20 minutes. Recommended when the existing trap is corroded chrome (mid-century homes), cracked plastic, or has perpetually-loose slip nuts that leak.

**Replace the tailpiece.** $5-$10. 15 minutes. Recommended when the existing tailpiece is cracked, corroded, or showing signs of wear.

## When to hire a plumber

- **Slow drains that don't clear after trap cleaning.** The clog is in the wall-side line, not the trap. Plumber needs a snake or auger.
- **Recurring leaks at slip-nut connections** despite new washers. Sometimes the pipe end is bent or scored; replacement section needed.
- **Strange configurations** — S-traps (now code-illegal in most places), drum traps, missing vent.
- **Anything involving cutting into the wall.** DIY territory ends at the wall plane.

## Common mistakes

- **Pipe-wrenching the slip nuts.** Cracks the plastic; ruins the threads. Hand-tight only, or a quarter-turn with channel pliers if hand-tight doesn't seal.
- **Skipping the washer when reassembling.** The compression washer is the seal. Without it, the joint leaks immediately.
- **Putting Teflon tape on slip-nut threads.** Not needed; the seal is at the washer, not the threads. Teflon tape on these connections can actually prevent proper sealing.
- **Connecting a dishwasher drain directly to the disposal without removing the disposal's knock-out plug.** New disposals ship with a plastic plug in the dishwasher inlet that must be knocked out before connecting the hose. Skipping this step means the dishwasher won't drain.
